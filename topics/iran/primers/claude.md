# Operation Epic Fury: The US-Israeli war on Iran

On February 28, 2026, the United States and Israel launched a massive joint military assault on Iran — codenamed **Operation Epic Fury** by the Pentagon and **Operation Roaring Lion** by the IDF — that killed Supreme Leader Ayatollah Ali Khamenei, decimated Iran's senior military and political leadership, and plunged the Middle East into its most dangerous crisis since the 2003 Iraq invasion. The operation, which President Trump announced via Truth Social at 2:30 AM EST as "major combat operations," explicitly aims at regime change in Tehran, with both Trump and Netanyahu calling on Iranians to overthrow their government. Iran has retaliated with unprecedented missile and drone strikes against Israel and US military bases across six countries, killing three American soldiers, at least nine Israeli civilians, and foreign nationals in Gulf states. As of March 1, 2026 — barely 24 hours into the conflict — the situation is rapidly escalating with no ceasefire in sight, the Strait of Hormuz effectively closed, and the IRGC threatening its "most intense offensive operation in history."

The strikes came just two days after the conclusion of what Oman's foreign minister called a diplomatic "breakthrough" in US-Iran nuclear talks in Geneva, where Iran had reportedly agreed never to stockpile enriched uranium. That timing has drawn worldwide condemnation and comparisons to the worst excesses of American unilateralism. The operation represents the largest US military buildup in the Middle East since 2003, featuring two carrier strike groups, over 150 forward-deployed aircraft, and some **40,000–50,000 American service members** across the region.

---

## How the largest Middle East operation since Iraq unfolded

The first explosions rocked Tehran at approximately **9:27 AM local time** (06:27 GMT) on Saturday, February 28 — chosen deliberately as a workday morning when millions of Iranians were at work and school, and when US and Israeli intelligence had identified an extraordinary window: three simultaneous meetings of Iran's most senior political and security officials across Tehran.

The CIA had spent months tracking Khamenei's movements, security routines, and meeting schedules. When analysts detected an unusual high-level leadership gathering planned for Saturday morning, the intelligence — described as "high fidelity" — was passed to Israel. The IDF originally planned a nighttime strike but shifted to a mid-morning window to exploit this intelligence. Israeli warplanes dropped **30 bombs** on Khamenei's residential and office compound in Tehran's Pasteur district, reducing it to smoldering rubble visible in Airbus satellite imagery. Simultaneously, strikes hit two other locations hosting senior defense and security council meetings.

The IDF reported striking targets across **24 of Iran's 31 provinces** in the first 24 hours, dropping over **1,200 munitions** from more than 200 aircraft. Targets included the Iranian Parliament (destroyed), the Ministry of Intelligence, the Supreme National Security Council building, the Tehran Revolutionary Court, IRGC intelligence headquarters, the Atomic Energy Organization of Iran, and the Parchin military complex. Beyond Tehran, strikes hit Isfahan's missile production plants, Kermanshah's military facilities, missile sites in Tabriz and Bushehr, and radar installations across the country. Four B-2 Spirit stealth bombers flew nonstop from the continental United States. American forces employed Tomahawk cruise missiles from warships, HIMARS rocket artillery, carrier-based F-35C and F/A-18E/F aircraft, and — in their first-ever combat use — **LUCAS one-way attack drones**, low-cost autonomous systems designed by SpektreWorks at roughly $35,000 per unit, deployed by CENTCOM's newly formed Task Force Scorpion Strike.

The human toll is severe and still emerging. Iran's Red Crescent reported **201 civilians killed and 747 injured** by mid-afternoon on the first day, figures certain to rise. In the southern city of Minab, a strike hit an elementary girls' school, killing between 51 and 148 people — the perpetrator is bitterly disputed, with Iran blaming US-Israeli forces and some analysts suggesting a misdirected Iranian rocket. CBS News, citing intelligence and military sources, reported that **40 Iranian officials** were killed across multiple locations.

## The killing that changed everything in Tehran

Iranian state media confirmed Khamenei's death early on March 1 after hours of denial. The 86-year-old supreme leader was killed "in his office in the household of the leader" while "carrying out his duties," according to Fars News Agency. His daughter, son-in-law, daughter-in-law, and grandchild were also killed. Documentation of Khamenei's body was reportedly shown to Prime Minister Netanyahu. Trump posted on Truth Social: "Khamenei, one of the most evil people in History, is dead."

The strike decapitated Iran's leadership far beyond the supreme leader himself. Confirmed dead are **Defense Minister Aziz Nasirzadeh**, **IRGC Commander-in-Chief Mohammad Pakpour**, **Armed Forces Chief of Staff Abdolrahim Mousavi**, **SNSC Secretary Ali Shamkhani**, **Intelligence Minister Esmail Khatib**, and at least four senior IRGC intelligence commanders — including the heads of foreign intelligence, counterterrorism, and security units. This represents the most comprehensive elimination of a nation's senior security establishment through airstrikes in modern history. Notably, both Pakpour and Mousavi had been appointed just months earlier, in June 2025, after their predecessors were killed in the Twelve-Day War — meaning Iran has now lost two complete layers of military leadership in eight months.

The succession question is now Iran's most critical political challenge. The constitution mandates a **Provisional Leadership Council** comprising the president, judiciary chief, and a Guardian Council jurist to assume supreme leader duties until the 88-member Assembly of Experts selects a permanent successor. This council was formed on March 1, consisting of President Masoud Pezeshkian, hardline judiciary chief Gholamhossein Mohseni-Eje'i, and Ayatollah Alireza Arafi. But the CIA had concluded, in assessments produced in the two weeks before the strikes, that "hardline figures" of the IRGC would be most likely to dominate any post-Khamenei power structure. Leading succession candidates include Mojtaba Khamenei (the late leader's 56-year-old son, controversial for dynastic implications), Hassan Khomeini (grandson of the revolution's founder), and Arafi himself. The Kalshi prediction market gives **49% odds the supreme leader position is abolished entirely**, making structural transformation the single most likely outcome.

Civilian reaction inside Iran was strikingly divided. As news of Khamenei's death spread, celebrations erupted in Isfahan, Karaj, Kermanshah, Qazvin, Sanandaj, and Shiraz — cities that had been centers of the December 2025–January 2026 anti-government protests. Simultaneously, government supporters poured into the streets of Tehran for mourning rallies, and the state declared 40 days of mourning with seven days of national holidays.

## An unprecedented American military footprint in the Gulf

The operation was preceded by the **largest US military buildup in the Middle East since 2003**, a fact that telegraphed the coming strikes to much of the world. Two carrier strike groups anchor the deployment: the **USS Abraham Lincoln** in the Arabian Sea (redirected from the South China Sea in late January) and the **USS Gerald R. Ford** — the world's largest warship — positioned off Israel's coast after departing Souda Bay, Crete on February 26. Together, the two carriers contribute roughly 10,700 additional personnel and over 150 aircraft.

The US forward-deployed **12 F-22 Raptors to Ovda Airbase in southern Israel** on February 24 — the first-ever deployment of American offensive weaponry on Israeli soil. F-15E Strike Eagles moved from RAF Lakenheath to Muwaffaq Salti Air Base in Jordan, which became the Pentagon's most critical hub after Gulf states refused to allow their bases or airspace for offensive operations against Iran. Fourteen USAF refueling tankers positioned at Israel's Ben Gurion Airport, E-3 Sentry AWACS aircraft provided airborne command, and additional F-35s and A-10s staged through Crete. The total regional US presence stands at an estimated **40,000–50,000 service members** across permanent and forward-deployed positions in Qatar, Bahrain, Kuwait, UAE, Saudi Arabia, Jordan, Israel, and at sea.

Israel mobilized with comparable intensity. The IDF called up **70,000 reservists** as Iranian retaliation began, declared a nationwide state of emergency closing schools and restricting workplaces, and moved hospital patients to underground fortified complexes. Gas platforms in the Mediterranean were shut down as a precaution. Israel's multilayered missile defense — Iron Dome, David's Sling, and Arrow — was fully activated, though subsequent events proved it was not impenetrable.

A critical constraint shaped the operation: **Saudi Arabia, UAE, and other Gulf states refused** to allow their airspace or bases for offensive strikes on Iran, and the UK indicated its bases (including Diego Garcia) were similarly off-limits. This forced heavy reliance on Jordan, Israel, carriers at sea, and long-range assets like B-2 bombers flying from the continental US.

## Iran's "True Promise 4" and a region-wide retaliation

Iran responded with startling speed. Within approximately two hours of the first strikes, the IRGC launched **"Operation True Promise 4"** — a name continuing the sequence from previous retaliatory operations. The response came so quickly that Chatham House analysts assessed it reflected extensive pre-planning and coordination for exactly this scenario. By March 1, the IRGC had announced its seventh and eighth waves of attacks and declared that "all American and Israeli assets and interests in the Middle East have become a legitimate target."

The retaliation struck an extraordinary breadth of targets. Iranian ballistic missiles, cruise missiles, Shahed drones, and reportedly **Fattah hypersonic missiles** hit US military installations in six countries simultaneously. The US Navy 5th Fleet headquarters in Bahrain took a verified missile impact, with video showing a fire raging at the base. Al Udeid Air Base in Qatar, Ali Al-Salem Air Base in Kuwait, Al-Dhafra Air Base in the UAE, and facilities in Jordan and Saudi Arabia were all targeted. CENTCOM initially reported no casualties and only "light damage" but updated its assessment on March 1 to confirm **three American soldiers killed and five seriously wounded** — the first US combat deaths of the conflict. The IRGC claimed strikes on 27 bases and even asserted it had targeted the USS Abraham Lincoln with four ballistic missiles, a claim CENTCOM flatly denied.

Israel absorbed significant punishment. The deadliest single incident was a ballistic missile strike on **Beit Shemesh** that directly hit a public bomb shelter beneath a synagogue, killing nine people including children — made worse by an acknowledged IDF failure in which sirens never sounded before impact. In Tel Aviv, a separate strike killed two women, injured 121 people, and damaged approximately 40 buildings. Haifa reported "enormous damage" from two Iranian missiles. Israel's total death toll by March 1 reached at least **11 killed**.

Gulf states — none of which authorized offensive operations from their territory — found themselves under fire regardless. The UAE faced the most intense barrage: **165 ballistic missiles** (152 intercepted), **541 drones** (506 intercepted), and 2 cruise missiles. Three foreign nationals were killed. Debris caused fires at the Port of Jebel Ali and the facade of the Burj Al Arab hotel. Dubai International Airport, the world's busiest for international travel, was shut down indefinitely. Kuwait's international airport took a drone hit. In Bahrain, a Shahed drone struck a tower block near the 5th Fleet headquarters and another hit the Crowne Plaza Hotel. Jordan intercepted 49 drones and ballistic missiles. The IRGC effectively **closed the Strait of Hormuz** to shipping, with vessels receiving messages that "no ship is allowed to pass."

Iran's proxy network activated along a broad front. **Kataib Hezbollah** in Iraq warned it would "soon begin attacking American bases." At least four of seven militias in the Iraqi Resistance Coordination Committee had pre-committed to opening fronts if conflict erupted. In Pakistan, pro-Iran protesters attempting to storm the US Consulate in Karachi were met with live fire, killing at least nine. Crowds in Baghdad attempted to breach the Green Zone and storm the US Embassy. **Hezbollah** condemned the strikes and declared solidarity with Iran but, critically, had not conducted retaliatory attacks as of the end of February 28 — a decision being watched minute by minute given that Khamenei's killing crosses what Hezbollah officials previously called a "red line." **Houthi** leader Abdul-Malik al-Houthi declared forces in "a state of high readiness" and announced the resumption of attacks on US and Israeli-flagged shipping in the Red Sea.

## The nuclear dimension looms over every calculation

The IAEA's February 27 report — released just one day before the strikes — cast a shadow that makes this conflict potentially existential. The agency revealed for the first time that Iran had stored most of its **highly enriched uranium in an underground tunnel complex at Isfahan** that survived the June 2025 US strikes. Iran's verified stockpile stands at **440.9 kilograms of uranium enriched to 60% purity** — enough, the IAEA assessed, for potentially **up to 10 nuclear weapons** with further enrichment. The Institute for Science and International Security calculated Iran could convert this material to weapons-grade at Fordow in as little as **two to three days**.

The IAEA acknowledged it "cannot verify whether Iran has suspended all enrichment-related activities" and "cannot provide any information on the current size, composition, or whereabouts" of Iran's enriched uranium stockpile, having been denied inspector access since Iran suspended cooperation after the June 2025 war. Director General Rafael Grossi confirmed the new February 28 strikes again targeted nuclear sites at Isfahan, Fordow, and Natanz, with "localized radioactive as well as chemical release inside affected facilities" but no off-site radiation detected. Still, the fundamental calculus that motivated the strikes — preventing a nuclear-armed Iran — may paradoxically have accelerated the very outcome it sought to prevent, as a cornered regime now has powerful incentives to sprint toward a weapon.

## A world divided but leaning toward condemnation

The diplomatic response has been swift, voluminous, and largely critical of the US-Israeli action. Russia issued perhaps the strongest condemnation, calling the strikes "a pre-planned and unprovoked act of armed aggression" and President Putin labeling Khamenei's killing a "cynical murder." Moscow co-requested with China an emergency UN Security Council session, which convened on February 28. China called the attacks "unacceptable," with Foreign Minister Wang Yi warning that "the situation in the Middle East may be pushed into a dangerous abyss." Secretary-General Guterres condemned the operation as risking "igniting a chain of events that nobody can control."

The E3 powers — UK, France, and Germany — walked a careful line. Their joint statement notably **declined to either condemn or endorse** the US-Israeli strikes, instead condemning Iran's retaliatory attacks on neighboring states "in the strongest terms" while calling for "resumption of negotiations." Norway's foreign minister was more blunt, declaring the strikes "not in line with international law" because "preventive attacks require an immediately imminent threat." France's President Macron called them "an outbreak of war." Spain rejected the "unilateral military action." The Czech Republic and Albania expressed full support.

Gulf states' reactions reflected their impossible position. Saudi Arabia's condemnation focused **exclusively on Iran's retaliatory strikes**, pointedly declining to criticize the US-Israeli operation itself. The UAE, Bahrain, Kuwait, and Qatar all condemned Iran's attacks as violations of sovereignty — while Oman, the only GCC state not targeted by Iran, called the US-Israeli strikes illegal and urged Washington "not to get sucked in further." Turkey's President Erdoğan condemned both sides but warned the region risked "being dragged into a ring of fire."

The timing of the strikes relative to the nuclear talks drew particular fury. Oman's foreign minister revealed that Iran had agreed to "never stockpile enriched uranium" just two days earlier. Russia's Dmitry Medvedev accused the US of using talks as "a cover-up before military operations." A senior US administration official countered that America had offered "free nuclear fuel forever" but Iran insisted on preserving enrichment capability "for nuclear bomb" purposes.

At the Security Council, the US took over the rotating presidency on March 1 — giving Washington procedural control over agendas and the ability to block further emergency sessions. No resolution vote occurred during the February 28 emergency session, and any draft condemning the strikes faces a certain American veto.

## Experts largely skeptical, but divided on what comes next

The analytical community has produced a remarkably rapid and substantive body of assessment. The dominant expert view is captured by CFR's Ray Takeyh: **"Bombing a regime out of extinction is rarely an effective strategy."** The Stimson Center was blunter: "Air strikes alone cannot topple a government, and Iran in 2026 is likely to emerge battered but not broken — a costly example of American hubris and the limits of airpower." Chatham House's central finding — **"Iran is not Iraq in 2003"** — reflects the consensus that Iran possesses more cohesive state institutions, a deeply embedded ideological structure, and regional networks that extend beyond its borders.

Brookings' Michael O'Hanlon mapped the historical record: Kosovo 1999 took 2.5 months of bombing; Iraq 2003's "shock and awe" led to an eight-year ground presence; Libya 2011's six-month campaign produced anarchy that persists today; Afghanistan 2001 required the Northern Alliance on the ground. In every case where air campaigns toppled governments, indigenous armed forces played the decisive role. **No comparable ground force exists inside Iran.** As one 19FortyFive analyst noted, "there are more police stations in Iran than Tomahawk missiles in US stocks."

A minority of analysts are more optimistic about regime change prospects. The Hudson Institute's Michael Doran argued "there is no scenario in which the Islamic Republic survives 2026 with its power intact." The Atlantic Council's Hagar Chemali noted the regime "was already standing at the edge of a cliff" after the December–January protests killed thousands. CSIS's Clayton Swope theorized that strikes could achieve two effects: emboldening protesters and creating critical doubt among regime enablers — though he cautioned "what comes next for Iran's people is an open question."

The escalation dynamics concern nearly all observers. Hassan Ahmadian, a University of Tehran professor, told Al Jazeera that Khamenei's doctrine of "strategic patience" died with him: **"The decision has been made. If attacked, Iran will burn everything."** Multiple think tanks warn of a "scorched earth" posture where field commanders, freed from political caution, lash out with greater ferocity. CSIS mapped Iran's "use it or lose it" dilemma — that Tehran may resort to direct attacks on Arab Gulf oil infrastructure as its "last card." Foreign Affairs published "Why Iran Will Escalate," warning of quagmire. The CFR's Steven Cook warned that the most likely successors to the current regime — IRGC-dominated military rule or continuation of hardline clerical governance — "are also the least auspicious for U.S. interests."

## Congress confronts a constitutional crisis alongside a military one

The domestic political fault lines are sharp. Trump launched the operation without congressional authorization, notifying only the Gang of Eight shortly before strikes began. The White House has not presented a formal legal justification, reportedly relying on Article II commander-in-chief authority and a stretched interpretation of the 2001 AUMF. House Democratic Leader Hakeem Jeffries insisted the administration "must seek authorization for the preemptive use of military force that constitutes an act of war." Senator Tim Kaine called the strikes "a colossal mistake." Representative Ro Khanna declared: "The president has launched an illegal war when there is no imminent threat. He did not consult with Congress or allow for a debate in Congress, which even George W. Bush did."

Notably, several Republicans broke ranks. Representative Thomas Massie declared "I am opposed to this War. This is not 'America First.'" Senator Rand Paul invoked his constitutional oath to oppose "another Presidential war." Former Representative Marjorie Taylor Greene protested: "We said 'No More Foreign Wars, No More Regime Change!'" A bipartisan **Khanna-Massie War Powers Resolution** was already drafted, with Democrats announcing a forced vote for the following week. While passage is possible given Republican defections, a presidential veto is certain and a two-thirds override virtually impossible.

Vice President JD Vance's position epitomizes the administration's internal tensions. Vance, who built his political brand partly on opposing the Iraq War and Middle East interventionism, told the Washington Post before the strikes that "there is no chance" of prolonged war. But reporting from The Atlantic revealed he **expressed private reservations** about Trump's war plan and was "frozen out" of core planning, monitoring from the Situation Room while Trump directed operations from Mar-a-Lago with Secretary of State Rubio and Defense Secretary Hegseth. Vance went silent on his personal social media accounts after the bombing began. A January 2026 Quinnipiac poll found **70% of Americans — and a majority of Republicans — opposed military intervention in Iran**.

## Conclusion

The February 28, 2026 strikes represent a qualitative break from every previous US and Israeli military action against Iran. Unlike the contained June 2025 Twelve-Day War or the limited tit-for-tat exchanges of 2024, Operation Epic Fury is open-ended, explicitly targets the regime itself, and has already drawn six additional countries into active combat. The killing of Khamenei eliminates the figure who, for better or worse, calibrated Iran's escalatory responses for 36 years. What replaces his calculated restraint is the central unknown of this crisis.

Three dynamics will determine whether this conflict expands or contracts. First, whether the IRGC — now Iran's most powerful surviving institution — escalates toward "scorched earth" or calculates that preservation requires measured response. Second, whether Hezbollah activates its arsenal against Israel, which would open a devastating second front. Third, whether the Strait of Hormuz closure triggers an energy crisis severe enough to shift international pressure toward a ceasefire. The nuclear dimension adds existential stakes: Iran retains enough enriched uranium for multiple weapons and faces an unprecedented incentive to sprint toward a bomb.

The historical record offers cold comfort to proponents of airpower-driven regime change. But Iran in 2026 is genuinely different from past cases — its regime is weakened by years of protest, economic collapse, and military degradation in ways that Iraq in 2003 or Libya in 2011 were not. The question is not whether the Islamic Republic will survive intact — most analysts believe it cannot. The question is what fills the vacuum, and whether the United States has any plan for the answer.
