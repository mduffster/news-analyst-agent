## What Changed Since Yesterday (March 25)

### Israel Kills IRGC Navy Commander Tangsiri — Hormuz Architect Eliminated

**[VERIFIED - US/Israel; Iran unconfirmed]** Israel said on Thursday that it had killed Commodore Alireza Tangsiri, the head of Iran's Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps Navy (IRGCN). Israel's Defence Minister Israel Katz said Tangsiri had been killed in a "precise and lethal operation."

"The man who was directly responsible for the terrorist operation of mining and blocking the Strait of Hormuz to shipping was blown up and eliminated," Katz claimed.

Alongside Tangsiri, the IDF said it had killed the head of the Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps's (IRGC) Navy Intelligence Directorate, Behnam Rezaei.

An official in Islamabad following the negotiations confirmed that Tangsiri was killed. The official spoke on condition of anonymity because they were not authorized to speak publicly about it.

Iran has not yet commented on the report.

**Assessment:** This is the most significant tactical killing since the war began specifically targeting Iran's Hormuz blockade capability. If confirmed, Tangsiri's death could disrupt Iran's naval command structure, at least in the short term. However, Iran's military system is designed to function through layered leadership.

### Israel Intensifies Strikes Ahead of Potential Ceasefire

**[VERIFIED]** The Israeli military said on social media it had completed a "wave of extensive strikes in Isfahan … targeting infrastructure."

Local media reported attacks in the central cities of Isfahan and Shiraz, as well as Bandar Abbas in the south, Tabriz in the northwest and Mashhad in the northeast — an area largely spared until now.

Israel says its airstrikes continue in southern Lebanon, in advance of what Israeli officials say will be a "prolonged" ground invasion targeting Iran-backed Hezbollah militants. Israeli officials say they plan to take Lebanese territory up to the Litani River, which runs 10 to 20 miles north of the border with Israel.

**Assessment:** Israel is maximizing military gains in anticipation that diplomatic efforts might produce a ceasefire. The expansion of strikes to previously less-targeted areas like Mashhad demonstrates sustained offensive capability.

### Iran Formalizing Hormuz Tolls Through Legislation — "Tehran Tollbooth"

**[REPORTED]** The Iranian parliament is working on a draft bill that would impose a fee on vessels seeking safe passage through the Strait of Hormuz, according to the semi-official Fars news agency. Fars, citing an unnamed lawmaker, said the plan would be finalized next week, and would legally recognize Iran's oversight of Hormuz.

Iranian media reported lawmaker Mohammadreza Rezaei Kouchi as saying that "parliament is pursuing a plan to formally codify Iran's sovereignty, control and oversight over the Strait of Hormuz, while also creating a source of revenue through the collection of fees."

A draft bill could formalise fees reportedly reaching $2 million, dubbed the "Tehran Tollbooth", for vessels seeking safe passage. Dimitris Ampatzidis, a risk and shipping behaviour expert, says operators will avoid frameworks seen as compliance with unilateral Iranian demands due to sanctions and reputational risks.

Jasem Mohamed al-Budaiwi, the secretary-general of the Gulf Cooperation Council, a bloc of Arab nations, said in a briefing that Iran is already charging fees for safe passage — in violation of international law.

**Assessment:** Iran is attempting to convert a wartime blockade into a permanent revenue-generating sovereignty claim. This directly contradicts international maritime law but shows Iran is playing for long-term strategic gains regardless of ceasefire outcome. Charging a fee would transform Hormuz from an international waterway (where passage is a right) to a domestic waterway (where passage is a privilege Iran can grant or withhold). This is precisely the transformation Iran seeks — and precisely what UNCLOS prohibits.

### Iran's Five Conditions — Maximalist Demands Unchanged

**[VERIFIED]** Iran rejected the proposal, and Foreign Minister Abbas Araghchi said the country wants to end the war only on "our own terms." Iran has given five conditions: "end to aggression by the enemy, concrete guarantees preventing the recurrence of war, clear determination, guaranteed payment of war damages and compensation, comprehensive end to the war across all fronts, incl. against all resistance groups, recognition of Iran's sovereignty over Strait of Hormuz."

Iranian officials have insisted they are not negotiating with the U.S., saying the countries have only exchanged messages via regional intermediaries.

### Israel Reportedly Removed Iranian Diplomats from Hit List

**[REPORTED]** As Pakistan is attempting to broker peace talks between the United States and Iran, an unnamed Pakistani official told Reuters news that the Israeli military had the coordinates to target Iranian Foreign Minister Abbas Araghchi and parliamentary speaker Mohammad Baqer Qalibaf. But Islamabad warned the US administration that there would be "no one else to talk to" if they were killed, prompting Israel to also back off.

CNN cannot independently verify the Reuters report. Asked about the reports on Thursday, the Israel Defense Forces international spokesperson Nadav Shoshani responded: "We have a rigorous process before every operation we carry out, every strike we carry out — legal experts, intelligence experts, aerial experts, senior commanders."

**Assessment:** If accurate, this strongly suggests back-channel negotiations are real. You don't remove negotiating counterparts from a target list unless those counterparts have a role to play in talks.

### G7 Meeting Opens in France — Allies Seeking Clarity

**[VERIFIED]** France hosts G7 foreign ministers near Paris, with the Iran war and Ukraine set to dominate the agenda. France widens participation by inviting Saudi Arabia, South Korea, Brazil and India, alongside the EU's foreign affairs chief Kaja Kallas.

## Critical Assessments

### The Tangsiri Killing: Tactical Gain, Strategic Risk

The killing of Tangsiri represents the most direct attack on Iran's Hormuz blockade capability. He personally oversaw the closure and was posting on social media about which ships were permitted passage. However:

- His death may temporarily disrupt IRGC naval command but is unlikely to immediately reopen the strait
- Iran's move to formalize tolls through legislation suggests the blockade strategy will continue under new leadership
- The killing may harden Iranian positions at exactly the moment diplomacy needs flexibility

### Israel's 48-Hour Acceleration: Hedging Against Ceasefire

Israel's decision to speed up strikes ahead of a potential ceasefire reveals fundamental mistrust of diplomatic outcomes. If Israel believes a ceasefire is possible within days, it's maximizing military gains now — suggesting Israeli leadership views any diplomatic pause as temporary rather than final.

### Iran's Hormuz Strategy: From Blockade to Tollbooth

Iran's parliamentary push to codify Hormuz transit fees represents a strategic shift:
- Converting wartime leverage into permanent peacetime control
- Creating a revenue stream independent of sanctions relief
- Establishing legal precedent (however contested) for sovereignty claims
- Signaling that even ceasefire won't restore pre-war status quo

This creates a fundamental problem for any peace deal: Iran is demanding recognition of Hormuz sovereignty, while the US and international community cannot accept permanent toll arrangements.

### The G7's Impotence

The G7 meeting reveals allied frustration and powerlessness:
- No joint communique planned to avoid highlighting divisions
- Allies seeking "clarity" from Rubio — meaning they don't know US strategy
- Germany calling the war a "catastrophe" while offering only post-ceasefire help
- None willing to participate militarily despite economic damage
