## What Changed Since Yesterday

### Trump Escalates Mine Warfare Posture

**[OFFICIAL]** President Trump issued an explicit shoot-to-kill order for boats engaged in mining operations:

"I have ordered the United States Navy to shoot and kill any boat, small boats though they may be (Their naval ships are ALL, 159 of them, at the bottom of the sea!), that is putting mines in the waters of the Strait of Hormuz. There is to be no hesitation," Trump wrote in a post on Truth Social.

Trump also said he is ordering U.S. minesweepers to continue clearing the strait "at a tripled up level!"

"We have total control over the Strait of Hormuz," he wrote in another Truth Social post later Thursday morning. "No ship can enter or leave without the approval of the United States Navy. It is 'Sealed up Tight,' until such time as Iran is able to make a DEAL!!!"

Earlier this month, the New York Times, citing US officials, reported that Iran is finding it increasingly difficult to reopen the Strait of Hormuz due to its inability to locate all the naval mines it planted during the war.

**Assessment:** Trump's explicit authorization of lethal force against minelaying boats represents the most significant escalation in rules of engagement since the ceasefire began. While previous orders allowed for mine destruction, this is the clearest authorization to date for direct attacks on IRGC personnel engaged in mining operations. Combined with the six-month mine-clearing timeline, this suggests the Hormuz situation will not normalize quickly even if peace is achieved.

### US Expands Maritime Interdiction to Global Waters

**[OFFICIAL]** U.S. forces have stopped and boarded another sanctioned, stateless oil tanker in the Indian Ocean, the Pentagon announced Thursday, making it the third vessel the military has halted as part of its blockade on Iranian ports.

The development marked the second tanker boarding outside the Middle East in recent days as the U.S. blockade on Iran expands into global waters.

"We will continue global maritime enforcement to disrupt illicit networks and interdict vessels providing material support to Iran, wherever they operate," a Pentagon statement said. Ship-tracking data showed the Majestic X in the Indian Ocean between Sri Lanka and Indonesia, roughly the same location as the oil tanker Tifani, earlier seized by American forces.

The boarding of Majestic X comes as maritime pressure appears increasingly two-sided. Iran this week seized two MSC containerships attempting to exit the Persian Gulf. Together, the moves suggest maritime enforcement is evolving from localized interdictions into a distributed campaign stretching from Hormuz into the wider Indian Ocean.

U.S. Central Command reported it has directed 31 vessels to turn around or return to port since the start of the blockade on April 13.

**Assessment:** The seizure of tankers in the Indian Ocean — between Sri Lanka and Indonesia — demonstrates a significant expansion of US operations well beyond the Middle East. This global interdiction campaign suggests the US is attempting to strangle Iranian oil revenue regardless of geography, while Iran responds with its own seizures closer to home.

### Strait Traffic at Near-Total Standstill

**[VERIFIED]** In the wake of the Iranian attack on three ships in the Strait of Hormuz yesterday, MarineTraffic tracking data shows an almost total standstill of vessels transiting through the waterway. Just one ship, the Greek-owned bulk carrier LB Energy, has made the journey in the last 12 hours.

Tanker traffic remains far below pre-war levels in the strait, which normally serves as the pathway for 20% of the world's oil. More than 100 ships, including dozens of tankers, would transit the strait each day in peace times. But that number has fallen to single digits on most days after Iran imposed its de facto closure. At least eight ships transited the strait on Wednesday, including three oil tankers, according to tracking data from LSEG.

Since the Feb. 28 start of the war between Iran, Israel and the United States, over 30 ships have come under attack in the waters of the Persian Gulf, the Strait of Hormuz and the Gulf of Oman. The threat of attack, rising insurance premiums and other fears have stopped traffic from moving through the strait. Iran's ability to restrict traffic through the strait, which leads from the Persian Gulf to the open ocean, has proved a major strategic advantage.

**Assessment:** Yesterday's IRGC seizure of two ships attempting to run the strait has effectively shut down any remaining commercial traffic. The risk-reward calculus for shipping companies has become untenable — even vessels that managed to enter the Gulf are now trapped.

### Iran Confirms Toll Revenue Collection

**[REPORTED]** Iran confirmed it is actively collecting fees for Hormuz transit:

Iran has begun collecting fees from commercial ships in the Strait of Hormuz. Lawmakers confirmed tolls depend on vessel size, cargo and volume. Commercial traffic in the strait has dramatically decreased due to security concerns. Funds from the tolls are deposited into Iran's central bank account.

Some ships have paid Iran as much as $2 million for safe passage through the strait, but the mechanism by which the ships paid the fee and the currency used is not clear. Some reports found that the Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps, which has established effective control over the strait, has accepted payments in cryptocurrency and Chinese yuan.

The international community has mostly rejected a permanent fee structure in the Strait of Hormuz. Secretary of State Marco Rubio said last month during a Group of Seven meeting that the U.S. would not accept a post-war scenario in which Iran can make money off the strait.

**Assessment:** Iran's confirmation that it is receiving toll revenue represents a significant development — Tehran is monetizing its control of the waterway and potentially establishing a de facto regime that could outlast the war. This directly contradicts US policy and creates a new negotiating obstacle.

### Oil Prices Surge Past $103

**[VERIFIED]** Global crude oil markets have entered a volatile phase, with benchmark prices climbing above the psychologically significant $100-per-barrel mark amid tightening supply signals and escalating geopolitical tensions. The rally has been led by Brent Crude, which rose to $103.38 per barrel, gaining 1.44% in the latest session. Its US counterpart, WTI Crude, also advanced to $94.46, up 1.67%. This marks the fourth consecutive session of gains.

Over the past month, Brent's price has risen 1.79%, and is up 53.86% compared to the same time last year.

The ongoing disruption is contributing to severe supply concerns, with estimates of demand destruction nearing 4 to 5 million barrels per day, or about 5 percent of global supply, with Asia most affected.

**Assessment:** Oil has risen ~$7 from yesterday's $96 levels to above $103. This is the fourth consecutive day of gains since the ceasefire extension failed to improve the Hormuz situation. Sustained prices above $100 will create significant global economic and political pressure.

### Navy Secretary Fired During Active Blockade

**[VERIFIED]** Hegseth had a conversation with Trump and informed Phelan he needed to resign or be fired. "President Trump and Secretary Hegseth agreed new leadership at the Navy is needed," one of the sources, a senior administration official, told CNN.

Multiple sources told CNN there was tension for months between Phelan and Hegseth, who believed Phelan was moving too slowly on implementing shipbuilding reforms and was also irked by Phelan's direct communication with Trump, which Hegseth viewed as an attempt to bypass him. Deputy Defense Secretary Steve Feinberg also wanted to take control of major responsibilities for shipbuilding and navy acquisitions. It was during a meeting between Trump and Hegseth on shipbuilding at the White House Wednesday that the issues came to a head. Trump, frustrated by slow shipbuilding progress himself, became convinced during the meeting that Phelan needed to be replaced.

Firing the head of the Navy when the U.S. is enforcing a naval blockade caught members of Congress and Pentagon officials by surprise.

Phelan's removal is the latest in a series of senior military leaders being fired or are leaving during the US-Israeli war on Iran. Among the most notable dismissals was Army Chief of Staff General Randy A. George, in the first week of April.

**Assessment:** Firing the Navy Secretary during an active naval blockade operation is extraordinary and creates significant leadership instability during wartime operations. This continues a pattern of Pentagon purges that has now removed multiple senior military leaders during active combat operations.

## Analysis: The Shoot-to-Kill Paradox

**What's happening:**

Trump's explicit authorization of lethal force against minelaying boats represents a critical escalation in what is officially a ceasefire. The administration is simultaneously:
- Extending the ceasefire indefinitely
- Authorizing shoot-to-kill against IRGC personnel
- Expanding global interdiction operations
- Tripling mine-clearing efforts

This creates a paradox: the ceasefire preserves the pause in airstrikes while maritime conflict intensifies.

**The ROE creates binary risk:**

The shoot-to-kill order for minelayers will either:
1. **Deter mining operations** — If Iran believes the threat is credible, it may pause mining, reducing escalation risk
2. **Trigger direct engagement** — If IRGC continues operations and US forces engage, any IRGC casualties could trigger rapid escalation

The IRGC's track record suggests it will continue operations rather than be deterred. This makes direct engagement within days more likely than not.

**Iran's toll regime complicates peace:**

Iran's confirmation of toll revenue collection and planned legislation to formalize it creates a new obstacle to peace. The US has explicitly rejected any post-war arrangement where Iran profits from Hormuz control. Iran is now establishing facts on the ground that entrench IRGC economic control.

**Six-month mine problem:**

Even if peace is achieved tomorrow, the strait won't fully reopen for up to six months due to mines Iran planted but can't fully locate. This means:
- Oil prices will remain elevated for the foreseeable future
- Shipping companies face prolonged uncertainty
- Any peace deal will have limited immediate economic impact

**Pentagon instability during war:**

Firing the Navy Secretary during an active naval blockade is unprecedented and concerning. Combined with the earlier firing of the Army Chief of Staff and other senior leaders, this creates command uncertainty at a moment when precise coordination is essential.
