## Day 24 — Monday, March 23, 2026

## Executive Summary

Day 24 marks a potential turning point as President Trump announced a five-day pause on strikes against Iranian power plants, citing "productive conversations" with Tehran toward a "complete and total resolution" of hostilities. The president's announcement sent markets soaring and energy prices diving, as investors bet Iran's blockade of a key shipping chokepoint could soon end. However, "There is been no negotiation and there is no negotiation, and with this kind of psychological warfare, neither the Strait of Hormuz will return to its pre-war conditions nor will there be peace in the energy markets," state media reported.

The conflicting narratives create profound uncertainty. Sources tell Axios that intermediaries have been passing messages between the U.S. and Iran, but Iran unequivocally denied that there had been any direct communication with Washington. A U.S. source said Turkey, Egypt, and Pakistan have been passing messages between the U.S. and Iran over the past two days. The foreign ministers of the three countries held separate talks with White House envoy Steve Witkoff and Iranian Foreign Minister Abbas Araghchi.

The IEA issued its starkest warning yet: The world is facing a worse energy crisis than the twin oil shocks of the 1970s and the fallout of the Ukraine war combined. IEA Executive Director Fatih Birol said the energy crunch prompted by the US-Israel war on Iran exceeded the 1973 and 1979 oil shocks and gas shortages stemming from Russia's 2022 invasion of Ukraine put together.

Oil prices swung wildly—Brent crude fell more than 7% to around $104 a barrel, having plunged by more than 13% at one point. Earlier in the day, Brent had climbed above $114 a barrel.

## What Changed Since Friday (March 20)

### Trump's Five-Day Pause on Power Plant Strikes

**[OFFICIAL — US]** In an all caps post on Truth Social on Monday morning Trump wrote that the U.S. and Iran have had "very good and productive conversation" over the last two days that focused on ending hostilities in the region. "BASED ON THE TENOR AND TONE OF THESE IN DEPTH, DETAILED, AND CONSTRUCTIVE CONVERSATIONS, WITCH WILL CONTINUE THROUGHOUT THE WEEK, I HAVE INSTRUCTED THE DEPARTMENT OF WAR TO POSTPONE ANY AND ALL MILITARY STRIKES AGAINST IRANIAN POWER PLANTS AND ENERGY INFRASTRUCTURE FOR A FIVE DAY PERIOD."

Trump asserts there are 15 points of agreement, including Iran accepting no nuclear weapons. Trump told CNN the Strait of Hormuz will be "open very soon," adding he'd like to see the vital shipping route under joint US and Iranian control. "It'll be jointly controlled," he added.

Trump said Iran and the U.S. are going to speak "today," and will probably meet soon in person. If talks don't go well, he said, "we'll just keep bombing our little hearts out." "We're going to get together today, by probably phone because it's very hard to find a country, it's very hard for them to get out, I guess," Mr. Trump told reporters. "But we'll at some point very, very soon meet. We're doing a five-day period. We'll see how that goes. And if it goes well, we're going to end up with settling this. Otherwise, we'll just keep bombing our little hearts out."

### Iran Categorically Denies Negotiations

**[OFFICIAL — Iran]** There are no talks between Tehran and Washington, Iran's foreign ministry said, rejecting remarks by US President Donald Trump. The ministry said Trump's comments were part of an effort to lower energy prices and buy time for his military plans.

IRGC-affiliated Fars News said on Monday there had been "no direct or indirect contact" with US President Donald Trump. "There has been no direct or indirect contact with Trump," Fars reported, citing an Iranian source. The source added that Trump "backed down" after being warned Iran would target "all power plants in West Asia."

Iranian state media responded to Trump's post by saying the U.S. president has "backed down" after Iran's firm response. Tehran has not confirmed any talks with the U.S. and previously vowed not to negotiate. "Trump, fearing Iran's response, backed down from his 48-hour ultimatum," read a graphic on IRIB state television.

**Assessment:** The most likely explanation is that intermediary-mediated communications (Turkey, Egypt, Pakistan, Oman) occurred, which Trump characterizes as "talks" while Iran characterizes as "regional de-escalation efforts" not involving Washington directly. Both statements may be technically accurate from each side's perspective.

### Trump's 48-Hour Ultimatum and Iran's Counter-Threats

Prior to the pause, Trump issued a dramatic ultimatum. "If Iran doesn't FULLY OPEN, WITHOUT THREAT, the Strait of Hormuz, within 48 HOURS from this exact point in time, the United States of America will hit and obliterate their various POWER PLANTS, STARTING WITH THE BIGGEST ONE FIRST!" Trump warned.

Iranian officials warned Monday that if the U.S. attacks Iran's power plants, Iran would retaliate against energy and water infrastructure across the Gulf region – including in countries that host U.S. military bases. In a separate statement, Iran's Defense Council said "non-belligerent" countries could only transit through the Strait of Hormuz through coordination with Iran, and warned that any attack on Iran's coasts or islands would trigger mine-laying across Gulf sea lanes.

### IEA: "Worse Than Two Oil Shocks Combined"

**[VERIFIED]** IEA Director Birol said the effective closure of the Strait of Hormuz and attacks on energy facilities had reduced global oil supplies by about 11 million barrels per day (bpd), more than double the combined shortfalls of the 1970s' crises.

The oil crises of 1973 and 1979, he said, lost together 10 million barrels per day, causing "major economic problems around the world, the recessions. And today, only as of today, we lost 11 million barrels per day — so more than two major oil shocks put together." After Russia's invasion of Ukraine, he said, the gas markets, especially in Europe, "lost about 75 billion cubic meters, 75BCM. And as of now, as a result of this crisis, we lost about 140BCM, almost twice (as much)."

The head of the International Energy Agency said on Monday that at least 40 energy assets across nine countries in the Middle East have been "severely or very severely" damaged since the Iran war began. Speaking at the National Press Club in Australia's capital, IEA Executive Director Fatih Birol said damage to oil and gas fields, refineries and pipelines across the Middle East would take some time to repair.

Even in the scenario where the conflict concludes and the Strait reopens, the physical infrastructure of Gulf energy production has been so severely damaged that recovery will be measured in months and years, not weeks. "It will take a long time," he said. "It will be six months for some sites to be operational, others much longer."

### Israel Escalates in Lebanon — Litani Bridges Destroyed

**[VERIFIED]** Israeli forces have attacked the Qasmiyeh Bridge, a key crossing linking Lebanon's south to the rest of the country, in an escalation that President Joseph Aoun called a "prelude to ground invasion". Sunday's strike on the vital artery and other civilian infrastructure came after Israeli Defence Minister Israel Katz ordered the military to destroy all crossings over the Litani River.

Defence Minister Israel Katz ordered all Litani River bridges destroyed and vowed to accelerate demolitions near the border, citing models used in Gaza. Strikes and infrastructure damage have killed more than 1,000 people, displaced over a million, and deepened isolation of southern communities amid ongoing clashes.

The Israeli army chief said the operation against Hezbollah "has only begun" and would be "prolonged".

**Assessment:** Israel is preparing for expanded ground operations in Lebanon regardless of any US-Iran diplomatic developments. The destruction of Litani bridges isolates southern Lebanon and signals imminent territorial operations.

### Iran Death Toll Surpasses 1,500

**[VERIFIED]** Preliminary figures are 1,500 dead in Iran, at least 18 in Israel, 13 US soldiers and 21 killed in Gulf states.

The NGO HRANA documented 3,114 deaths in Iran due to airstrikes by 17 March, including 1,354 civilians, 1,138 military personnel, and 622 unclassified. HRANA states that "military casualties are significantly higher than the figures reported in these reports."

Note: Government figures and independent monitoring groups vary significantly. Al Jazeera's tracker shows 1,500+ (Iranian Health Ministry), while HRANA reported 3,114 through March 17.

### Congressional Resistance to $200 Billion Persists

**[VERIFIED]** In the coming weeks, President Donald Trump could ask Congress to spend as much as $200 billion to fund the ongoing war. But it will be enormously difficult to pass. GOP leaders do not believe they have the votes to fund the war even in their own party without far more detailed plans from the White House.

Rep. Lauren Boebert robustly confirmed that she would not vote in favor of any war supplementals. "I've already told leadership, I am a 'no' on any war supplementals. I am so tired of spending money elsewhere."

Even if the war with Iran ends, Trump indicated that he'd still seek $200 billion in funding for the Pentagon, saying that "it's always nice to have."

### Strait of Hormuz Remains Effectively Closed

**[VERIFIED]** Maritime traffic through the Strait of Hormuz — a vital route for exports of oil, natural gas and other commodities from the Persian Gulf — remains at a near-standstill weeks after the US and Israel launched strikes against Iran.

Over the past week, Iran has toned down the rhetoric to say the strait is only closed to Tehran's enemies. Japan may soon join the small cohort of countries – mainly China, India, and Pakistan – whose vessels have been allowed to transit the waterway. Lloyd's List reported that 10 ships have transited the strait by sailing close to Iran's coastline – a route that is emerging as a "safe corridor" for shipping.

US officials are furiously trying to avert a potential monthslong closure of the Strait of Hormuz, privately acknowledging that reopening the key waterway is a problem without a clear solution. "One of the core conundrums of this conflict is the Iranians have real leverage with this, and there's not an obvious fix for it," an intelligence official said.

### Diplomatic Reactions

Omani Foreign Minister Badr Albusaidi said Oman is "working intensively" toward the safe passage of vessels through the Strait of Hormuz. "Whatever your view of Iran, this war is not of their making. This is already causing widespread economic problems and I fear they promise to get much worse if the war continues."

China's envoy to the Middle East, Zhai Jun, said nonmilitary targets, including those related to energy, "must not be attacked." "This is a war that should never have happened," the envoy told reporters in Beijing today. "While negotiations were still underway, the United States and Israel suddenly provoked conflict, causing diplomatic efforts to collapse."

## Critical Assessments

### Is This a Real Diplomatic Opening?

The five-day pause creates what all three reports identify as the most significant de-escalation signal since the war began. However, the narrative conflict between Trump's claims and Iran's denials complicates assessment:

**What we know:**
- Turkey, Egypt, and Pakistan have been passing messages between the U.S. and Iran over the past two days.
- "The mediation is ongoing and making progress. The discussion is about ending the war and resolving all outstanding issues. We hope to have answers soon."
- US has operationally paused power plant strikes for five days
- Oil markets responded as if the pause is meaningful (7%+ drop)

**What remains unclear:**
- Whether Iran's categorical denials are domestic face-saving or substantively accurate
- Who has authority to negotiate on Iran's behalf (leadership vacuum persists)
- Whether intermediary message-passing constitutes "talks" in any meaningful sense

**Assessment:** [REPORTED] Most likely scenario: Indirect channel communications via intermediaries are occurring, which Trump is characterizing as "talks" while Iran maintains plausible deniability. The pause is real; the question is whether it leads anywhere.

### Iran's Escalation Threats Are Now Explicit

Iran's Defense Council warned that any attack on Iran's coasts or islands would trigger mine-laying across Gulf sea lanes that could effectively block maritime traffic beyond the narrow strait.

This directly addresses the Kharg Island scenario identified in the March 20 synthesis. Any US movement toward coastal/island operations now has an explicit Iranian counter: widened mining of the entire Persian Gulf, potentially affecting not just the Strait but Gulf ports themselves.

### Energy Infrastructure Damage Is Structural

Even in the scenario where the conflict concludes and the Strait reopens, the physical infrastructure of Gulf energy production has been so severely damaged that recovery will be measured in months and years, not weeks. "It will take a long time."

This is critical for understanding that even successful diplomacy does not immediately resolve the energy crisis. Markets have not fully priced in this structural damage.
