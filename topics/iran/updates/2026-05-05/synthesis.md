## Critical Developments Since May 4

### 1. Pentagon: "Ceasefire Is Not Over" Despite Combat Exchanges

**[OFFICIAL]** US Defence Secretary Pete Hegseth says the ceasefire with Iran is "not over" despite escalating tensions in the Strait of Hormuz, adding US President Donald Trump will determine whether any escalation constitutes a "violation of ceasefire".

**[OFFICIAL]** "Right now the ceasefire certainly holds but we're going to be watching very, very closely," Hegseth said.

**[OFFICIAL]** Gen. Dan Caine, chairman of the Joint Chiefs of Staff, told reporters Tuesday that Iran's attacks fall "below the threshold of restarting major combat operations at this point." Hegseth said the "ceasefire is not over."

**[OFFICIAL]** Secretary of Defense Pete Hegseth said the U.S. is "locked and loaded" in the Strait of Hormuz as it moves to open up the waterway. "We prefer this to be a peaceful operation, but are locked and loaded to defend our people, our ships, our aircraft, and this mission without hesitation," Hegseth said during a press conference at the Pentagon Tuesday.

**Assessment:** The US is drawing a legal/semantic distinction between "Project Freedom" operations and the broader ceasefire framework. This creates space for continued military action without declaring the ceasefire broken, but it's an inherently unstable posture.

### 2. US Sank 6-7 Iranian Small Boats — Iran Denies

**[VERIFIED]** The U.S. military said it fired on Iranian forces and sank six small boats targeting civilian ships as it moved to reopen the Strait of Hormuz on Monday.

**[OFFICIAL]** U.S. Central Command announced the engagement in a press call with reporters, saying Iran's military capability has been "dramatically degraded." CENTCOM Commander Adm. Brad Cooper said on the call that Iran historically has deployed "between 20 and 40 small boats" when harassing vessels in the strait. "Today, we saw just six, and eliminated them quickly," Cooper said.

**[OFFICIAL]** He said Iran launched multiple cruise missiles, drones and small boats at civilian ships under the U.S. military's protection. U.S. military helicopters sank six of the small boats, Cooper said, adding that "each and every" threat had been defeated.

**[DISPUTED]** Iran's state media denied Cooper's claim that any of their boats were destroyed, characterizing Iranian actions as "warning shots."

**Assessment:** This is the first confirmed direct US-Iran kinetic exchange since the ceasefire began April 7. The administration's insistence that this is "below the threshold" of breaking the ceasefire establishes a troubling new normal where active combat can occur within a nominal truce.

### 3. Iran Attacks UAE for Second Consecutive Day

**[VERIFIED]** For a second day straight, the United Arab Emirates says its air defence systems are currently engaged in shooting down missiles and drones launched from Iran.

**[VERIFIED]** The UAE Defense Ministry said its air defenses had engaged 15 missiles and four drones fired by Iran. Authorities in the eastern emirate of Fujairah said one drone sparked a fire at a key oil facility, wounding three Indian nationals.

**[VERIFIED]** The UAE said it reserved the "full and legitimate right" to respond to the latest Iranian attacks.

**[VERIFIED]** Schools in the United Arab Emirates are shifting to remote learning this week after renewed Iranian strikes targeted the Persian Gulf country. The Emirati Ministry of Education said in a statement that schools and nurseries would operate remotely from Tuesday through Friday "out of concern for the safety of students and all those working in the education sector."

**[VERIFIED]** The United Arab Emirates has accused Iran of attacking the country with a barrage of missiles and drones, setting an oil refinery ablaze in the eastern emirate of Fujairah and wounding three Indian nationals. The attacks on Monday mark the first on the UAE since Iran and the United States agreed to a ceasefire on April 8.

**Assessment:** Iran is now conducting sustained daily attacks against UAE during what is nominally a ceasefire. The pattern suggests Iran is retaliating against Project Freedom by hitting Gulf states rather than directly engaging US ships.

### 4. Iran Also Struck Oman — Key Mediator Hit

**[VERIFIED]** In Oman, authorities said a residential building near the strait "was targeted," resulting in two foreign workers wounded, four vehicles damaged and nearby windows shattered.

**Assessment:** This is significant because Oman has been a key mediator in US-Iran talks. Striking Omani territory — even if unintentional — could complicate Pakistani mediation efforts and further isolate Iran diplomatically.

### 5. Project Freedom: Only 2 Ships Through; Shipping Industry Not Buying It

**[VERIFIED]** Day one of Project Freedom, the US military's plan to get shipping moving again through the Strait of Hormuz, was a success, the top US commander in the Middle East says. But independent analysts are more circumspect, pointing out that only two US-flagged vessels made it through the strait under US protection and scaling up the operation to restore pre-war traffic of 120 or vessels a day seems like a long shot, especially if Iran is not cooperating.

**[VERIFIED]** Danish shipping company Maersk said Monday that a U.S.-flagged vessel has exited the Persian Gulf after transiting the Strait of Hormuz under U.S. military protection. The Alliance Fairfax, operated by the company's Farrell Lines subsidiary, had been stranded in the Gulf since the conflict with Iran erupted on Feb. 28 and unable to depart because of the hostilities, according to a statement obtained by CNBC. The company said the journey was completed without incident, and all crew members aboard the vessel were safe and unharmed.

**[REPORTED]** Major shipping company Hapag-Lloyd said transits "are for the moment not possible for our ships."

**[VERIFIED]** Multiple experts told Breaking Defense that while Project Freedom is getting a full-court press from the administration, it is unlikely to open the floodgates and restore mass transit in the region in the short term. Mick Mulroy, a former US deputy assistant secretary of defense for the Middle East, estimated that there are roughly 20,000 captains and crew stuck on ships right now due to the dueling blockades in the international waterway, leading to humanitarian crises. However, it may take time, he added, to see whether ships will trust their ability to get through without being attacked, and, "perhaps more importantly" if the insurance companies will back it.

**[REPORTED]** Eurasia Group warned that, unless there is "buy-in" from Iran or a major naval deployment in the region, Project Freedom will fail. "The US plan will not substantially raise shipping volume through the strait in the near term," consulting firm Eurasia Group wrote in a report on Monday.

**Assessment:** The critical variable is not military capability but commercial confidence. Even if the US can escort ships, insurers and shipping companies won't take the risk without guarantees. Project Freedom may become a long-running armed extraction/escort mission rather than a quick reopening.

### 6. Iran's Top Negotiator Warns of "New Equation"

**[OFFICIAL]** In a post on X Tuesday, Iran's powerful parliamentary speaker and chief negotiator, Mohammad Bagher Qalibaf, accused Washington of undermining shipping security in the Strait of Hormuz. Qalibaf warned that a "new equation" in the strait is taking shape. He signaled that Tehran has yet to fully respond to the U.S.

**[OFFICIAL]** Tehran did not confirm or deny the attacks but early on Tuesday, Iran's Foreign Minister Abbas Araghchi said on X that both the U.S. and the UAE "should be wary of being dragged back into quagmire."

**Assessment:** The phrase "we have not even begun yet" suggests Iran is holding back and could escalate further. This creates uncertainty about whether the current level of conflict is sustainable or a prelude to something larger.

### 7. South Korea "Reviewing" Participation After Ship Fire

**[VERIFIED]** South Korea said Tuesday it will "review its position" on joining U.S. operations in the Strait of Hormuz after President Trump urged Seoul to take part in what he has dubbed "Project Freedom," in the wake of an apparent Iranian attack on one of its ships. An explosion and fire were reported on a South Korean cargo vessel on Monday in the key waterway, which has been effectively blocked since the Mideast war erupted on Feb. 28. South Korea's foreign ministry said Tuesday that all 24 crew members aboard the stricken HMM Namu -- including six South Korean nationals -- were unharmed and the fire on the vessel had been "completely extinguished."

**Assessment:** If Asian nations start participating, it could significantly change dynamics — but it would also mean more countries becoming embroiled in the conflict.

### 8. Oil Prices Volatile — Hegseth Statement Provides Relief

**[VERIFIED]** As of 8:45 a.m. Eastern Time today, oil is trading at $116.55 per barrel, based on the Brent benchmark. That's $1.54 above yesterday morning's level and about $56 higher than where it stood a year ago.

**[VERIFIED]** Oil prices fell Tuesday after Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth said the U.S. ceasefire with Iran remained in place, easing fears that the region would return to full-scale war after attacks on the United Arab Emirates this week. International benchmark Brent crude futures fell more than 2% to $111.45 per barrel by 9:17 a.m. ET, while U.S. West Texas Intermediate futures lost more than 3% to trade at $102.65.

**[VERIFIED]** Brent prices have risen more than 50 percent since the start of the war in late February, amid an estimated daily production shortfall of 14.5 million barrels.

**[REPORTED]** Exxon Mobil CEO Darren Woods warned Friday that the market has not fully absorbed the impact of Iran's blockade of the strait. He warned that even higher oil prices are likely on the way. "It's obvious to most that if you look at the unprecedented disruption in the world supply of oil and natural gas, the market hasn't seen the full impact of that yet," Woods told investors on Exxon's first-quarter earnings call. "There's more to come if the strait remains closed," the CEO said.

**Assessment:** Oil prices are reacting to every statement from Washington and Tehran. The morning spike to $116+ followed by a fall to ~$111 after Hegseth's "ceasefire holds" statement shows markets are pricing on rhetoric as much as fundamentals.

### 9. Energy Crisis Could Extend to 2027

**[REPORTED]** S&P Global Energy warned in a report on Tuesday that even after Hormuz reopens, it will take an additional seven months "at minimum" to fully restore upstream production.

**[VERIFIED]** Even if Washington and Tehran reach a deal to end the war, oil prices are likely to remain elevated for some time due to the backlog of unloaded cargo, damaged regional infrastructure, and the need to clear Iranian mines, according to analysts.

**[VERIFIED]** Chevron CEO Mike Wirth warned it will likely take months for oil exports through the strait to normalize.

**Assessment:** The structural damage to global energy markets is now being quantified. Even an immediate peace deal wouldn't restore normalcy until 2027.

## Analysis: The "Frozen Conflict" Scenario Emerges

**May 5 reveals a potential new trajectory: prolonged "frozen conflict" with periodic combat.**

Both sides appear to have decided — for now — that formal war resumption serves neither interest:

- **For the US:** The "ceasefire" framing avoids War Powers complications and maintains domestic political cover while conducting military operations.
- **For Iran:** Attacking UAE and resisting Project Freedom inflicts costs without triggering full US military response.

**This creates a new equilibrium:**
- Combat exchanges occur but are characterized as "below threshold"
- The ceasefire exists on paper but not in practice
- Neither side declares victory or defeat
- Economic damage continues accumulating

**The danger:** This equilibrium is inherently unstable. Any of the following could collapse it:
- US military casualties
- Major infrastructure hit (Saudi Aramco, UAE pipelines)
- Iranian strike on US warship (not just warning shots)
- Gulf state counter-attack on Iran
- Congressional intervention after May 11
- Public opinion shift (gas hits $5/gallon)
