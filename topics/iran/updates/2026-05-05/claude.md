#### Iran Attacks UAE for Second Consecutive Day
**[VERIFIED]** The UAE, a key American ally, said it came under attack by Iranian missiles and drones for a second straight day on Tuesday. At least three people were wounded in attacks the day before, and a drone sparked a fire at a key oil facility in the eastern emirate of Fujairah.

**[VERIFIED]** The UAE has strongly condemned renewed Iranian attacks on the country, saying they represented a "dangerous escalation" and reserved the right to respond. The Ministry of Defence said air defence systems had engaged 12 ballistic missiles, three cruise missiles and four drones, all launched from Iran on Monday.

**[VERIFIED]** The UAE Ministry of Defence said on May 4, 2026, that air defence systems intercepted 12 ballistic missiles, three cruise missiles, and four UAVs launched from Iran, leaving three people moderately injured. Since the escalation began, UAE air defences have intercepted 549 ballistic missiles, 29 cruise missiles and 2,260 UAVs linked to ongoing attacks.

**Assessment:** Iran is now conducting sustained daily attacks against the UAE during what is nominally a ceasefire. The UAE has issued four missile alerts for the first time since April 8 and shifted schools to remote learning.

#### Iran Also Struck Oman
**[VERIFIED]** In Oman, a separate Iranian strike targeted the town of Bukha in the Musandam Governorate in the country's northwest, wounding two foreign employees of a company in the Tibāt area in stable condition, damaging four vehicles, and causing structural damage to nearby homes.

**Assessment:** This is significant because Oman has been a key mediator in US-Iran talks. Striking Omani territory — even accidentally — could complicate Pakistani mediation efforts.

#### US Sank 6-7 Iranian Boats; Iran Denies
**[VERIFIED]** Iran has fired cruise missiles and drones, and has launched small boats to target U.S.-flagged ships in the Strait of Hormuz, but none were struck, according to the commander of U.S. Central Command. The U.S. used attack helicopters to blow up the small boats and intercept the drones, according to Adm. Brad Cooper.

**[OFFICIAL]** U.S. Forces sank six Iranian small boats near the Strait of Hormuz amid President Donald Trump's "Project Freedom" on Monday. U.S. Central Command announced the engagement in a press call with reporters, saying Iran's military capability has been "dramatically degraded." CENTCOM Commander Adm. Brad Cooper said on the call that Iran historically has deployed "between 20 and 40 small boats" when harassing vessels. "Today, we saw just six, and eliminated them quickly."

**[DISPUTED]** Iranian state media denied Cooper's claim that any of their boats were destroyed. Iranian forces had fired "warning" shots at a U.S. destroyer nearing the Strait of Hormuz in the Sea of Oman, Iran's official news agency IRNA reported.

#### Hegseth: Ceasefire "Not Over"
**[OFFICIAL]** Secretary of Defense Pete Hegseth said Tuesday the U.S.-Iran ceasefire "certainly holds" for now. He called Project Freedom, an effort to guide commercial ships through the Strait of Hormuz — which drew Iranian attacks on Monday — a totally separate and "temporary" operation.

**[OFFICIAL]** Joint Chiefs Chairman Gen. Dan Caine, speaking at the briefing alongside Hegseth, said beyond the operation in the Strait of Hormuz, U.S. forces "remain ready to resume major combat operations against Iran if ordered to do so." "No adversary should mistake our current restraint with a lack of resolve," he added.

**Assessment:** The US is officially drawing a distinction between "Project Freedom" operations and the ceasefire itself, creating semantic space to avoid declaring the ceasefire broken.

#### Iran's Top Negotiator Warns of "New Equation"
**[OFFICIAL]** In a post on X Tuesday, Iran's powerful parliamentary speaker and chief negotiator, Mohammad Bagher Qalibaf, accused Washington of undermining shipping security in the Strait of Hormuz. Qalibaf warned that a "new equation" in the strait is taking shape. He signaled that Tehran has yet to fully respond to the U.S.

**[OFFICIAL]** Qalibaf warned that a "new equation" in the strait is taking shape. He signaled that Tehran has yet to fully respond to the U.S. attempt to reopen the waterway, saying: "We know full well that the continuation of the status quo is intolerable for America; while we have not even begun yet."

**[OFFICIAL]** Iranian Foreign Minister Abbas Araghchi has warned the United States and United Arab Emirates against getting drawn into a "quagmire," saying that recent "events in (the Strait of) Hormuz make clear that there's no military solution to a political crisis." Araghchi also dismissed Washington's "Project Freedom" plan to guide merchant ships out of the strait, writing: "Project Freedom is Project Deadlock."

#### Project Freedom: Only 2 Ships Through; Shipping Companies Wary
**[VERIFIED]** Day one of Project Freedom, the US military's plan to get shipping moving again through the Strait of Hormuz, was a success, the top US commander in the Middle East says. But independent analysts are more circumspect, pointing out that only two US-flagged vessels made it through the strait under US protection and scaling up the operation to restore pre-war traffic of 120 or vessels a day seems like a long shot.

**[REPORTED]** After triggering fresh violence in the Gulf, "Project Freedom" appears to have backfired and shipping executives remain extremely wary about crossing the Strait of Hormuz. "The strait is still incredibly hazardous and I expect most ships will continue to avoid transit until both sides come up with something more concrete," said Tim Huxley, Chairman of Mandarin Shipping.

**[VERIFIED]** So far, just two civilian vessels, both U.S.-flagged merchant ships, are known to have passed through the strait as part of the lane the U.S. says it has created. Hamburg, Germany-based shipping company Hapag-Lloyd AG said in a statement: "Transits through the Strait of Hormuz are for the moment not possible for our ships."

#### South Korean Ship Fire; Seoul "Reviewing" Participation
**[VERIFIED]** South Korea said Tuesday that a fire on a South Korean-operated vessel in the Strait of Hormuz has been fully extinguished and that none of the 24 crewmembers were hurt. Officials earlier said an explosion and fire broke out Monday evening on the Panama-flagged ship operated by South Korean shipping company HMM and that the cause was not immediately known.

**[REPORTED]** South Korea said Tuesday it will "review its position" on joining US operations in the Strait of Hormuz after President Trump urged Seoul to take part. An explosion and fire were reported on a South Korean cargo vessel on Monday in the key waterway. South Korea's foreign ministry said Tuesday that all 24 crew members aboard the stricken HMM Namu — including six South Korean nationals — were unharmed.

France and the U.K. have led efforts to build a coalition of nations willing to help ensure the strait remains safe and passable after the war.

#### Oil Prices Volatile
**[VERIFIED]** As of 8:45 a.m. Eastern Time today, oil is trading at $116.55 per barrel, based on the Brent benchmark. That's $1.54 above yesterday morning's level and about $56 higher than where it stood a year ago.

**[VERIFIED]** International benchmark Brent crude futures fell more than 2% to $111.45 per barrel by 9:17 a.m. ET, while U.S. West Texas Intermediate futures lost more than 3% to trade at $102.65.

#### Energy Crisis Could Extend to 2027
**[REPORTED]** The energy crisis won't simply end once the Strait of Hormuz reopens and could even stretch into 2027. Even after Hormuz reopens, it will take an additional seven months "at minimum" to fully restore upstream production, S&P Global Energy warned in a report on Tuesday.

**[REPORTED]** The war in the Middle East has caused oil demand to collapse at a historic pace. Oil demand is currently experiencing the sharpest decline on record outside of the pandemic collapse, according to S&P Global Energy. S&P expects total demand for oil and petroleum products like gasoline and jet fuel to tumble by almost 5 million barrels per day this quarter. The findings demonstrate "demand destruction."
