### Key Developments

#### US Aircraft Downed Over Iran — Search for Pilot Underway

**[CLAIMED]** A second US fifth-generation F-35 fighter jet has reportedly been shot down over central Iran by an advanced air-defence system operated by the Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps (IRGC), Iranian state media said on Friday. According to Press TV, the aircraft was brought down using a newly deployed air-defence system belonging to the IRGC Aerospace Force.

**[REPORTED]** Although Iranian media claimed an F-35 has been shot down, images from the crash site clearly show debris belonging to an F-15E Strike Eagle. In particular, the remains of one of the tails still sport the U.S. Air Force in Europe badge as well as a red tail flash belonging to the 494th Fighter Squadron based at RAF Lakenheath.

**[UNCONFIRMED]** Tasnim, the semi-official news agency in Iran associated with the Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps, claims that U.S. attempts to rescue a downed fighter pilot have failed. The report continues: "The Americans have been conducting search operations for hours using Black Hawk helicopters and a Hercules 130 aircraft to rescue their fighter pilot who was shot down by Iranian armed forces; however, their attempt has failed."

**[REPORTED]** Iran announced a reward will be given to the person who captures the American pilot alive. The police department of the Kohgiluyeh region has promised a reward to those who assist in capturing the pilot or pilots of a US fighter jet shot down in Iranian airspace.

**[REPORTED]** If confirmed, this would be the fourth F-15E lost by the U.S. Air Force since the beginning of Operation Epic Fury.

**Assessment:** This appears to be the most significant US aircraft loss of the war to date. While Iranian claims of downing an F-35 are contradicted by debris evidence showing F-15E wreckage, a US combat aircraft has almost certainly been lost. The fate of the crew—one or two pilots depending on aircraft type—remains unclear and could become a major escalation trigger if captured.

#### Trump Escalates Infrastructure Threats After Bridge Strike

**[VERIFIED]** On 2 April, Trump made a speech addressing the nation in which he said the attacks on Iran will escalate over the next two to three weeks to "bring them back to the Stone Ages, where they belong." Two US strikes on the B1 bridge between Tehran and Karaj, which was described as the highest bridge in the Middle East, reportedly killed eight people and wounded 95 others, causing it to collapse.

**[OFFICIAL]** US officials said the bridge bombed in Iran was used to transport material for Iranian military drones. But Iran insisted the bridge was civilian infrastructure, which, if true, would make the attack a violation of international law.

**[OFFICIAL]** After destroying a major bridge in Iran, US President Donald Trump pledges more strikes on civilian facilities and power plants, despite warnings of possible US war crimes. President Masoud Pezeshkian calls Trump's threat to send Iran "back to the Stone Age" an admission of intent to commit a 'massive war crime'.

**[VERIFIED]** The US and Israel expanded their attacks on Iran, hitting a century-old medical research centre in Tehran, steel plants and a bridge near the capital. A drone strike also hit a Red Crescent aid warehouse in Iran's Bushehr province.

**[REPORTED]** Iran's Ministry of Foreign Affairs said more than 600 schools and education centres have been hit.

**Assessment:** The bridge strike and explicit threat to target power plants represent a significant escalation toward civilian infrastructure. These actions increase the humanitarian toll and could constitute war crimes under international law.

#### Hegseth Fires Army's Top General During Wartime

**[VERIFIED]** The defense secretary fired the Army chief of staff, Gen. Randy George, as well as the chief of chaplains and the commanding general of Army Transformation and Training Command. Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth has ousted the Army chief of staff.

**[OFFICIAL]** One of the sources said Hegseth wants someone in the role who will implement President Trump and Hegseth's vision for the Army. Chief Pentagon spokesperson Sean Parnell said in a statement that George "will be retiring from his position as the 41st Chief of Staff of the Army effective immediately."

**[REPORTED]** The New York Times said the removal was related to clashes between George and Hegseth over the latter's decision to single out and block the promotion of four army officers. Most of the officers on the list are white men, while two blocked by Hegseth are Black, and the other two are women. Hegseth's decision led senior military officers to question whether racial or gender bias was at play.

**[VERIFIED]** With George's ouster, Hegseth has nearly remade the entire Joint Chiefs of Staff. Gen. Eric M. Smith, commandant of the Marine Corps, and Gen. B. Chance Saltzman, head of the Space Force, are the only two remaining since Hegseth took office in January 2025.

**[REPORTED]** LaNeve served as a military aide to Hegseth and will serve as acting Army chief of staff.

**Assessment:** Firing the Army's top officer during an active war is extraordinary. This continues Hegseth's pattern of replacing Biden-era military leadership, but doing so mid-conflict raises questions about command stability and suggests potential internal dissent over war strategy.

#### Iran Strikes Kuwait Power/Desalination Infrastructure (Second Time)

**[VERIFIED]** Gulf nations continue to face retaliatory strikes on the 35th day of the war by the United States and Israel on Iran. Kuwaiti authorities blamed Iran for the strike before midday on Friday. The plant was not identified and the extent of the damage isn't known.

**[OFFICIAL]** Following the attack, Iran's Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps (IRGC) denied responsibility and shifted blame to Israel. Iran "condemn[s] this inhuman act and announce that the American bases and military in the region and the military and security centres of the Zionist regime in the occupied Palestinian territories" remain targets.

**[VERIFIED]** This is the second attack on Kuwait's power and desalination facilities in less than a week. The 346,000 b/d Mina al-Ahmadi refinery came under attack for the third time in two weeks early on Friday. The country's 454,000 b/d Mina Abdullah refinery was also struck in the middle of March.

**[VERIFIED]** Kuwait's air defenses have intercepted more than 500 drones and 300 ballistic missiles since the US-Israel war with Iran began on 28 February.

**[REPORTED]** On Friday, Crown Prince Sheikh Sabah Khaled and UK Prime Minister Keir Starmer discussed the deployment of a British ground-based air defence system in Kuwait.

**Assessment:** Iran is systematically targeting Gulf state critical infrastructure—water, power, and oil refining—as retaliation. The UK's offer to deploy air defense systems to Kuwait represents direct military support to US-allied Gulf states and further entanglement.

#### Oil: Spot Prices Hit 2008 Highs, Futures Above $109

**[VERIFIED]** The world's most important price for real-world oil barrels surged above $140 on Thursday, the highest since 2008. Dated Brent, the price of shipments bought and sold in the North Sea, reached $141.37, surpassing levels seen when Russia invaded Ukraine.

**[VERIFIED]** The high price for more immediate oil deliveries points to the tightness of physical supply right now due to the huge disruption trigged by the Iran's closure of the Strait of Hormuz. The price was $32.33 higher than the Brent crude futures contract for June delivery, which closed at $109.03 on Thursday. The futures price is "almost giving a false sense of security that things are not that stressed," said Amrita Sen, founder of Energy Aspects.

**[VERIFIED]** U.S. West Texas Intermediate crude futures for May rose more than 11%, or $11.42, to close at $111.54 per barrel. International benchmark Brent crude gained nearly 8%, or $7.87, to settle at $109.03.

**Assessment:** The $32+ gap between spot and futures prices signals acute physical supply shortages not fully captured in futures markets. Real-world oil buyers are paying crisis-level premiums not seen since the 2008 financial crisis.

#### UN Security Council Hormuz Vote Delayed; China Opposes Force

**[VERIFIED]** Diplomats said Bahrain, the current chair of the Security Council, finalized a draft resolution that would authorize "all defensive means necessary" to protect commercial shipping.

**[OFFICIAL]** In remarks to the Security Council on Thursday morning, China's U.N. envoy Fu Cong opposed authorizing force. Such a move would be "legitimizing the unlawful and indiscriminate use of force, which would inevitably lead to further escalation of the situation and lead to serious consequences," he added.

**[VERIFIED]** A fourth draft of the resolution was put under a so-called silence procedure for approval until Thursday noon, but the silence had been broken, by China, France and Russia.

**[VERIFIED]** The draft resolution has been molded in a bid to rally several countries that have appeared skeptical. Revised wording no longer explicitly invokes Chapter 7 of the UN Charter, which allows the Security Council to authorize armed force to restore peace.

**Assessment:** The vote delay—ostensibly for Good Friday but practically due to Chinese, Russian, and French opposition—signals the resolution faces likely failure. Even watered-down language authorizing "defensive means" faces veto threats.

#### UK Hormuz Summit Concludes — Military Planners to Meet

**[OFFICIAL]** Foreign Secretary Yvette Cooper convened a meeting of over 40 countries from every continent of the world, as well as key international organisations including the International Maritime Organisation and the European Union. This meeting showed clearly the determination of the international community to secure freedom of navigation and re-open the Strait of Hormuz.

**[VERIFIED]** Although the meeting ended without any specific agreements, there was a consensus Iran should not be able to introduce transit fees on ships using the waterway and all nations should be able to use it freely. The next stage of talks will be held when military planners meet next week to discuss options, including potential mine-clearing work and providing a reassurance force for commercial shipping.

**[OFFICIAL]** Partners today called for the immediate and unconditional reopening of the Strait and respect for the fundamental principles of freedom of navigation. We discussed increasing international diplomatic pressure, exploring coordinated economic and political measures such as sanctions.

**Assessment:** The summit produced consensus on principles but no concrete action. The planned military planners' meeting next week will determine whether any coordinated response is feasible while fighting continues.

#### Iran Seeks Permanent Hormuz Control

**[REPORTED]** Iran said yesterday it would seek to oversee shipping traffic through the Strait of Hormuz even after the war. An Iranian deputy foreign minister said that though Iran was drafting a protocol for his country and Oman to monitor transit through the strait, Iran would not be imposing restrictions.

**[REPORTED]** Since the war began, Iran has demanded international recognition of its right to exercise authority over the Strait of Hormuz as one of its five conditions for calling a ceasefire.

**Assessment:** Iran is positioning for permanent control over Hormuz as a war outcome—institutionalizing the "Tehran toll booth" arrangement. This represents a major shift in regional power dynamics if achieved.

### Bottom Line

Day 35 marks a potential inflection point. The apparent loss of a US combat aircraft over Iran—with the pilot's fate unknown—introduces hostage crisis dynamics that could transform the conflict's trajectory. Trump's explicit threats to target power plants and bridges represent escalation toward civilian infrastructure warfare, while Iran's continued strikes on Gulf desalination facilities show it is willing to threaten regional water security.

The firing of Army Chief of Staff Gen. George during an active war is unprecedented in modern US history and signals either profound discord within the military leadership or a deliberate purge of those who might constrain the war's expansion. With the UN Security Council resolution stalled and spot oil prices hitting 2008 crisis levels, the international community appears unable to coordinate an effective response.

The April 6 Hormuz deadline looms with no indication Iran will comply. The next 72 hours may determine whether this conflict escalates into direct attacks on civilian power infrastructure across the region.
