## Critical Assessments

### The Three Stress Points

**1. Lebanon Precondition Crisis**

Iran has now explicitly conditioned continued negotiations on Israeli restraint in Beirut. The departure of an Iranian delegation to Islamabad has already been postponed several times due to Israeli "aggression." If Israel strikes central Beirut again before or during talks, Iran may walk out.

**2. Hormuz "Opening" Is Not Opening**

| Pre-war traffic | Ceasefire promise | Actual traffic | Iran's new cap |
|-----------------|-------------------|----------------|----------------|
| 100-130 ships/day | "Complete, immediate, safe opening" | 5-9 ships in first 48 hours | 15 ships/day max |

Shipping will return to the Strait of Hormuz not when it is declared open, but when it is assessed to be safe enough. That will require a sustained period without attacks, a visible international effort to secure the waterway, and clear signalling that the rules governing international straits will be upheld. Until then, the ships will wait.

**3. The Three-Version Problem Persists**

- **Iran's 10-point proposal:** Includes Lebanon, enrichment rights, Hormuz oversight
- **US position:** White House press secretary Karoline Leavitt said Iran's 10-point proposal was "literally thrown in the garbage by President Trump." Trump initially called a plan from Iran "workable," but Leavitt said he was referring to a different set of unspecified points.
- **Pakistan's position:** When the ceasefire was first announced, Pakistan's Prime Minister Shehbaz Sharif said, "Iran and the United States of America, along with their allies, have agreed to an immediate ceasefire everywhere, including Lebanon and elsewhere, effective immediately."

### The Tolls Battleground

**[REPORTED]** Iran is seeking payment for Hormuz transits, creating a compliance flashpoint:
- Iran's suggestion that ships may need to pay for passage has sparked intense debate among legal experts. Some have described the idea as a "tollbooth" system that could set a dangerous precedent for international waterways. Under widely accepted maritime norms, straits used for global navigation are expected to remain open to transit without interference.
- Charging vessels for passage would cut directly against that principle and set a dangerous precedent for other strategic waterways.
- If Iran insists on tolling while the US insists on "unimpeded" passage, Hormuz may stay partially closed without overt hostilities—yet still trigger US claims of Iranian "noncompliance."

## Emerging Issue: The "Tollbooth" Precedent

A new structural issue has emerged that goes beyond the ceasefire itself: Charging vessels for passage would cut directly against the principle of freedom of navigation and set a dangerous precedent for other strategic waterways. There are early signs Iran is testing the boundaries. Reports of radio calls warning vessels they require approval to transit, and suggestions that ships should notify Iranian authorities before transiting, point to an attempt to exert greater control over the strait. That should be resisted. Allowing a toll, or even limited restrictions, to take hold in the Strait of Hormuz would have far-reaching consequences, undermining the central principle of maritime trade: freedom of navigation.

While President Donald Trump suggested in an interview with ABC News that the U.S. and Iran could establish a "joint venture" to charge tolls—a remarkable potential concession—European allies have firmly rejected any toll system.

This creates a trilemma:
- **Accept tolls:** Legitimizes Iranian control, sets global precedent
- **Enforce free passage:** Requires military action, violates ceasefire
- **Remain deadlocked:** Strait stays effectively closed, global economic damage continues
