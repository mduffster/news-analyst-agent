## What Changed in the Last 24 Hours

### Lebanon Diplomacy: Aoun-Netanyahu Call Collapses

**[OFFICIAL]** Trump announced late Wednesday that the leaders of Israel and Lebanon would speak Thursday—the first such direct contact in 34 years. United States President Donald Trump announced that the leaders of Israel and Lebanon will speak to each other for the first time in 34 years on Thursday. The announcement, made on Trump's Truth Social account on Wednesday, came a day after Israeli and Lebanese ambassadors held their first direct diplomatic talks in more than three decades in Washington, DC.

**[VERIFIED]** However, the call did not happen. "Netanyahu and Trump want this conversation for public relations purposes." Lebanese President Joseph Aoun has told U.S. Secretary of State Marco Rubio he would not speak with Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu on the phone.

**[REPORTED]** Aoun spoke with Parliament Speaker Nabih Berri, who advised him to refuse the call. Druze leader Walid Jumblatt, who maintains a political alliance with Hezbollah, also reportedly advised Aoun against the conversation.

**[VERIFIED]** Meanwhile, Israel's security cabinet discussed a potential Lebanon ceasefire but made no decision. "Our assessment is that within a few days, we will have no choice but to fully cease fire in Lebanon," a senior Israeli political source told Channel 12. The network said that the US is pressing Israel to agree to a temporary one-week ceasefire.

**[OFFICIAL]** The US denied pressuring Israel. The US denied pressuring Israel to agree to a ceasefire in Lebanon. "This is not something we have asked for, nor is it part of the peace negotiations with Iran, but the president would welcome the end of hostilities in Lebanon as part of a peace agreement between Israel and Lebanon."

**Assessment:** The Aoun-Netanyahu call collapse demonstrates the political constraints facing Lebanon's government. Direct talks with Israel while under bombardment risks domestic backlash from Hezbollah and its allies. Israel's security cabinet indecision suggests Netanyahu is under cross-pressures from the US (seeking ceasefire) and his coalition (opposing territorial concessions).

### US-Iran: Ceasefire Extension Remains Unconfirmed

**[REPORTED]** The US and Iran are considering a two-week ceasefire extension to allow more time to negotiate a peace deal, according to a person familiar with the matter, reducing the risk of renewed fighting despite an intensifying standoff over the Strait of Hormuz. With the initial truce due to expire next week, mediators are seeking technical talks to overcome the most contentious issues preventing a longer-term agreement.

**[OFFICIAL]** However, formal agreement remains elusive. The Associated Press, citing regional officials, reported Wednesday morning that the U.S. and Iran have an "in principle agreement" to extend their fragile two-week ceasefire in order to allow for more diplomacy. But a senior U.S. official told CNBC that the U.S. "has not formally agreed to an extension of the ceasefire." "There is continued engagement between the U.S. and Iran to reach a deal."

**[REPORTED]** Pakistani mediation continues to advance. Iranian state media announced that a Pakistani delegation, led by Pakistan's Army Chief Asim Munir and Interior Minister Mohsin Naqvi, had arrived in Iran to deliver a message from the US regarding planning for a second round of ceasefire negotiations. A senior Iranian source told Reuters that the delegation includes Pakistan's army chief and that they will arrive in Tehran soon in order to work to "narrow gaps between the US and Iran."

**[REPORTED]** Pakistani officials reportedly expect a nuclear breakthrough. Sources told Al Jazeera they anticipate progress on Iran's nuclear programme, with the central sticking point being enrichment duration—"between five years of no enrichment to 20 years of no enrichment"—and handling Iran's 440 kg of highly enriched uranium.

**[OFFICIAL]** No date has been set for a second round of talks between the U.S. and Iran, Pakistan's foreign ministry has said, adding that a ceasefire in Lebanon is "essential."

**Assessment:** The situation is increasingly defined by a gap between formal positions (no extension agreed) and informal progress (mediators report movement). Pakistan's "essential" linkage of Lebanon ceasefire to Iran talks is significant—it signals that Iran-backed pressure for a Lebanon truce is a blocking issue for any broader deal.

### IEA Warning: Energy Crisis Reaching Critical Stage

**[OFFICIAL]** Europe has "maybe 6 weeks or so (of) jet fuel left," the head of the International Energy Agency said Thursday. IEA Executive Director Fatih Birol painted a sobering picture of the global repercussions of what he called "the largest energy crisis we have ever faced," stemming from the pinch-off of oil, gas and other vital supplies through the Strait of Hormuz.

**[OFFICIAL]** Birol said people can expect higher gasoline prices and high electricity prices. He also said that in Europe, "I can tell you soon we will hear the news that some of the flights from city A to city B might be canceled as a result of lack of jet fuel."

**[VERIFIED]** Economic pain will be felt unevenly and "the countries who will suffer the most will not be those whose voice are heard a lot. It will be mainly the developing countries. Poorer countries in Asia, in Africa and in Latin America," said the Turkish economist. But without a settlement of the Iran war that permanently reopens the Strait of Hormuz, "Everybody is going to suffer."

**Assessment:** The IEA warning adds significant pressure to the diplomatic timeline. Six weeks of jet fuel means European flight cancellations could begin by late May if the strait remains closed. This creates a hard deadline for Western governments and likely increases pressure for a deal—but also raises stakes for Iran's leverage.

**[VERIFIED]** The U.S. military says it has turned back 13 ships since launching its blockade of Iranian ports.

**[REPORTED]** The Washington Post reported that the US Department of Defense plans to deploy an additional 6,000 troops to the region in the coming days, followed by a further 4,200 by the end of the month—totaling 10,200 additional troops.

**[OFFICIAL]** Senate Republicans blocked for a fourth time a Democratic measure that would prohibit Trump from taking further military action in Iran without congressional approval. The war powers resolution was voted down 47-52, largely along party lines.

**Assessment:** The US is maintaining maximum pressure while diplomacy proceeds. The troop deployment and Hegseth's rhetoric serve as leverage, signaling the US is prepared for renewed operations if talks fail. The fourth Senate defeat of war powers constraints confirms Trump has free rein.

### UK-France Hormuz Summit: Tomorrow (April 17)

**[OFFICIAL]** France and the United Kingdom are stepping forward with a coordinated diplomatic initiative. French President Emmanuel Macron and British Prime Minister Keir Starmer will co-chair a virtual summit this Friday, April 17, 2026, bringing together around 40 countries willing to support maritime security in the region.

**[REPORTED]** According to a senior German official, Germany is also highly likely to participate in this plan. Until now, Germany had maintained a cautious stance regarding military intervention in the Strait of Hormuz. Germany is expected to announce its intention to participate as early as April 16.

**[REPORTED]** This initiative has three main objectives: to assist the movement of hundreds of ships stranded in the strait, to remove large numbers of naval mines, and to establish a regular military escort and surveillance system to ensure safe navigation. In particular, mine removal is an area where Europe has an advantage over the United States, with more than 150 related vessels in operation.

**Assessment:** Tomorrow's summit will test whether European powers can establish a credible alternative to US strategy. Germany's expected participation strengthens the initiative significantly. The three objectives (stranded ships, mine removal, escort system) address practical concerns that could accelerate Hormuz reopening once fighting stops.

### Bint Jbeil: Fighting Continues

**[VERIFIED]** The Israeli military has nearly completed the capture of the Hezbollah stronghold of Bint Jbeil in southern Lebanon, killing more than 100 members of the terror group in the area in the process, the Israel Defense Forces said Monday.

**[VERIFIED]** IDF Chief of Staff instructed troops to turn the area up to the Litani River into a "kill zone for Hezbollah terrorists," and said forces were operating in several sectors, including Bint Jbeil and the Beit Lif area. "We are paying a heavy price, but we understand that this is our mission, and we will continue to advance, strike Hezbollah and destroy terrorist infrastructure."

**[VERIFIED]** The organization has suffered more than 1,700 dead since the start of the campaign.

**[VERIFIED]** Officials in Jerusalem say that at no stage did Israel agree to stop operations in southern Lebanon, and that it intends to continue those operations up to the Litani River and maintain the security zone. At the same time, Israel is not ruling out stopping attacks deep inside Lebanon, describing that as a possible ceasefire.

**Assessment:** Israel is defining a "partial ceasefire" that would halt strikes on Beirut while continuing ground operations south of the Litani. This approach could satisfy both US pressure (de-escalation signal) and Israeli security demands (buffer zone), but Hezbollah and Lebanon reject any agreement that doesn't include full Israeli withdrawal.

## Emerging Analysis: The Lebanon Blocking Problem

The collapse of the Aoun-Netanyahu call illuminates a fundamental structural problem in the peace process:

**Linkage intensifying:**
- A ceasefire in Lebanon "is as important as a ceasefire in Iran," Iran's powerful parliament speaker said in a call with his Lebanese counterpart.
- Pakistan's foreign ministry says a ceasefire in Lebanon is "essential" for Iran peace talks.
- Iran explicitly demands Lebanon inclusion in any comprehensive deal

**Lebanon's constraints:**
- Aoun cannot talk to Netanyahu while Israel bombs Lebanon—domestic backlash risk
- "You cannot conduct negotiations to stop the fighting if you are under fire and under pressure," a Hezbollah member of the Lebanese parliament told Al Jazeera.
- Hezbollah opposes direct talks absent ceasefire and Israeli withdrawal

**Israel's position:**
- Officials in Jerusalem say that at no stage did Israel agree to stop operations in southern Lebanon, and that it intends to continue those operations up to the Litani River.
- Israel willing to pause Beirut strikes but not southern operations
- Netanyahu needs Bint Jbeil victory for political cover

**US position:**
- Officially denies linkage between Iran and Lebanon tracks
- But clearly pressing Israel toward ceasefire to facilitate Iran deal
- While the two tracks are officially separate, a Lebanon ceasefire could help President Trump reach a peace deal with Iran.

**Assessment:** The Lebanon track has become the potential spoiler for the Iran deal. Iran and Pakistan insist on linkage; the US denies it but is pressuring Israel anyway; Israel refuses a full ceasefire; and Lebanon cannot negotiate under fire. A "partial ceasefire" (Beirut pause only) may emerge as a face-saving compromise, but Hezbollah is unlikely to accept any deal that legitimizes Israeli occupation of southern Lebanon.
