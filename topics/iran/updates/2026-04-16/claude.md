### What Changed in the Last 24 Hours

#### US-Iran Talks: "Major Breakthrough" Expected?

**[REPORTED]** Pakistani officials are expecting a "major breakthrough" in talks between Iran and the United States on Tehran's nuclear programme, sources have told Al Jazeera, as Islamabad steps up diplomatic efforts to end a war that has killed thousands of people.

**[OFFICIAL]** The optimism on Wednesday came as a high-level Pakistani delegation, headed by Army Chief Asim Munir, arrived in Tehran to deliver a message from the US to the Iranian leadership, according to Iran's Press TV broadcaster.

**[REPORTED]** The central sticking point remains the duration of any enrichment freeze by Iran and the country's stockpile of 440 kilogrammes of highly enriched uranium. "We know that both sides are essentially stuck on between five years of no enrichment to 20 years of no enrichment. And there is a solution in the middle."

**[REPORTED]** There are multiple options for Iran's enriched material – "whether sending it abroad to a third party or bringing it down to either uranium in its natural form or up to 3 percent."

**[OFFICIAL]** The Trump administration feels "good about prospects of a deal" with Iran, the White House said Wednesday, while noting that Pakistan would be the likely location of a potential second round of talks.

**[OFFICIAL]** The US and Iran are considering a two-week ceasefire extension to allow more time to negotiate a peace deal, according to a person familiar with the matter. With the initial truce due to expire next week, mediators are seeking technical talks to overcome the most contentious issues preventing a longer-term agreement.

**Assessment:** Pakistani mediation appears to be gaining traction on the nuclear file. If both sides can agree on enrichment duration (somewhere between 5-20 years) and a mechanism for handling Iran's HEU stockpile, this would be significant progress. However, the gap remains substantial.

#### US Military Posture: Hegseth's Warning

**[OFFICIAL]** United States Defence Secretary Pete Hegseth has threatened Iran, saying US forces are ready to restart combat if Tehran does not agree to a deal.

**[OFFICIAL]** Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth warned Iran "we're watching you" and said the U.S. was "locked and loaded" for renewed combat if Tehran didn't agree to a peace deal.

**[VERIFIED]** The U.S. military says it has turned back 13 ships since launching its blockade of Iranian ports.

**[REPORTED]** The Washington Post reported that the US Department of Defense plans to deploy an additional 6,000 troops to the region in the coming days, followed by a further 4,200 by the end of the month.

**[OFFICIAL]** For the fourth time this year, the Senate rejected a measure aimed at restricting Trump's war powers by requiring congressional approval for any future military action against Iran. Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent said the US is "now willing to apply secondary sanctions" on countries that buy Iranian oil or take Tehran's money.

**Assessment:** The US is adopting a "good cop/bad cop" strategy — diplomatic optimism from the White House combined with military threats from Pentagon. The troop deployment signals preparation for potential resumed combat if talks fail before April 21.

#### Iran's Response: Mixed Signals

**[OFFICIAL]** "Trump wants to become the police of the Strait of Hormuz. Is this really your job? Is this the job of a powerful army like the U.S.?" Mohsen Rezaei, a former commander-in-chief of Iran's Revolutionary Guards who was named as a military adviser by Khamenei last month, told state TV. "These ships of yours will be sunk by our first missiles and have created a great danger for the U.S. military."

**[OFFICIAL]** Rezaei also added, without giving further details: "I am not in favour of extending the ceasefire at all and this is a personal view."

**[OFFICIAL]** Iranian Foreign Minister Abbas Araghchi said Tehran remains committed to promoting peace and stability in the region. "Our commitment to promoting peace and stability in the region remains firm, and this commitment is shared between us."

**[REPORTED]** Iran's Fars News Agency separately reported that a sanctioned Iranian supertanker had crossed the Strait of Hormuz despite the blockade, though it gave no further details.

**Assessment:** Iran is maintaining a dual posture — hardliners like Rezaei signal defiance while diplomats like Araghchi express commitment to peace. The claim of a supertanker crossing the blockade, if true, would be a significant challenge to US enforcement claims.

#### Israel-Lebanon: Trump Announcement vs. Lebanese Denial

**[OFFICIAL]** U.S. President Donald Trump on Wednesday announced that talks between Israel and Lebanon will begin Thursday. In a Truth Social post published just before midnight, Trump said he was "trying to get a little breathing room between Israel and Lebanon." "It has been a long time since the two leaders have spoken, like 34 years," he added.

**[VERIFIED]** Lebanese officials told news agencies they had "no ⁠information" ⁠about any upcoming contact or talks with Israel's leadership, while Aoun stressed the importance of a ceasefire before any direct negotiations. A ceasefire is a "natural entry point for direct negotiations between the two countries," Aoun said.

**[VERIFIED]** A member of Israel's security Cabinet confirmed the news, though there was no public confirmation from Lebanon.

**[OFFICIAL]** Netanyahu said in a video statement that Israel was about to defeat the Hezbollah stronghold of Bint Jbeil, and that he had instructed Israeli forces to extend the security zone eastward to the slopes of Mount Hermon. He said ongoing negotiations with Lebanon were focused on disarming Hezbollah and achieving sustainable peace.

**[OFFICIAL]** Iran's powerful parliament speaker said a ceasefire in Lebanon "is as important as a ceasefire in Iran" in a call with his Lebanese counterpart.

**[OFFICIAL]** Pakistan's foreign ministry said no date has been set for a second round of talks between the U.S. and Iran, adding that a ceasefire in Lebanon is "essential."

**Assessment:** Trump appears to be attempting to force diplomatic momentum by publicly announcing talks that have not been agreed to by Lebanon. Aoun's insistence on a ceasefire first creates a chicken-and-egg problem. The linkage between Lebanon and Iran talks is becoming more explicit from all parties.

#### Lebanon Ground Operations: Continued

**[VERIFIED]** Israel is continuing its strikes on southern Lebanon in its war with Iran-backed Hezbollah but had not targeted the Lebanese capital since a series of attacks across the country on April 8 that killed more than 350 people. Lebanon's state-run National News Agency reported two separate Israeli strikes on two vehicles, one in the seafront town of Saadiyat and another on a coastal highway in neighboring Jiyeh, around 12 miles south of Beirut. NNA also reported several other strikes across southern Lebanon.

**[VERIFIED]** The Israeli military said it had detected "approximately 30 launches" by Hezbollah militants toward Israel since the early hours of Wednesday. Hezbollah said it launched rockets at 10 northern Israeli areas.

**[VERIFIED]** On the ground, the Israeli army again on Thursday called on civilians to evacuate the entire area of southern Lebanon up to the Zahrani River, about 40 kilometers north of the border.

**[VERIFIED]** Lebanon's state-run National News Agency reported that the Israeli military struck and entirely destroyed a bridge [over the Litani]. The Israeli military said it did not strike the bridge but "struck adjacent to it." It comes amid Israeli attacks that have targeted a number of bridges across the Litani, severing the south's connection to the rest of the country.

**Assessment:** Israel continues expanding its security zone while diplomatic talks proceed — a pattern consistent with creating "facts on the ground" before any negotiated settlement.

#### UK-France Hormuz Summit: Tomorrow (April 17)

**[OFFICIAL]** On 14 April, French president Emmanuel Macron and British prime minister Keir Starmer announced that they are holding an online meeting on 17 April for countries interested in being involved in a "defensive multilateral mission" to maintaining the Strait of Hormuz open.

**[REPORTED]** According to French and British officials, the United States is not expected to attend this meeting. China and India have also been invited, but their participation remains uncertain.

**[REPORTED]** According to a senior German official, Germany is also highly likely to participate in this plan. Until now, Germany had maintained a cautious stance regarding military intervention in the Strait of Hormuz. Germany is expected to announce its intention to participate as early as April 16.

**[REPORTED]** This initiative has three main objectives: to assist the movement of hundreds of ships stranded in the strait, to remove large numbers of naval mines, and to establish a regular military escort and surveillance system to ensure safe navigation. In particular, mine removal is an area where Europe has an advantage over the United States, with more than 150 related vessels in operation.

**Assessment:** Tomorrow's summit will be a key test of whether European powers can establish a credible alternative to US blockade strategy. Germany's expected participation would significantly strengthen the initiative.

### Bottom Line

**Optimism rising, but significant gaps remain.**

The past 24 hours produced the most concrete signs of diplomatic progress since the ceasefire began:

- **Positive**: Pakistan expects "major breakthrough" on nuclear; AP reports "in principle" ceasefire extension; White House "feels good" about deal prospects; oil prices falling; markets rallying
- **Negative**: Hegseth warns US "locked and loaded"; 10,200 more troops deploying; Lebanon talks confused by Trump announcement vs. Lebanese denial; ceasefire still not formally extended

Pakistani diplomatic momentum and reported "in principle" extension agreement justify a meaningful reduction in collapse probability. However, the US troop deployment and Hegseth's warnings indicate the administration is hedging its bets.
