### US Globalizes Interdiction Campaign

INDOPACOM is the US Indo-Pacific Command, responsible for the Indian and Pacific oceans and surrounding areas. INDOPACOM is not responsible for the Persian Gulf or the Strait of Hormuz, which fall under the US Central Command. However, the Pentagon states that the Tifani was tied to Iranian smuggling activity. "As we have made clear, we will pursue global maritime enforcement efforts to disrupt illicit networks and interdict sanctioned vessels providing material support to Iran - anywhere they operate," the Pentagon wrote.

The M/V Tifani, the oil tanker boarded by U.S. forces in the Indian Ocean, around 500 nautical miles off of the western coast of Sumatra. According to MarineTraffic data, the Tifani has a carrying capacity of nearly 300,000 tonnes and is currently laden with cargo. The ship spent multiple hours off the coast of Galle, Sri Lanka on April 18 before travelling west in the direction of Malaysia. It continued on this path until it made a sharp turn, heading south before it was intercepted by the United States.

At least 26 vessels linked to Iran's so-called "shadow fleet" have bypassed the US naval blockade, Lloyd's List reported on Monday.

**Assessment:** This is a meaningful escalation in geographic scope. The US is signaling it will treat the blockade/interdiction concept as effectively globalized pressure on Iran-linked shipping, not a narrow Hormuz enforcement problem. This increases Iran's incentive to respond asymmetrically (proxy action, cyber, or reciprocal seizures) because it widens the set of Iranian interests at risk.

### Strait of Hormuz Remains Closed; Traffic at Virtual Standstill

US forces seized an Iranian-flagged cargo ship in the Gulf of Oman, triggering a virtual standstill in Strait of Hormuz traffic. Transits plunged from more than 20 on Saturday to zero by Sunday, far below the pre-war daily average of 138.

According to the IEA, resuming flows through the Strait of Hormuz remains the single most important variable in easing the pressure on energy supplies, prices and the global economy.

The economic costs of the conflict are mounting as the Strait of Hormuz — which normally carries roughly one-fifth of global oil supply — has been effectively closed for nearly two months. Analysts estimate supply disruptions of around 13 million barrels of crude, condensates and natural gas liquids per day. "That cumulative effect has already breached above half a billion barrels," with warnings that even an imminent deal announcement would not immediately unwind the damage.

## Analysis: The Paradox of Simultaneous Escalation and Diplomacy

**What's happening:**

The US is pursuing two tracks simultaneously — escalating maritime enforcement while preparing for talks. The Tifani seizure in the Indo-Pacific, announced hours before both delegations were expected to travel to Islamabad, exemplifies this approach. Trump's statement that he's "ready to go militarily" and "expects to be bombing" creates negotiating leverage but also risks torpedoing diplomacy.

**Iran's calculation:**

"Iran does not seek to re-engage in renewed conflict," analyst Muhammad Khatibi told Al Jazeera. "But from Tehran's perspective, this is a war of survival, and it is prepared to fight with all available means until the very end."

As one analyst noted: "This point cannot be stressed enough: when faced with a choice between conceding to U.S. demands or escalating a confrontation it believes it can manage and even win, Iran's decision is not difficult to predict. It will not capitulate."

**The blockade's limited effectiveness:**

The US has now seized two ships while dozens more evade interdiction. This explains the expansion to global waters — but also highlights the challenge of enforcing a blockade against a sophisticated sanctions-evasion network.

**The energy crisis backdrop:**

IEA chief Fatih Birol called this "the biggest crisis in history," noting that it compounds "the effects of the petrol crisis and the gas crisis with Russia." "Even if the Strait of Hormuz opens tomorrow, returning to the pre-war period will require significant time, a recovery process, and large volumes of capital," Birol warned.
