What specifically moved the odds since the last update

Hormuz looks "sticky," not a temporary spike. Vessel traffic has nearly halted at points (tracking data showed one transit on March 12), which is consistent with a multi-week-to-month mine/attack risk problem rather than a short disruption.

The U.S. is still trying to assemble an escort coalition but isn't getting firm commitments, which raises the probability of a prolonged, largely U.S.-carried maritime burden if the policy goal remains "restore flows."

Lebanon has moved deeper into mass displacement (Reuters reporting surpassing ~800,000 displaced and ~850 deaths), which increases the risk that the regional theater keeps expanding even if Iran's mainland campaign "winds down."

The IEA's coordinated release (over 400 million barrels) is a strong signal that policymakers/markets expect disruption to persist long enough to require major stabilization measures.

U.S. officials publicly predict the war could end in weeks, but Iran is publicly rejecting negotiation and signaling continued retaliation; that combination tends to produce "weeks become months."
