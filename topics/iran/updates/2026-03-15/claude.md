Days 15-16. The war is entering its third week. Here's where things stand today, March 15.
Key developments since my last update:
1. Iran's FM Araghchi says Tehran "never asked for a ceasefire" — directly contradicting Trump's claims. Araghchi stated Iran is "ready to defend as long as it takes." Iran set conditions for ending the war: guarantees against renewed attacks and compensation for damage. Araghchi also warned Iran would strike US companies' facilities in the region if its energy infrastructure is hit. This is not a regime looking for an off-ramp.
2. The US has struck 6,000+ targets and sunk 65+ Iranian naval vessels in 16 days. 90 military targets on Kharg Island alone in Friday night's raid. The war has cost the US $12 billion so far — roughly $750 million per day. The Pentagon estimated $11.3 billion for the first week alone. This is an unsustainable burn rate without supplemental appropriations.
3. Israel says it has "thousands of targets" remaining and is planning at least three more weeks of campaign. That would take the war into early April. Israel struck a healthcare center in Lebanon killing 12 medical staff. Israeli shells hit a UN peacekeeping headquarters. Lebanon's death toll has climbed to 773. Hezbollah leader Qassem said his group is ready for "a long confrontation." The Lebanon front is fully active and expanding.
4. Energy Secretary Wright said the war will end "in the next few weeks, could be sooner." This is the clearest administration signal yet of a timeline — but it's been contradicted by Israel's three-more-weeks plan and Hegseth's "most intense day of strikes" announcements. The IEA is releasing 412 million barrels from emergency stockpiles — the largest coordinated release in history. Oil remains above $100 with analysts warning of $150 if Hormuz stays closed.
5. Trump called on China, France, Japan, South Korea, and the UK to send warships to Hormuz. No country has committed. Germany is "skeptical" about extending EU naval operations. Trump said he might hit Kharg Island again "just for fun." Iran's ambassador to Saudi Arabia denied responsibility for attacks on Saudi oil infrastructure — a partial walkback from earlier IRGC claims, suggesting some internal debate about the Gulf targeting strategy.
6. Mojtaba Khamenei remains wounded, unseen, communicating through intermediaries. The US offered a $10 million reward for information on him and other top officials. The IRGC says Iran — not the US — "will determine when the war ends." The IRGC also rejected US claims its missile program is destroyed, saying it's deploying projectiles "in greater numbers, with warheads weighing more than 1 tonne."
7. The war's cost to Iran's civilian infrastructure is staggering. 1,444 killed, 18,551 wounded. 3.2 million displaced. Internet blackout now 15 days — connectivity dropped further today, possibly from fiber cuts or power outages from strikes. Isfahan and Tabriz under heavy bombardment today. Cultural heritage sites damaged. WHO reports 13+ healthcare facilities hit. US reportedly used double-tap airstrikes to maximize casualties. The Senate Democrats condemned the Minab school strike; NYT evidence suggests it was a US strike.
8. 13 US service members dead total (7 combat deaths, 6 in the KC-135 crash). 2,200 Marines deploying on amphibious ships. 10,000 AI drones deployed. Three carrier strike groups in theater. B-1 Lancers operating from RAF Fairford in England. The US military footprint is at its largest since 2003 and still growing.
9. Pezeshkian is talking to Macron. France remains one of the few channels between Iran and the West. But Araghchi's "we never asked for a ceasefire" statement undercuts any French mediation effort unless it produces terms Iran finds acceptable — which currently means guarantees and compensation, not unconditional surrender.
10. Netanyahu posted a video of himself drinking coffee to disprove Iranian rumors he was dead. Darkly comic, but illustrative of the information warfare environment. Meanwhile Israel is hitting Hezbollah's financial infrastructure in Lebanon and IRGC targets across Iran simultaneously.

Assessment at the two-week mark:
The war has settled into a pattern that is devastating but not yet decisive. The US and Israel are systematically destroying Iran's military infrastructure, but the regime hasn't collapsed. Iran's retaliatory capacity is >90% degraded but not eliminated — enough to keep Gulf states under fire, Hormuz closed, and oil above $100. Lebanon is a full second front with nearly 800 dead. The global economy is absorbing the largest oil disruption in history.
The most likely near-term trajectory: the next 2-3 weeks see continued intense strikes (Israel's plan), followed by a gradual US pivot toward declaring objectives "substantially achieved." The mechanism for ending the war is not negotiation — Araghchi has shut that door. It's exhaustion and unilateral declaration. Trump declares victory, scales back strikes, attempts to reopen Hormuz with naval escorts. Iran claims survival as victory. Both sides claim they won. The low-level conflict continues indefinitely — periodic strikes, proxy warfare, economic warfare — but the high-intensity phase winds down by early-to-mid April.
The risk that prevents this: Kharg Island's oil infrastructure. Trump threatened it. Iran said attacking it would trigger strikes on all regional oil facilities. If Trump hits Iranian oil, Iran hits Saudi/UAE oil, and the global economy enters recession. That's the escalation ladder that could break the pattern and push this into truly catastrophic territory. Trump's "just for fun" comment about Kharg suggests he doesn't fully appreciate — or doesn't care about — the consequences. That single decision point may matter more than everything else combined.
