**[OFFICIAL]** Trump used familiar talking points in his first address to the nation on the Iran war, telling Americans the war is "nearing completion" and projected another two or three weeks of involvement. However, Trump signaled that the US is prepared to intensify its military response in the remaining time period and threatened to bring Iran "back to the stone ages."

**[VERIFIED]** Trump said if there is no deal with Iran, "we are going to hit each and every one of their electric generating plants very hard, and probably simultaneously." Trump said the U.S. would follow through on that threat in two to three weeks if no deal is struck, adding, "we could hit" their oil.

**[REPORTED]** In addition, after weeks of insisting that thwarting Iran's nuclear capabilities was central to the war, Trump in a Wednesday interview with Reuters said he's not concerned about Iran's stockpile of enriched uranium: "That's so far underground, I don't care about that."

**[REPORTED]** In his Wednesday speech, Trump said he never aimed for regime change but nevertheless declared that he had accomplished it. "We never said regime change, but regime change has occurred because of all of their original leaders' death — they're all dead," Trump said. "The new group is less radical and much more reasonable."

**Assessment:** The speech provided no strategic clarity despite expectations of a major announcement. Bloomberg opinion characterized it as "another 'mission accomplished'" moment, noting Trump sent "all possible messages at once." The contradiction between claiming victory and threatening massive escalation remains unresolved.

**Assessment:** This represents a significant diplomatic realignment — US allies organizing independently to address a crisis caused in part by US policy. The explicit acknowledgment that military action is unrealistic during active conflict confirms Hormuz resolution requires a ceasefire and Iranian cooperation.

### Iran's "Tehran Toll Booth" Institutionalizing

**[VERIFIED]** Before the war, 110 ships were passing through every day. Today, something like five to 10 ships are passing through every day, and those ships are no longer going unmolested through the middle of the strait. They are being forced into Iranian waters between a series of islands. This has been nicknamed the Tehran toll booth. The Iranians are inspecting these ships as they come by. They are charging some of them $2 million to pass through, and every indication is that the Iranians want to make this into a long-term arrangement, even after the war continues.

**[REPORTED]** Iran is forcing oil tankers to take a new route in the Strait of Hormuz through a narrow passage controlled by its Revolutionary Guard, with some ships charged millions of dollars to transit. The new system — dubbed "the Tehran toll booth" by shipping industry experts — indicates Iran remains firmly in control of the critical waterway despite intense strikes on the country, and may be looking to cement that control over the longer term.

**[REPORTED]** Traffic through the Tehran Toll Booth is growing steadily, with at least 20 ships transiting the corridor as of March 23. It accounts for anywhere between 10% and 20% of all traffic through the strait since the start of the war — and the Iranian parliament is now moving to formalize its operations with permanent taxes and fees.

**[REPORTED]** The idea that the U.S. is going to step away and basically leave the Iranians in control of the Strait of Hormuz, in the hope that they are just going to naturally give up this level of control that they have asserted, is going to be very worrying for people here in the Middle East.

**Assessment:** Iran appears to be institutionalizing control over Hormuz rather than treating closure as a temporary wartime measure. Trump's claim that the strait will "open up naturally" after the war contradicts emerging evidence that Iran is building permanent extraction mechanisms.

Less than a half-hour after the president was done, Israel said its military was also working to intercept incoming missiles.

**[VERIFIED]** Sirens sounded in Bahrain, home to the U.S. Navy's 5th Fleet, immediately after the speech.

**Assessment:** Iran's timed missile salvos during and immediately after Trump's address were a deliberate demonstration that Tehran retains offensive capability despite weeks of US-Israeli strikes. The messaging contest between "decisive degradation" (Washington) and "resilient capacity" (Tehran) is intensifying.

More than 30 rockets were launched into the region over a few minutes, marking the second such assault on the area in one hour.

**[VERIFIED]** More than 1,200 people have been killed and more than 1 million displaced in Lebanon. Ten Israeli soldiers have also died there.

**Assessment:** The Lebanon conflict continues independently from any US-Iran diplomatic track. Israel's explicit commitment to permanent occupation up to the Litani creates a separate, prolonged conflict that may continue regardless of any US-Iran resolution.

Trump's speech and Macron's response crystallize a fundamental divergence:

| "Grab" Hormuz, "cherish" it | Military action "unrealistic" |

| Threatens escalation if no deal | Wants "peace as soon as possible" |

In unusually strong criticism, Macron accused Trump of constantly contradicting himself on the Iran war and of undermining faith in the NATO military alliance.

**Assessment:** The US is prosecuting a war while expecting allies to clean up the consequences, but refuses to participate in the cleanup planning. This creates a two-track reality: US-Israel military operations continue while European-led diplomatic/security planning proceeds separately.

### Trump's April 6 Deadline: 4 Days Away

The U.S. has presented Iran with a 15-point plan for a ceasefire, but Trump didn't say anything in his speech about the diplomatic efforts or bring up his April 6 deadline for Iran to reopen the Strait of Hormuz or face severe retaliation.

The April 6 deadline — when Trump threatened to "obliterate" Iran's power plants — is now 4 days away. Notable absences from Wednesday's speech:
- No mention of the April 6 deadline
- No mention of negotiation details
- No mention of ground troops
- No mention of Hormuz reopening plan

**Assessment:** Trump may be walking back the April 6 deadline without explicitly acknowledging it, or he may follow through on infrastructure strikes. The silence on this deadline in a major address is telling.

### The "Natural" Reopening Myth

Trump's claim that Hormuz will reopen "naturally" after the war faces several challenges:

1. **Iran is institutionalizing control** — Parliament drafting permanent toll legislation
2. **The "toll booth" is operational** — Ships paying $2M for passage
3. **IRGC defiance** — "Even if the whole world comes together, they cannot reopen the Strait of Hormuz"
4. **No allied military option** — Macron: "unrealistic" during active conflict
5. **US withdrawal from security role** — Trump: "not America's job"

**Assessment:** Post-war Hormuz normalization will require either major concessions to Iran, a sustained military commitment the US has renounced, or prolonged disruption. "Natural" reopening is increasingly implausible.
