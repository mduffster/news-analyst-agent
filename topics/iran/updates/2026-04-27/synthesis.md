### 1. Iran Proposes Hormuz-for-Blockade Swap — Nuclear Talks Deferred

**[REPORTED]** Araghchi raised the plan to bypass the nuclear issue during his meetings in Islamabad. One source said Araghchi made it clear to the Pakistani, Egyptian, Turkish and Qatari mediators over the weekend that there's no consensus inside the Iranian leadership about how to address the U.S. demands.

The new proposal, given to the U.S. via the Pakistani mediators, focuses on solving the crisis over the strait and the U.S. blockade first. Reports indicated that Iran had submitted a new proposal to the US aimed at reopening the Strait of Hormuz and easing tensions. The proposal, reportedly conveyed via Pakistani mediators, called for extending the ceasefire to allow progress toward a lasting settlement, while postponing nuclear talks until the US blockade of the strait is lifted.

**[OFFICIAL]** The White House received the proposal but signaled skepticism. "These are sensitive diplomatic discussions and the US will not negotiate through the press. As the president has said, the United States holds the cards and will only make a deal that puts the American people first, never allowing Iran to have a nuclear weapon," White House spokesperson Olivia Wales told Axios.

**Assessment:** This represents a significant strategic shift by Iran — essentially offering to separate the immediate shipping crisis from the core nuclear dispute. However, the Iranian proposal would bypass the nuclear issue en route to a faster deal. But lifting the blockade and ending the war would remove President Trump's leverage in any future talks to remove Iran's stockpile of enriched uranium and convince Tehran to suspend enrichment — two primary war objectives for Trump. The proposal is unlikely to succeed in its current form.

### 2. Trump Cancels Pakistan Trip, Dismisses Talks

The meeting comes after the president abruptly canceled plans for his top envoys Steve Witkoff and Jared Kushner to travel to Pakistan for another round of negotiations over the weekend.

**Assessment:** Trump's cancellation and dismissive tone suggests the administration believes its blockade pressure will eventually force Iran to capitulate on nuclear issues. The "call us" posture is negotiating hardball, not genuine disengagement.

### 7. May 1 War Powers Deadline — 4 Days Away

**[VERIFIED]** Trump has until May 1 to obtain congressional approval under the War Powers Resolution. It states that he must limit deployments in any ongoing conflict after 60 days – unless he is granted specific authorisation to continue. To grant this, both the House of Representatives and the Senate must pass a joint resolution – with a simple majority in each – within that 60-day limit. That's not happened so far.

**[VERIFIED]** The Senate has rejected another bid to rein in President Trump's ability to use further military force against Iran, marking Democrats' fifth effort to do so since the war began on February 28. On Wednesday, the Senate voted 46 to 51 to defeat the war powers resolution, with Senator Rand Paul the only Republican voting to advance the measure, and Senator John Fetterman the lone Democrat in opposition.

**[REPORTED]** It's too early to tell what most Republican lawmakers will do if the clock runs out on May 1 and the war against Iran is still ongoing. Some, such as Sen. James Lankford, have argued that it's too far out to speculate, while others, such as Sen. Susan Collins, have indicated that they would join with Democrats to vote in favor of resolutions ordering an end to the fighting. But it would take just a few Republicans in both the House and the Senate crossing the aisle for resolutions ordering an end to the war to pass and be sent to Trump's desk.

**[VERIFIED]** While the deadline comes on May 1, the president may unilaterally certify in writing to Congress that the continued use of force is necessary to ensure the orderly withdrawal of U.S. forces from the region — adding 30 days to the president's power to wage Operation Epic Fury.

**[OFFICIAL]** Vice President JD Vance said in January, before the Iran war, that the War Powers Resolution would not affect how Trump leads the country. "The War Powers Act is fundamentally a fake and unconstitutional law," Vance said. "It's not going to change anything about how we conduct foreign policy over the next couple of weeks, the next couple of months." Despite multiple administrations holding that view, the law has never been used to end a military action and courts have shied away from getting involved.

## Analysis: The Proposal That Went Nowhere

**What happened:**

Iran submitted its most significant diplomatic proposal since the war began — offering to reopen Hormuz and end the war in exchange for lifting the US blockade, with nuclear talks deferred to a later stage. This represents a strategic acknowledgment that Tehran cannot sustain the dual-blockade indefinitely.

**Why it failed:**

The proposal fails from Washington's perspective because it sequences events in the wrong order. Trump wants nuclear concessions before releasing pressure; Iran wants pressure released before discussing nuclear issues. The proposal would:
- Remove US leverage (blockade) before securing core objectives
- Allow Iran to retain enriched uranium indefinitely
- Give Iran time to rebuild and diversify while nuclear talks "proceed later"

**What it reveals:**

Iran's internal divisions are real — Araghchi told mediators there's "no consensus" on how to address US nuclear demands. This proposal may represent the maximum moderates can achieve without hardliner buy-in.

**The Russia factor:**

Araghchi's meeting with Putin serves multiple purposes:
- Demonstrate Iran has great-power backing
- Coordinate on sanctions circumvention
- Explore Russian uranium storage option
- Signal to Washington that isolation strategy isn't working

**Lebanon as pressure release:**

The Lebanon front is deteriorating faster than the Iran front. Sunday's 14 deaths (deadliest since ceasefire) and continued IDF expansion beyond the declared security zone suggest Netanyahu is using the "ceasefire" period to degrade Hezbollah rather than prepare for withdrawal. If Lebanon collapses first, it could trigger Iranian escalation.
