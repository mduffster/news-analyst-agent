## Executive Summary

Day 26 crystallizes the fundamental tension defining this conflict: **the United States is simultaneously deploying ground forces while pursuing diplomatic de-escalation, and Iran is publicly rejecting peace terms while privately signaling openness to talks.**

Three major developments today:

1. **82nd Airborne deployment confirmed**: Between 2,000 and 3,000 U.S. Army paratroopers from the 82nd Airborne Division have received written orders to deploy to the Middle East. The troops are expected to come from the division's Immediate Response Force, which can mobilize worldwide within 18 hours. The deployment, combined with two Marine Expeditionary Units already moving toward the Persian Gulf, could bring 6,000 to 8,000 U.S. ground troops into close proximity to Iran.

2. **Iran publicly rejects US ceasefire plan**: Iran said it rejected a US ceasefire proposal and maintained attacks on Israel and Gulf Arab states, delivering a blow to Washington's efforts to end a war that's wreaked havoc across the Middle East and global markets. "Iran will end the war when it decides to do so and when its own conditions are met," Press TV quoted the official as saying.

3. **Philippines declares first national energy emergency**: The Philippines has become the first country in the world to declare a state of national energy emergency in response to the war in Iran. The Philippines is dependent on oil imports, 98% of which comes from the Middle East. The Philippines had enough gasoline to last a little more than 53 days, diesel for nearly 46 days, jet fuel for 38 days and liquefied petroleum gas for 23 1/2 days.

**Oil prices dropped sharply** — Brent crude futures declined 5% to $99.30 per barrel — suggesting markets believe talks are real despite public denials. Meanwhile, drones hit a fuel tank and sparked a fire at Kuwait International Airport, demonstrating Iran's continued capacity to strike across the Gulf.

**The March 28 deadline (3 days away)** — when Trump's pause on power plant strikes expires — remains the key inflection point. The diplomatic track is now formalized around a 15-point proposal, but Iran's initial response is flatly negative, setting the stage for critical brinkmanship in the next 72 hours.

## What Changed Since Yesterday (March 24)

### 82nd Airborne Ordered to Deploy — Ground Option Now Live

**[VERIFIED]** The Pentagon on Tuesday ordered a couple thousand paratroopers from the 82nd Airborne Division to deploy to the Middle East, U.S. officials said, as President Donald Trump weighs a significant escalation in the U.S.-Israeli war with Iran.

The contingent includes Maj. Gen. Brandon Tegtmeier, commander of the 82nd Airborne Division, and division staff, as well as a battalion of the 1st Brigade Combat Team which is currently acting as the division's Immediate Response Force (IRF).

Citing Fox News correspondent Jennifer Griffin, Maj. Gen. Brandon Tegtmeier and his headquarters staff are deploying to establish forward command and control for potential joint forcible-entry missions, including strikes on strategic targets such as Iran's Kharg Island oil export hub.

The Trump administration has ordered the 82nd Airborne Division to deploy about 2,000 soldiers to the Middle East, a person familiar with the matter said, as the White House weighs options to ease Iran's chokehold on the Strait of Hormuz. They add to the two Marine Expeditionary Units — with about 5,000 troops — that should start arriving in the region in the coming days.

**Assessment:** This is the most significant escalation signal since the war began. The 82nd Airborne specializes in rapid airborne assaults and seizing critical terrain. Combined with two MEUs, the US is positioning 6,000-8,000 ground troops within striking distance of Iran — a capability set designed for forcible-entry operations like seizing Kharg Island or establishing beachheads near Hormuz. This fundamentally changes Trump's options even as he pursues diplomacy.

### US 15-Point Ceasefire Plan Delivered — Iran Publicly Rejects

**[VERIFIED]** The 15-point plan now in Iranian hands is "a comprehensive deal" to reach a ceasefire, according to the Egyptian official. Mediators are pushing for possible in-person talks between the Iranians and the Americans, perhaps as soon as Friday in Pakistan.

Two officials from Pakistan described the 15-point U.S. proposal broadly, saying it addressed sanctions relief, a rollback of Iran's nuclear program, limits on missiles and reopening the Strait of Hormuz, through which a fifth of the world's oil is shipped. An Egyptian official involved in the mediation efforts said the proposal also includes restrictions on Iran's support for armed groups.

Iran will not accept a ceasefire offer from the United States, Iranian state media reported Wednesday. The FARS News Agency state media outlet, citing what it called an informed source, said there have been increased U.S. efforts to put a ceasefire into effect and start indirect talks with Iran. "Iran does not accept a ceasefire," that source told FARS.

A move by US President Donald Trump to start indirect talks is illogical and not viable at this stage of the conflict, Iran's semi-official Fars news agency reported. Iran is focused on achieving its objectives and only if those are met will an end to the war — not a ceasefire — be possible.

**Iran's counter-demands** reportedly include: reparations, guarantees against future attacks, and "The war must end completely and permanently, and guarantees must be given that this situation will not happen again. The damages inflicted on Iran must also be compensated."

**Assessment:** The shift from vague mediation feelers to a structured 15-point proposal represents meaningful diplomatic progress. However, Iran's initial response through state media is flatly negative. Key question: Is this public posturing while engagement occurs privately, or genuine rejection? The reported conditions — reparations, no-attack guarantees, de facto Hormuz control — are maximalist positions that suggest Iran is bargaining from what it perceives as strength, not seeking a quick off-ramp.

### Possible In-Person Talks as Early as Friday

**[REPORTED]** Mediators are pushing for possible in-person talks between the Iranians and the Americans, perhaps as soon as Friday in Pakistan, the Egyptian and Pakistani officials said.

It is understood that Pakistan, Egypt and Turkiye have been pressing for a peace meeting between the US and Iran in Pakistan's capital Islamabad, to be held by Thursday.

All three reports note that Iran has reportedly indicated it prefers to engage with Vice President Vance over envoy Steve Witkoff or Jared Kushner — a meaningful signal that private channels may exist even as public denials continue.

### Kuwait Airport Struck Again — Iran Maintains Pressure

**[VERIFIED]** Kuwaiti Civil Aviation Authority said Iranian drones struck a fuel depot at Kuwait International Airport igniting a fire at the facility. Official spokesperson Abdullah Al Rajhi told Kuwait News Agency (KUNA) that initial reports indicate the damage is limited to material losses, with no casualties reported.

Iran's Islamic Revolutionary Guards Corps said in a statement carried by state broadcaster IRIB that they had launched missiles and drones at military bases hosting US forces in Kuwait, Jordan and Bahrain as well as targets in Israel.

The Kuwait National Guard said in a statement that its forces intercepted six drones. The Kuwait airport is largely closed to commercial flights and has come under attack many times since the regional war began on February 28.

**Assessment:** This is the fourth confirmed attack on Kuwait airport infrastructure. Iran is demonstrating sustained ability to strike across the Gulf even as diplomatic talks proceed — maintaining pressure while negotiating.

### Philippines First to Declare National Energy Emergency

**[VERIFIED]** Philippine President Ferdinand Marcos Jr has declared a national energy emergency in response to the US-Israel war on Iran ⁠and what he called the "imminent danger" posed to the country's energy supply.

The Philippines imports 98% of its oil from the Gulf, and the price of diesel and petrol has more than doubled in the country since the war broke out on 28 February.

The Philippines said grounding planes due to a shortage of jet fuel brought on by the war in Iran is a "distinct possibility," according to President Ferdinand Marcos Jr. "Several countries have already told our airlines they cannot fuel their aircraft, so they have to carry fuel there and back," Marcos said.

Energy research firm Wood Mackenzie predicted if the war continues, Brent oil prices could climb as high as $150 a barrel in the coming months, and warned an average price of $125 a barrel this year would trigger a global recession. "With a geopolitical stalemate, a war drifting on and inventory outside the Gulf dwindling, prices across the entire crude and product complex will push up."

**Assessment:** The Philippines is the canary in the coal mine. Other net energy importers across Asia face similar vulnerabilities. This emergency declaration previews the global economic pressure that will mount if the conflict extends beyond a few more weeks.

### Oil Prices Drop Sharply on Diplomacy Hopes

**[VERIFIED]** At 8:30 a.m. Eastern Time today, the price of oil sits at $99.75 per barrel, using Brent as the benchmark. That's a decrease of $2.72 since yesterday morning—but $26.64 more than at this time last year.

Goldman Sachs noted that near-term price movements are being driven less by changes in the base case outlook and more by shifts in the perceived probability of worst-case scenarios. Crude is effectively trading on a geopolitical risk premium as investors hedge against prolonged disruptions and critically low inventories.

The bank's base case assumes flows through the Strait of Hormuz to normalize in April over a four-week period.

### Israel-Lebanon: Ground Operations Continue

**[VERIFIED]** Cross-border clashes have rapidly escalated into a campaign that now appears aimed at reshaping control of territory along Israel's northern border. The fighting intensified earlier this month after Hezbollah launched missiles into northern Israel following U.S.-Israel strikes tied to the broader Iran conflict. Israeli forces responded with sustained airstrikes and ground operations across southern Lebanon, moving beyond short incursions and pushing deeper toward the Litani River.

U.N. spokesperson Stéphane Dujarric told reporters in New York that the rhetoric was "very much concerning," as the humanitarian toll continues to climb, with more than 1 million people displaced and more than 1,070 people killed in Lebanon since the escalation intensified.

In a joint statement, Canada, France, Germany, Italy, and the UK said such an operation should be avoided. Syria was pressured to militarily intervene in Lebanon by the United States by sending its troops and striking positions in Lebanon alongside the IDF in order to further weaken Hezbollah, however the Syrian government refused.

**Assessment:** Israel's Lebanon campaign continues independently of US-Iran diplomatic developments. Western allies are expressing "grave concern" but taking no concrete action to restrain Israel. The occupation of southern Lebanon to the Litani appears to be proceeding regardless of ceasefire discussions.

### Gulf States Consider Joining War — With High Threshold

**[REPORTED]** Gulf states such as Saudi Arabia and the United Arab Emirates are considering joining the war against Tehran, according to several people with knowledge of the situation. They would only do so if the Islamic Republic attacks vital power and water infrastructure — a high threshold.

Turkey, meanwhile, is conducting intense diplomacy to try and prevent Gulf Arab countries from becoming involved.

## Critical Assessments

### The Paradox of Parallel Tracks

Today's developments reveal a fundamental tension in US strategy: Trump is simultaneously deploying thousands of ground troops while claiming imminent diplomatic breakthrough. This is either:

1. **Deliberate coercive diplomacy** — using force posture to pressure Iran into concessions
2. **Hedging against failure** — preparing ground options in case diplomacy collapses by March 28
3. **Internal administration conflict** — different factions pursuing different strategies

The deployment of the 82nd Airborne division commander suggests preparation for potential Kharg Island seizure or Hormuz operations. This is not routine force protection — it's offensive capability positioning.

### Iran's Rejection: Posturing or Position?

Iran's public rejection of the 15-point plan through state media must be weighed against:
- Private signals through intermediaries suggesting willingness to engage
- Preference for Vance over Witkoff/Kushner (indicating engagement on negotiator selection)
- Continued Hormuz selective transit for "friendly" nations (maintaining bargaining leverage rather than maximum escalation)

The most likely assessment: Iran is publicly rejecting while privately probing for better terms. Tehran wants: reparations, security guarantees, formalized Hormuz control. The US proposal reportedly offers: sanctions relief, nuclear rollback, Hormuz reopening. The gap is substantial but potentially bridgeable — especially under time pressure.

### The Asia Energy Crisis Is Real

The Philippines emergency declaration is not overreaction. Some nations including China have restricted overseas shipments of fuel and other materials to safeguard their own domestic inventory. South Korea is considering limiting exports of naphtha, a petroleum by-product used in plastics manufacturing. A naphtha shortfall is also prompting production cuts at petrochemical companies in Japan.

While President Trump is touting optimism the war may end soon, analysts have said even if a ceasefire came into effect, there will be months, if not years of economic pain ahead. The conflict, now in its fourth week, has strained the commodity stockpiles that nations usually keep in reserve.

This creates external pressure on all parties: the longer the war continues, the more countries will face Philippines-level crises.
