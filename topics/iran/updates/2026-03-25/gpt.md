### What changed since the last report (new since Mar 24)

1) **A concrete U.S. ceasefire/exit proposal is now on the table (15 points), transmitted via Pakistan — and Iran publicly rejects it (with a counter-list)**
- AP reports a **15‑point U.S. proposal** was delivered to Iran through **Pakistani intermediaries**, with Pakistani and Egyptian officials describing core elements: **sanctions relief**, **rollback of Iran’s nuclear program**, **limits on missiles**, **reopening the Strait of Hormuz**, and **restrictions on Iran’s support for armed groups**. **[REPORTED]** ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/be07c54139bcc70672bb33f0773ede6a?utm_source=openai))  
- Iran, via **Press TV citing an anonymous official**, said it **rejected** the U.S. proposal and put forward its own **five-point** stance including **reparations**, assurances against future war, and continued assertion of “sovereignty” over Hormuz. **[REPORTED]** ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/be07c54139bcc70672bb33f0773ede6a?utm_source=openai))

**What it means:** This is a meaningful step-change from “mediation feelers” to a **structured term sheet**. But the initial Iranian response (via state media, anonymously) is **flatly negative**, suggesting the “pause” track is not (yet) producing real de-escalation. The proposal’s inclusion of missiles/proxies remains a likely deal-breaker (Iran has historically treated both as core deterrence), so absent fast reframing into a narrower interim package, odds rise for **continued fighting while talks churn** rather than a quick ceasefire. **[REPORTED]** ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/be07c54139bcc70672bb33f0773ede6a?utm_source=openai))

2) **Potential in-person talks are being floated with near-term timing (Pakistan/Islamabad), but it’s still not locked**
- AP reports mediators are pushing for possible **in-person U.S.–Iran talks “as soon as Friday” in Pakistan**, per Egyptian and Pakistani officials. **[REPORTED]** ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/be07c54139bcc70672bb33f0773ede6a?utm_source=openai))  
- Axios similarly reports the U.S. push for in-person talks **as soon as Thursday in Islamabad**, while noting Iranian suspicion the diplomacy could be a ruse amid U.S. force movements. **[REPORTED]** ([axios.com](https://www.axios.com/2026/03/25/iran-war-trump-peace-talks-tehran-suspicious?utm_source=openai))

**What it means:** Compared with yesterday’s “facilitation offer,” we now have a **credible claim of an imminent meeting window**. The risk is that the timeline is being used as **leverage signaling** (on both sides) rather than indicating readiness to compromise. Still, even a failed convening attempt can matter if it establishes a durable channel for narrower deconfliction (e.g., Hormuz procedures).

3) **U.S. escalates force posture: airborne + Marines en route, expanding “options” even as diplomacy is attempted**
- AP: the U.S. military is preparing to deploy **at least 1,000 troops from the 82nd Airborne** to the Middle East “in the coming days,” and is also sending **~5,000 more Marines** plus additional sailors. **[REPORTED]** ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/be07c54139bcc70672bb33f0773ede6a?utm_source=openai))

**What it means:** This materially **raises the credibility of coercive threats** and hedges against escalation in multiple theaters (base defense, contingency seizures, evacuations, raids). It also **validates Tehran’s suspicion** that “talks” are being used to buy time for deployments—potentially hardening Iran’s stance and reducing odds of quick acceptance of U.S. terms. Net: diplomacy and military posture are now moving **in parallel**, not sequentially.

4) **Iranian attacks broaden Gulf pressure: drone strike causes major fire at Kuwait International Airport fuel tank**
- AP reports an Iranian assault **hit a fuel tank at Kuwait International Airport**, sparking a **huge fire**; Kuwait said it shot down multiple drones. **[REPORTED]** ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/be07c54139bcc70672bb33f0773ede6a?utm_source=openai))

**What it means:** This is a sharp reminder that Gulf states remain **active battle space**, not just collateral risk. Even if casualties are limited, airport fuel infrastructure is politically salient and can intensify Gulf pressure on Washington for stronger defensive/offensive action—pushing the system toward **regional entanglement**.
