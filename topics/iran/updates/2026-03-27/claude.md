## Key Developments Since Yesterday

### Trump Extends Deadline to April 6

**[OFFICIAL - White House]** As the war enters day 28, United States President Donald Trump has delayed planned attacks on Iran's energy infrastructure by 10 days until April 6, saying peace talks are going "very well" – even as Iranian officials describe a US proposal as "one-sided and unfair".

Trump's initial five-day pause on strikes against Iranian energy infrastructure was supposed to expire Saturday, and a dramatic military escalation will grow more likely if no progress is made in diplomatic talks, particularly if the Strait of Hormuz remains closed. The Pentagon is developing military options for a "final blow" in Iran that could include ground forces and a massive bombing campaign.

**Assessment:** The extension signals the administration is seeking an off-ramp but has not found one yet. The new deadline creates another decision point; the pattern of deadline extensions may continue if talks show any progress.

### Vance-Netanyahu Tensions Surface

**[REPORTED - Axios]** Vance has already had multiple calls with Israeli Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu, met Gulf allies about the war and been involved in indirect communications with the Iranians. He's expected to be the top U.S. negotiator in potential peace talks. Vance was highly skeptical of Israel's rosy prewar assessment of how the war would unfold, and currently expects the war to continue for another few weeks.

"Before the war, Bibi really sold it to the president as being easy, as regime change being a lot likelier than it was. And the VP was clear-eyed about some of those statements," the U.S. source said.

Vance advisers think some in Israel are trying to undermine the VP, possibly because they find him insufficiently hawkish. Israeli officials deny that.

**Assessment:** This is a significant new development revealing intra-coalition tensions. Vance's skepticism about regime change suggests the U.S. may push for a negotiated outcome rather than continued escalation. This could create friction with Israel, which appears to prefer continued military pressure.

### Israel Strikes Iranian Steel Factories, Vows Escalation

**[VERIFIED - Israel/Iran]** Israel says it will "escalate" its strikes on Iran in response to waves of Iranian missile fire. Fresh strikes have been carried out across the region, with Iran reporting overnight attacks in multiple cities.

The strikes hit Khuzestan Steel near Ahvaz and Mobarakeh Steel in Isfahan. The strikes on the factories, which are partially owned by the IRGC, are expected to cause major damage to the Iranian economy.

"In strikes carried out across Tehran, the IDF targeted infrastructure and sites used by the regime to produce weapons, with an emphasis on ballistic missile production facilities. In western Iran, the Air Force, guided by Military Intelligence, struck the Iranian terror regime's fire arrays throughout the night. Among the targets struck were launchers and missile storage sites."

**Assessment:** Israel is expanding targeting from purely military to economic/industrial targets. This represents a shift designed to impose costs on the Iranian economy and may complicate negotiations by raising the stakes.

### IDF Chief Warns of Military "Collapse"

**[VERIFIED - Israeli Media]** The broadened military activity has intensified pressure on IDF manpower. An IDF spokesperson acknowledged Thursday that the military has a shortage of about 15,000 soldiers. The shortfall comes amid an ongoing conscription crisis.

Zamir raised "10 red flags," noting that reserve units are in their sixth and seventh mobilization cycles, regular forces are in "complete collapse," and the army lacks sufficient personnel to sustain operations. He warned that urgent legislative action, including a new Draft Law, Reserve Service Law, and extended mandatory service, is needed to prevent the IDF from being unable to continue missions.

Former PM Bennett said: "The IDF is 20,000 soldiers short. There are 100,000 young Haredim of military age in good health today, and if they only recruited a fifth of them, there would be no problem. But the government puts politics above everything."

**Assessment:** This is a critical constraint on Israeli operations. The multi-front war (Iran, Lebanon, West Bank) is stretching the IDF beyond its capacity. This may force Israel to seek an earlier end to the conflict than its maximalist statements suggest.

### Oil Prices Continue Elevated

**[VERIFIED]** At 9 a.m. Eastern Time today, oil was priced at $107.81 per barrel with Brent serving as the benchmark. This is up slightly from yesterday's $105.85.

Oil prices edged up slightly early Friday after US President Donald Trump said he would hold off strikes against Iranian energy sites, citing ongoing talks with the country. Brent crude futures, the global oil benchmark, rose 1% to $109.1 a barrel at 3:20 am ET on Friday. WTI, the US benchmark, was up 0.4% to $94.9 a barrel.

The Iran conflict has added an estimated $15-20 per barrel risk premium to crude since hostilities began. Before the conflict, Brent was trading near $85-88.

**Assessment:** Markets are pricing in continued uncertainty. The deadline extension provided modest support, but traders remain skeptical of rapid resolution.

### G7 Meeting: Rubio Seeks Allied Support

**[VERIFIED]** Representatives from G7 countries gather in France, where U.S. Secretary of State Marco Rubio is expected to try to shore up support from reticent allies for the Trump administration's war on Iran.

Rubio's Europe trip also comes a day after Trump again slammed NATO for not coming to the aid of the U.S. European countries have said they were not informed of Trump's Iran plans beforehand and have no interest in entering what could be a protracted war. Germany's defense minister was blunt in his assessment this week, saying Washington had "no exit strategy."

Rubio has said countries should "step up" to help deal with the blockade of the Strait of Hormuz. "Very little of our energy comes through the Strait of Hormuz. It's the world that has a great interest in that, so they should step up and deal with it," he told reporters.

**Assessment:** The G7 is deeply divided. European allies are focused on de-escalation rather than military support. The lack of allied participation in Hormuz operations creates additional pressure on U.S. resources.
