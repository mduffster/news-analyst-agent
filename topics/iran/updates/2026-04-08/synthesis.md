## Executive Summary

**A fragile two-week ceasefire has halted direct US-Israeli strikes on Iran, but Lebanon has become the war's new flashpoint as Israel conducts its largest coordinated attack of the conflict just hours after the truce announcement.**

Five key developments since April 7:

1. **[OFFICIAL] Two-week ceasefire announced at 6:32 PM ET on April 7.** The U.S. and Iran reached a ceasefire deal on Tuesday, less than two hours before the deadline President Trump imposed for Iran to meet his demands or else face wide-scale destruction. As part of the agreement, Trump said the U.S. and Israel would suspend bombing Iran for two weeks, subject to Iran following through on its commitment to reopen the Strait of Hormuz for safe passage during the ceasefire period.

2. **[VERIFIED] Israel launches "biggest strike" on Lebanon — ceasefire explicitly excluded.** Israel's military called it the largest coordinated strike in the current war, striking more than 100 Hezbollah targets within 10 minutes in Beirut, southern Lebanon and the eastern Bekaa valley. Several hundred people were reported killed and wounded in and around Beirut on Wednesday, according to the Lebanese Red Cross.

3. **[OFFICIAL] Iran threatens to withdraw from ceasefire over Lebanon strikes.** Iran will withdraw from its ceasefire with the United States if Israel continues attacking Lebanon, the country's Tasnim news agency reported. According to Tasnim, Iran's armed forces began identifying targets to respond to Israel's strikes on Lebanon.

4. **[VERIFIED] Islamabad talks set for Friday, April 10 — first direct US-Iran negotiations.** The United States delegation will be headed by Vice President James David Vance. The Islamabad talks represent the first in-person negotiations between the two adversaries since the war began. The Iranian delegation will be led by Speaker of Parliament Mohammad Bagher Ghalibaf.

5. **[VERIFIED] Oil crashes 16%, markets surge on ceasefire relief.** Benchmark U.S. crude sank $18.43 to $94.52 a barrel, a nearly 16% decline. Brent crude, the international standard dropped $15.54 to $93.73 a barrel.

## What Changed Since April 7

### Ceasefire Agreement — A Dramatic Reversal

**[OFFICIAL]** The negotiations — facilitated by Pakistan — mark a breathtaking comedown from Trump's pledge made early Tuesday that a "whole civilization will die tonight, never to be brought back again" if a deal could not be reached by 8 p.m. ET to open the strait.

**[OFFICIAL]** In announcing the deal, Trump wrote on his social media platform: "This will be a double sided CEASEFIRE!" Pakistan's prime minister Sharif, who has been acting as an intermediary between Tehran and Washington, said the ceasefire will take effect immediately, and includes "Lebanon and elsewhere."

**[OFFICIAL]** Iranian leaders are also touting the ceasefire as a victory, noting that the "criminal U.S." has agreed to "the general framework" of Iran's 10-point proposal. "Good news to the dear nation of Iran! Nearly all the objectives of the war have been achieved," the Supreme National Security Council said in a statement.

**[OFFICIAL]** Iran officials warned that any deviation from the agreement could lead to future violence. "Our hands are on the trigger, and the moment the enemy makes the slightest mistake, it will be met with full force," the Supreme National Security Council said.

**Assessment:** Both sides claim victory in the ceasefire. Trump claims US military objectives achieved; Iran claims its 10-point framework accepted. The mutual victory claims suggest both sides found face-saving elements in the deal, but the Lebanon carve-out creates immediate instability.

### Lebanon Crisis — The Ceasefire's Critical Weakness

**[VERIFIED]** Israeli strikes hit several dense commercial and residential areas in central Beirut Wednesday afternoon without warning, hours after a ceasefire was announced in the U.S.-Israeli war with Iran. Israel has said the agreement does not extend to its war with the Iran-backed militant group Hezbollah in Lebanon.

**[OFFICIAL]** Netanyahu said the two-week ceasefire "does not include Lebanon." Pakistan, France and Egypt, which helped mediate the deal, all have asserted Lebanon's inclusion.

**[REPORTED]** Israel has rarely struck central Beirut since the outbreak of the latest Israel-Hezbollah war on March 2 but has regularly struck southern and eastern Lebanon and Beirut's southern suburbs. Before the wave of new strikes, a Hezbollah official told The Associated Press that the group was giving a chance for mediators to secure a ceasefire in Lebanon, but "we have not announced our adherence to the ceasefire since the Israelis are not adhering to it."

**[REPORTED]** Israeli media, including Channel 12, reported that the operation was intended to assassinate Hezbollah Secretary-General Naim Qassem.

**[VERIFIED]** Israeli airstrikes have killed more than 1,530 people in Lebanon, including more than 100 women and 130 children. The Israeli military has said it has killed hundreds of Hezbollah fighters. More than one million people have been displaced in Lebanon.

**Assessment:** The Lebanon exclusion is the ceasefire's most dangerous vulnerability. Israel appears to be using the pause in Iran operations to intensify attacks on Hezbollah. Iran's explicit threat to withdraw from the ceasefire over Lebanon strikes creates a direct pathway to resumption of hostilities.

### Iran's Threat to Withdraw — Critical Escalation Risk

**[REPORTED]** Iran will withdraw from its cease-fire with the United States if Israel continues attacking Lebanon, the country's Tasnim news agency reported. According to Tasnim, Iran's armed forces began identifying targets to respond to Israel's strikes on Lebanon.

**[REPORTED]** Hezbollah-affiliated MP Ibrahim Al-Moussawi said that "there has been no official announcement from Hezbollah regarding a ceasefire." "If the Israeli enemy does not adhere to a ceasefire, then no party will commit to it, and there will be a response from the region, including Iran."

**[REPORTED]** "The greatest threat to any ceasefire in the region remains Israel," King's College's Krieg warned, adding that Israel prefers "ambiguous ceasefire" deals that allow it to return to fighting "when it feels the situation favours the Israeli army".

**Assessment:** Iran has now formally conditioned its ceasefire compliance on Israel's behavior in Lebanon. This creates a direct link between the Israel-Hezbollah front and the US-Iran truce. If Israel continues major operations in Lebanon, Iran may resume attacks on Gulf states and Israel within days.

### Strait of Hormuz — Slow Reopening

**[VERIFIED]** At least two vessels have safely transited through the Strait of Hormuz since the ceasefire was announced between Iran, the United States and Israel, according to the latest data from MarineTraffic. That is a drop in the ocean of the massive backlog of vessels trapped in the Persian Gulf.

**[VERIFIED]** MarineTraffic said on Wednesday that hundreds of vessels remain in the region, including 426 tankers, 34 liquefied petroleum gas carriers and 19 liquefied natural gas vessels. On Wednesday at 8:44 a.m. UTC, a Greek-owned bulk carrier, called NJ Earth, crossed the strait. About two hours before that, the Liberia-flagged bulk carrier, the Daytona Beach, made it through to the Gulf of Oman. Both vessels appeared to have taken the route that passes by Iran's Larak Island, which Lloyd's List Intelligence has previously reported is a checkpoint used by the Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps to control access to the strait.

**[VERIFIED]** The Strait of Hormuz appeared to remain largely blocked on Wednesday, as shipowners try to understand if they can safely transit the vital waterway following a ceasefire. A total of seven ships were seen exiting the region since Tuesday morning, while three vessels entered, tracking data compiled by Bloomberg show. Total peace time transits stood at about 135 a day last year. More than 800 freighters are stuck inside the gulf, mostly waiting to leave.

**[REPORTED]** Shipowners are rushing to understand the fine print of a US-Iran ceasefire that could temporarily unblock the Strait of Hormuz and, at least in theory, provide an exit window for more than 800 vessels trapped in the Persian Gulf.

**Assessment:** Hormuz reopening is proceeding slowly. Iran is controlling passage through IRGC checkpoints. The gap between 7-10 daily transits now versus 135 pre-war shows the blockade remains substantially in place even under ceasefire terms. Insurance and uncertainty are keeping most vessels in place.

### Post-Ceasefire Attacks — Fog of War

**[VERIFIED]** Israel said that it had also halted attacks against Iran, but that it would continue its air and ground assault on Lebanon, contradicting mediator Pakistan. And Gulf states including Bahrain, the United Arab Emirates and Kuwait reported new Iranian attacks this morning after blasts were reported at oil sites in Iran.

**[REPORTED]** The missile and drone interceptions over the United Arab Emirates and Kuwait appear to be in response to the attacks that have hit Iranian oil fields. The drone attacks "took place hours after the targeting of oil facilities on Lavan Island in Iran," Iranian state broadcaster IRIB reported.

**[REPORTED]** Throughout the war, Iran's paramilitary Revolutionary Guard has called the shots in all decisions. Individual commanders have made decisions on what to strike and when, with the nation's political leadership sidelined. Whether they agreed to stop shooting with the declared ceasefire and negotiations being planned in Islamabad remained in question. However, many Mideast wars see combatants launch last-minute attacks to be able to claim victory with their populations.

**Assessment:** The mutual accusations of violations create pretext risk. Iran claims Lavan Island was struck first (justifying Gulf retaliation); the US/Gulf claims Iranian attacks continued. This fog could undermine the truce if either side decides violations are intentional rather than communication lags.

### Islamabad Talks — Historic Diplomatic Opening

**[OFFICIAL]** Sharif extended an invitation to "their delegations to Islamabad on Friday, 10th April 2026, to further negotiate for a conclusive agreement to settle all disputes". According to Iran's National Security Council, its 10-point proposal calls for Iranian dominance and oversight of the Strait of Hormuz. The proposal also calls for the withdrawal of all "US combat forces" from bases in the Middle East and a halt to military operations against allied armed groups across the region. It goes on to demand "full compensation" for war damages, as well as the lifting of all sanctions. The proposal also calls for the release of frozen Iranian assets abroad.

**[OFFICIAL]** Trump said the US has already "met and exceeded" all of its military objectives and "are very far along with a definitive Agreement concerning Longterm PEACE with Iran". He said the US has received a 10-point proposal from Iran, "and believe it is a workable basis on which to negotiate". The US and Iran have agreed on "almost all of the various points of contention," he said.

**[REPORTED]** Despite the ceasefire and the diplomatic engagement, deep mistrust remains on both sides. Iranian officials have been reported to enter talks with "complete distrust" toward the American side.

**[OFFICIAL]** The United States will work closely with Iran, which has undergone "regime change," and will be talking about tariff and sanctions relief with Tehran, US President Donald Trump says. "The United States will, working with Iran, dig up and remove all of the deeply buried… Nuclear Dust," he says in a Truth Social post. "There will be no enrichment of uranium," Trump adds.

**Assessment:** Trump's claims of near-agreement vs. Iran's maximalist 10-point demands suggest either genuine progress or mutual face-saving. Key gaps remain: Iran demands Hormuz oversight and US troop withdrawal; Trump demands no uranium enrichment. The Friday talks will test whether any real convergence exists.

### Market Reaction — Relief Rally

**[VERIFIED]** The US-Israel war on Iran saw the steepest monthly oil price rise in history, 50 percent in March. Oil prices have dived, bonds rallied, and stocks surged as a two-week ceasefire involving Iran spurred a relief rally, with investors cheering the possible resumption of oil and gas flows through the Strait of Hormuz.

**[VERIFIED]** In Asia, Japan's benchmark Nikkei 225 gained 5.4%. Australia's S&P/ASX 200 jumped 2.6%. South Korea's Kospi soared 6.9%. Hong Kong's Hang Seng surged 3.1%, while the Shanghai Composite added 2.7%.

**[REPORTED]** "Yet the mood remains one of cautious optimism rather than outright celebration," said Tim Waterer, chief market analyst at KCM Trade. "The ceasefire is only two weeks long, and markets will be watching closely to see whether shipping through the Strait of Hormuz normalizes as promised and whether the fragile truce can pave the way for a more durable peace agreement."

### The Lebanon Question — Ceasefire's Achilles Heel

**[CRITICAL]** The fundamental contradiction in the ceasefire:

- **Pakistan's position:** Ceasefire includes "Lebanon and elsewhere"
- **Iran's position:** Lebanon must be included; will withdraw from truce if Israel continues

**Risk Assessment:** This contradiction creates a 24-72 hour window where the entire ceasefire could unravel. If Iran determines Israel is acting in bad faith on Lebanon, it may resume Gulf attacks or provide Hezbollah with authorization to escalate.

## Bottom Line

**Net assessment: Ceasefire holds for US-Iran direct conflict but Lebanon escalation threatens collapse within days.**

The last-minute ceasefire represents a significant de-escalation from Trump's "civilization will die" threat and Iran's maximalist position. Markets have responded with relief. Diplomatic talks are scheduled. Both sides claim victory.

**However, three critical risks threaten the truce:**

1. **Lebanon**: Israel's "largest strike of the war" hours into the ceasefire, combined with Netanyahu's explicit exclusion of Lebanon, creates a direct pathway to resumption. Iran has now explicitly threatened to withdraw from the ceasefire over Lebanon strikes.

2. **Implementation gaps**: Only 7-10 ships transiting Hormuz daily vs. 135 pre-war. Iran retains effective control through IRGC checkpoints. The strait is "open" in rhetoric but blocked in practice.

3. **Unbridgeable negotiating gaps**: Trump demands "no enrichment"; Iran demands Hormuz oversight and US troop withdrawal. These positions cannot be reconciled in two weeks.

**The next 48-72 hours are critical.** If Israel continues major Lebanon operations and Iran follows through on its withdrawal threat, the ceasefire collapses. If both sides exercise restraint and find face-saving formulas at Islamabad, a longer-term de-escalation becomes possible.

**The ceasefire clock expires April 21-22. The Lebanon crisis could make that deadline irrelevant.**
