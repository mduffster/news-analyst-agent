## Daily incremental update — US–Israel–Iran conflict  
**As of Wed, April 8, 2026 (ET)**  
**Window scanned:** new reporting/statements **since Tue, April 7, 2026 (ET)**

### What changed since the last report

1) **An 11th-hour, Pakistan-brokered “2‑week ceasefire” framework was announced — averting the Apr 7 (8pm ET) infrastructure-strike threat.**  
- President Trump publicly said the U.S. would **suspend bombing/attacks for two weeks**, **conditional on** Iran agreeing to the “complete, immediate, and safe opening” of the Strait of Hormuz; reporting attributes the proposal to **Pakistan**. **[REPORTED]** ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/eddbcc14e06a6dcb5c7cc41021120fa8?utm_source=openai))  
- AP frames this as Trump backing away from the threatened “power plants/bridges” strike package shortly before the deadline. **[REPORTED]** ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/eddbcc14e06a6dcb5c7cc41021120fa8?utm_source=openai))  

**What it means:** This is a **material de-escalation from yesterday’s “strike vs extend” fork**—but it’s better described as a **time-limited freeze + Hormuz deliverable** than a durable settlement. The immediate “civilian infrastructure” escalation risk is down for now, but the *next* coercive cliff is now **“does Hormuz actually reopen and stay open during the two weeks?”**

2) **Ceasefire terms appear politically/operationally messy; reports indicate “some attacks continue.”**  
- AP reports that **after the announcement**, **Iran and Gulf Arab countries reported new attacks Wednesday**, and that it was unclear whether those strikes could scuttle the deal. **[REPORTED]** ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/38d75d5e4f1c7339a1456fc99415bb2a?utm_source=openai))  

**What it means:** The highest near-term failure mode is **ambiguity + ongoing fire** (misattribution, proxies, “not covered” theaters, or timing gaps) that triggers retaliation and collapses the pause. Treat the ceasefire as **fragile and implementation-dependent**, not as a resolution.

3) **UN track: Russia/China vetoed the UNSC Hormuz resolution (contrary/confusing early writeups), highlighting major-power friction.**  
- Multiple outlets report **Russia and China vetoed** the (already watered-down) Security Council draft on reopening/protecting shipping in Hormuz. **[REPORTED]** ([aljazeera.com](https://www.aljazeera.com/news/2026/4/7/russia-and-china-block-un-resolution-on-strait-of-hormuz?utm_source=openai))  

**What it means:** Even with a ceasefire announcement, the UNSC outcome signals **no unified great-power mandate** for enforcement in Hormuz. That makes “internationally backed” coercive reopening less likely and increases reliance on **ad hoc regional/naval arrangements** and bilateral bargaining—conditions that historically produce **more misunderstanding and escalation risk**, not less.

4) **Israel indicates the “Iran ceasefire” may not apply to other fronts (notably Lebanon), and strikes there continue.**  
- AP reports Israel struck **central Beirut** and said the Iran ceasefire “doesn’t apply” there. **[REPORTED]** ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/9402965418687c634d4a157c966ec6ea?utm_source=openai))  

**What it means:** This keeps open a key escalation channel: **multi-theater decoupling** (U.S.–Iran pause while Israel–Hezbollah continues) can still trigger spillover, including pressure on Iran to respond via proxies even if it’s trying to hold the Hormuz/bilateral track together.

### Bottom line (incremental)
- The system **moved away from immediate U.S. infrastructure strikes** and toward a **two-week coercive pause** centered on **Hormuz reopening**. **[REPORTED]** ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/758eb5cd680d7d275c4e1c38b2e01e6d?utm_source=openai))  
- The pause is **highly fragile**: continued attacks and “ceasefire doesn’t apply” carve-outs make **spoilers and miscalculation** the dominant risk in the next 24–72 hours. **[REPORTED]** ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/38d75d5e4f1c7339a1456fc99415bb2a?utm_source=openai))  
- Russia/China’s UNSC veto underscores that **global governance won’t reliably stabilize Hormuz**; practical stabilization will depend on **on-the-water behavior** and whether the parties can operationalize safe passage quickly. **[REPORTED]** ([un.org](https://www.un.org/en/middle-east-live-7-april-conflict-continues-humanitarian-and-economic-pressures-mount?utm_source=openai))
