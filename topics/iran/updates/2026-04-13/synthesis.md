## What Changed Since April 10

### Islamabad Talks: 21 Hours, No Deal

**[OFFICIAL]** President Donald Trump on Sunday said the U.S. will blockade the Strait of Hormuz after talks held in Pakistan to end the Iran war hit the skids. Peace talks hosted by Pakistan broke down over what the U.S. described as Iran's unwillingness to give up its efforts to obtain a nuclear weapon.

**[OFFICIAL]** Tehran's demands include control of the Strait of Hormuz, payment of war reparations and a ceasefire across the region, including in Lebanon, according to Iranian state TV and officials. It is also seeking the release of its frozen assets abroad. The U.S. delegation, led by Vice President JD Vance, met with Iranian and Pakistani negotiators for more than 21 hours during the rare face-to-face summit.

**[REPORTED]** Iranian Foreign Minister Abbas Araghchi said Iran had "encountered maximalism, shifting goalposts, and blockade" when just inches away from an "Islamabad MoU."

**[OFFICIAL]** The status of the two-week ceasefire is now uncertain. But Vance left open the possibility that an agreement could still be reached, saying: "We leave here with a very simple proposal: a method of understanding that is our final and best offer."

**Assessment:** The talks represented the highest-level direct US-Iran engagement since 1979. Both sides are framing the collapse differently—the US blames Iran's refusal on nuclear issues; Iran blames US "maximalism." The door is not fully closed, but neither side appears ready to move from its core demands.

### US Naval Blockade: Now In Effect

**[OFFICIAL]** The blockade will be enforced impartially against vessels of all nations entering or departing Iranian ports and coastal areas, including all Iranian ports on the Arabian Gulf and Gulf of Oman. CENTCOM forces will not impede freedom of navigation for vessels transiting the Strait of Hormuz to and from non-Iranian ports.

**[OFFICIAL]** Iran warned it would retaliate if the U.S. carries out the blockade of its ports, saying it would be illegal and, essentially, be piracy. Tehran said no Gulf ports will be safe if traffic to and from its own are impeded.

**[VERIFIED]** The USS Michael Murphy turned on its automatic identification system as it and another destroyer, the USS Frank E. Peterson, transited the strait, breaking the typical protocol of Navy ships sailing with their AIS turned off. "You just don't throw AIS on by accident on a Navy ship," Campbell University professor Salvatore Mercogliano said. "This is purposeful. They wanted to turn this on on the far side of the Strait of Hormuz to demonstrate that they have sailed through." U.S. Central Command said the destroyers had begun setting conditions for clearing mines.

**[VERIFIED]** Lloyd's List says shipping, which had been moving at reduced levels through the Strait of Hormuz, "immediately halted," with some vessels turning back, after the US president announced plans for a blockade.

**[REPORTED]** According to the Wall Street Journal, more than 60% of the IRGC's Hormuz naval fleet is still intact after six weeks of war. The IRGC mans the strait with a fleet of small speedboats and attack craft equipped to guard the key waterway with missiles and mines. Those boats are harder to target than traditional battleships, as they often evade satellite detection due to their size.

**Assessment:** The US blockade is narrower than Trump's initial rhetoric suggested—targeting Iranian ports specifically rather than all Hormuz traffic. However, Iran's threat that "no port in the Gulf will be safe" represents a significant escalation risk. The IRGC-Navy confrontations show tensions are already high.

### UK-France Separate Coalition Effort

**[OFFICIAL]** UK says it will not be involved in U.S. blockade of the Hormuz Strait, and is currently "urgently working with France and other partners to put together a broad coalition to protect the freedom of navigation."

**[OFFICIAL]** France and the UK plan to organize a conference in the coming days with countries willing to contribute to a "peaceful, multinational mission" aimed at restoring free transit through the Strait of Hormuz, French President Emmanuel Macron said. "This mission, which is strictly defensive and separate from the warring parties, is set to be deployed once the situation allows."

**[VERIFIED]** A senior NATO military official told CBS News, "The U.K. is leading planning efforts of a coalition comprised of more than 40 nations from around the world, many of which are from NATO, to reopen the Strait of Hormuz and protect freedom of navigation."

**Assessment:** A two-track approach is emerging: the US pursuing aggressive blockade/enforcement, while UK-France pursue a separate "defensive" multinational mission. This creates coordination challenges and potential for competing strategies.

### Israel-Lebanon: Talks Tomorrow, Fighting Today

**[OFFICIAL]** Israel will begin official peace talks with Lebanon next week, according to a statement from Leiter. "In the conversation earlier today in Washington between the Israeli and Lebanese ambassadors to Washington, together with the US ambassador to Lebanon, and under the auspices of the US State Department, Israel agreed to begin formal peace negotiations this coming Tuesday." "Israel refused to discuss a ceasefire with the Hezbollah terrorist organisation."

**[REPORTED]** Lebanese lawmaker Hassan Fadlallah, who is affiliated with Hezbollah, said on Saturday that the group rejects direct negotiations between Lebanon and Israel.

**[VERIFIED]** "Over the past week, the forces completed the encirclement and launched an offensive in the town of Bint Jbeil," the IDF said in a statement on Monday. "The troops eliminated more than 100 terrorists from the Hezbollah terror organization in close-quarters combat and from the air, destroyed dozens of terrorist infrastructures, and located hundreds of weapons in the area."

**[VERIFIED]** Israeli Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu said during a Cabinet meeting Monday that the military was expanding beyond the five hilltops it controlled in southern Lebanon since the ceasefire with Hezbollah in 2024, toward a "solid, deeper security zone." He said it was in order to protect northern Israel.

**[VERIFIED]** At least 2,055 people have been killed in Israeli strikes in Lebanon, the Health Ministry said, among them 252 women, 165 children, and 87 medical workers, while 6,588 were wounded.

**Assessment:** Israel and Lebanon have fundamentally different expectations. Israel wants to negotiate "peace" while continuing military operations; Lebanon wants a ceasefire as a precondition. The IDF is actively expanding its ground presence while simultaneously agreeing to talks.

Brent crude, the international benchmark, hit $101.88 per barrel, while U.S. crude reached $104.69.

**[VERIFIED]** Saudi Arabia said it has restored full pumping capacity through its East-West pipeline to the Red Sea, along with output from the Manifa field.

**[REPORTED]** Equity markets in Asia declined Monday morning, after peace talks between the US and Iran failed to remove the stranglehold Iran's military has on the Strait of Hormuz. Instead, President Donald Trump threatened his own blockade.

**[VERIFIED]** The Asian Development Bank predicted that growth in the Asia Pacific region would slow to 5.1% in 2026 and 2027, due to the conflict in the Middle East and trade uncertainty. "A prolonged conflict in the Middle East is the single biggest risk to the region's outlook, as it could lead to persistently high energy and food prices and tighter financial conditions."

**Assessment:** The collapse of talks and blockade announcement have reversed the modest price relief seen last week. Brent jumping ~7% in one day signals extreme market volatility. Saudi pipeline restoration is positive but does not offset the broader supply disruption.

## Emerging Issue: Maritime Escalation Ladder

The conflict's primary axis has shifted from air strikes to naval confrontation. A new escalation ladder is emerging:

| Level | US Action | Iranian Counter | Current Status |
|-------|-----------|-----------------|----------------|
| 1 | Blockade of Iranian ports announced | Piracy accusations; threats | ✅ Happening now |
| 2 | US warships transit Hormuz | Radio warnings; drone launches | ✅ Already occurred |
| 3 | Interdiction of ships that paid Iran tolls | Retaliation against Gulf ports | ⚠️ Threatened |
| 4 | Mine-clearing operations | IRGC attacks on minesweepers | ⚠️ US preparing |
| 5 | Naval engagement in strait | Full Hormuz closure + regional escalation | ❓ High risk |

This has already put about 20,000 lives in potential danger, which risks turning the strait into a floating prison. The two-week ceasefire was slowly paving the way for the evacuation of about 20,000 sailors aboard the roughly 2,000 ships that remain stranded in the Persian Gulf.

## Bottom Line

**The diplomatic window has closed. The conflict has entered a new phase of naval confrontation and economic warfare.**

The Islamabad collapse represents a critical turning point. The most consequential dynamics are now:

- **US naval blockade vs. IRGC response**: The next 24-48 hours will reveal whether the blockade triggers direct military confrontation.
- **Iran's "all Gulf ports" threat**: If Iran retaliates against Gulf neighbors, the conflict expands dramatically.
- **Oil prices**: Already back above $100; further escalation could push prices toward $110-120.
- **Lebanon talks Tuesday**: A secondary but important track—any Israel-Lebanon de-escalation would be significant.

The formal ceasefire is technically intact, but the blockade, IRGC warnings, and failed talks have rendered it largely meaningless as a framework for peace. The question is no longer whether the ceasefire will lead to agreement—it almost certainly won't—but whether the remaining days until April 22 see direct military confrontation.

**Ceasefire collapse probability: ~65% (up from 45% on April 10)**

The shift from "ceasefire holding" to "active blockade" represents a fundamental change in the conflict's character, even without new direct strikes on Iranian territory.
