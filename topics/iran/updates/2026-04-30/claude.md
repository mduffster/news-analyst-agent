#### Real War Cost May Be $40-50 Billion — Nearly Double Official Estimate
**[REPORTED]** The cost of the Iran war to the United States may be significantly higher than official Pentagon figures, with internal estimates suggesting it could reach $40–50 billion, according to CNN. Individuals familiar with the matter said the higher estimate factors in reconstruction of bases and replacement of destroyed military assets following Iranian strikes across several Persian Gulf states in the early stages of the war. The report said US facilities in Bahrain, Kuwait, Iraq, the United Arab Emirates and Qatar were among those damaged. Pentagon officials told Congress the $25 billion figure largely reflects munitions spending, while acknowledging that a full accounting of overseas damage has not yet been completed.

**Assessment:** The official $25 billion figure presented to Congress appears to significantly undercount actual costs by potentially 60-100%. This has implications for future supplemental funding requests and political debates about the war's cost.

#### Central Banks Freeze Amid Economic Uncertainty
**[VERIFIED]** The Bank of England has held interest rates at 3.75% as the war in Iran and Tehran's effective closure of the Strait of Hormuz send oil prices to their highest level in four years. The decision on Thursday was widely expected, following the US Federal Reserve's move the day before to leave rates unchanged for the third consecutive meeting and a similar hold by Japan's central bank on Tuesday.

**[VERIFIED]** The European Central Bank kept interest rates on hold at its April meeting, despite a surge in inflation in the euro zone since the war in Iran began. The ECB's governing council opted to hold its benchmark deposit facility rate at 2% on Thursday. In a statement, the bank said that while its previous assessment of the inflation outlook was largely unchanged, "the upside risks to inflation and the downside risks to growth have intensified."

**[VERIFIED]** The European Central Bank has kept its main interest rate unchanged at 2%, but warned that inflation could rise further and economic growth turn out weaker because of the effects of the Iran war. "The longer the war continues and the longer energy prices remain high, the stronger is the likely impact on broader inflation and the economy," the ECB said in a statement Thursday. Data published earlier Thursday showed that inflation across the 21 countries that use the euro climbed to 3% in April, from 2.6% in March.

**[REPORTED]** In an unusual development, the Bank of England published a range of forecasts given the geopolitical uncertainties. It said that in a worst-case scenario where oil and gas prices stay higher for a longer amount of time, U.K. inflation could rise to as much as 6.2% from 3.3% currently.

**[OFFICIAL]** President Christine Lagarde signaled the European Central Bank will consider a possible interest-rate hike in June after policymakers debated and rejected the idea on Thursday. "We made an informed decision on the basis of yet insufficient information," Lagarde said.

**Assessment:** Global central banks are in a "wait and see" holding pattern. The ECB's warning that inflation could be "stronger" the longer the war continues is notable. The BoE's 6.2% worst-case inflation scenario underscores economic risks. June rate hikes are now being discussed.

#### Pakistan Opens Land Routes to Iran
**[REPORTED]** While much of the world's attention has been on the vital Strait of Hormuz, Pakistan has opened up six land transit routes for goods destined for Iran. The Ministry of Commerce issued the Transit of Goods through Territory of Pakistan Order 2026 on April 25, bringing it into immediate effect, formalizing a road corridor through its territory to clear cargo backlogs. The order allows goods originating from third countries to be transported through Pakistan and delivered to Iran by road. Some 3,000 Iranian shipping containers have been sitting Karachi and Gwador port since the conflict began two months ago.

**Assessment:** This is a significant development that partially circumvents the US naval blockade. While land routes cannot replace sea transport for oil exports, they provide Iran a critical lifeline for imports, reducing blockade pressure.

**Assessment:** This ISW analysis suggests any revised Iranian proposal will be cosmetic rather than substantive. Iran appears to be using the ceasefire to rebuild military capabilities while maintaining red lines on nuclear program and Hormuz control.
