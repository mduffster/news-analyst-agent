## What Changed Since April 13

### Israel-Lebanon Talks: Historic Moment, Uncertain Outcome

**[OFFICIAL]** The negotiations are scheduled to take place on Tuesday at the US Department of State headquarters in Washington, DC. The meeting is scheduled to begin at 11am Eastern Time (15:00 GMT).

**[OFFICIAL]** The two sides are deeply divided on goals. Lebanon wants Israel to agree to a ceasefire, during which diplomats could begin negotiations to reach a comprehensive peace agreement. Israeli Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu has rejected calls for a ceasefire and has insisted that Israel will continue its efforts to destroy Hezbollah forces in Lebanon.

**[REPORTED]** Their most immediate challenge will be addressing conflicting preferences about ongoing Israeli military operations. This is focused on sorting out the concept of "ceasefire," which Lebanon wants and Israel rejects. Lebanese leaders shed no tears for Hezbollah or Amal fighters killed by Israel, but they want a ceasefire in order to validate their call for direct talks and ease the massive humanitarian challenge posed by the million-plus citizens who have been displaced.

**[OFFICIAL]** Hezbollah has fiercely condemned the negotiations, with Secretary-General Naim Qassem calling them a "free concession" to Israel and the US. The group's objections are rooted in several factors: Negotiating under fire: Hezbollah views negotiating while Lebanon is being bombarded as akin to signing a document of surrender. "You cannot conduct negotiations to stop the fighting if you are under fire and under pressure," Ali al-Miqdad, a Hezbollah member of the Lebanese parliament, told Al Jazeera.

**[REPORTED]** Deep political divisions inside Lebanon are adding volatility to the diplomatic push, with factions split over whether to engage in talks with Israel as fighting continues along the border. Hezbollah-aligned groups and their political allies have publicly opposed negotiations that sideline the militant group, warning of potential security consequences. The pressure has already had tangible effects. Lebanon's prime minister recently canceled a planned trip to Washington amid security concerns tied to the talks.

**Assessment:** The talks represent a historic milestone—the first direct Israeli-Lebanese engagement since 1993. However, fundamental gaps remain: Israel wants Hezbollah disarmament before ceasefire; Lebanon wants ceasefire as a precondition for talks. With Hezbollah explicitly rejecting any agreement, the Lebanese government's ability to implement any deal is questionable. The talks are more likely to produce a framework for future negotiations than any immediate breakthrough.

### US-Iran: Door Opens for Second Round

**[OFFICIAL]** The US blockade of Iran's ports is in effect, but US President Donald Trump says Iranian officials have called and "want to work a deal."

**[REPORTED]** After marathon talks over the weekend failed to secure an agreement, officials are reportedly looking at a second round of negotiations to end the U.S. and Israeli war against Iran. President Donald Trump and his Administration are open to resuming in-person talks as soon as he believes Iran is prepared to meet his demands, sources told CNN. That could mean a second meeting with Iranian officials before the two-week cease-fire expires on April 21 or potentially extending the cease-fire.

**[REPORTED]** Pakistan has proposed hosting a second in-person meeting in Islamabad, Pakistani officials told the Associated Press. Turkey, which was among several intermediaries involved in weeks of negotiations, is also stepping in to try and resolve the differences between the U.S. and Iran, a regional source told CNN.

**[OFFICIAL]** There appears to be an element of rebranding underway, with officials now increasingly referring to the "Islamabad Talks" as the "Islamabad Process," a shift that suggests an effort to frame the engagement as an ongoing diplomatic track rather than a one-off meeting. The top-level source also said Monday that Pakistan is in active contact with both Washington and Tehran, urging them to resume dialogue at the earliest opportunity. The primary objective is to reach a workable understanding before the current ceasefire — set to expire around April 22 — ends in order to prevent a return to all-out-war.

**[VERIFIED]** The United States made a lot of progress in talks with Iran, U.S. Vice President JD Vance said in an interview on Fox News. Vance, asked whether more talks were coming, said the ball was in Iran's court.

**Assessment:** Despite the blockade and tough rhetoric, significant diplomatic activity continues. The rebranding to "Islamabad Process" suggests Pakistan is building institutional framework for ongoing negotiations. Combined with Trump's claims about Iranian outreach and intermediary efforts from Turkey and Qatar, the path to a second round before April 21 appears viable—though not guaranteed.

### US Blockade: Day 2—Limited Enforcement

**[VERIFIED]** US President Donald Trump said his country's military has begun blockading Iranian ports in an effort to force Tehran to open the strategic Strait of Hormuz and accept Washington's terms to end the war. "We can't let a country blackmail or extort the world because that's what they're doing," Trump said of Iran at the White House.

**[VERIFIED]** The Malawi-flagged Chinese-owned tanker Rich Starry passed through the Strait of Hormuz on Tuesday, according to MarineTraffic data. The ship first attempted to pass through the strait around noon UTC on Monday but briefly turned back near Iran's Qeshm Island. It made a new attempt in the evening and appears to have passed the strait by early Tuesday UTC. Rich Starry is owned by Full Star Shipping Ltd, linked to Shanghai Xuanrun Shipping Co Ltd, and has been sanctioned by the US since 2023 due to its Iran ties.

**[VERIFIED]** Another U.S.-sanctioned ship, Elpis, passed through the Strait of Hormuz yesterday, according to MarineTraffic data. The Elpis, like the previously reported Rich Starry, is also sanctioned under the Treasury Department's 2018 package on dealings with Iran.

**[OFFICIAL]** China's Ministry of Foreign Affairs spokesperson Lin Jian called the U.S. blockade of the Strait of Hormuz "dangerous and irresponsible," adding that it will only escalate tensions between the U.S. and Iran. "We urge relevant parties to honor the ceasefire agreement, stick to the direction of peace talks and take concrete actions to de-escalate the situation."

**[REPORTED]** At least three tankers have entered the Gulf via the Strait of Hormuz on Tuesday, shipping data from LSEG and Kpler shows. The Panama-flagged Peace Gulf is heading to Hamriyah port in the UAE, after transiting the new route between the islands of Larak and Hormuz in Iranian territorial waters that the IRGC has ordered all vessels to take.

**Assessment:** The blockade is operational but enforcement appears limited. Sanctioned Chinese vessels are transiting without US interdiction, and some non-Iranian traffic is resuming via IRGC-approved routes. This suggests either deliberate US restraint or the practical difficulty of comprehensive enforcement. The lack of major confrontation in the first 24+ hours is a positive signal.

### Bint Jbeil: IDF Nears Full Control

**[VERIFIED]** The Israeli military has nearly completed the capture of the Hezbollah stronghold of Bint Jbeil in southern Lebanon, killing more than 100 members of the terror group in the area in the process, the Israel Defense Forces said Monday.

**[VERIFIED]** Bint Jbeil is considered a symbol of Hezbollah power and the IDF has been encircling the town in recent weeks as it seeks to extend its control of southern Lebanon. The IDF said it initially isolated and encircled the town to prevent Hezbollah from bringing in reinforcements or escaping. At least 150 Hezbollah operatives, including members of the terror group's elite Radwan Force, were estimated by the IDF to have been in the Bint Jbeil area ahead of the operation.

**[VERIFIED]** A military official said "Full operational control of Bint Jbeil will be achieved within days."

**[VERIFIED]** The stadium, which over the years has become a symbol of Hezbollah's victory over Israel after the IDF's withdrawal from southern Lebanon, was destroyed over the past few days during the battles. The "Spider Web" address, in which Nasrallah claimed that Israel was weaker than a spider web despite its nuclear weapons, shaped Hezbollah's worldview for a generation.

**[REPORTED]** The National News Agency and The Times of Israel reported that the IDF was still trying to "take control of the remaining neighborhoods in the city of Bint Jbeil." The NNA stated that the IDF was allegedly sustaining "significant losses" from Hezbollah. Israeli military sources admitted that combat conditions were harsh due to the terrain.

**Assessment:** The IDF's capture of Bint Jbeil—site of Nasrallah's famous 2000 "spider web" speech—represents a significant symbolic and military victory. The operation demonstrates Israel's intent to create a "solid, deeper security zone" in southern Lebanon regardless of diplomatic developments in Washington.

### Oil Markets and Economic Impact

**[VERIFIED]** Brent crude oil prices have remained volatile in the last one week, reacting to various developments around the US-Iran talks. Oil slipped nearly 3% to $96.48 per barrel today on fresh signs of US-Iran dialogue despite rising tensions and a naval blockade.

**[VERIFIED]** Oil prices today: Brent $97.89, WTI $104.37/bbl. Updated 4/14/26, 12:52 PM GMT.

**[VERIFIED]** Trump's statement that Iran "wants to make a deal" kept brent crude rates below $100 per barrel. Morgan Stanley expects brent crude at $110 a barrel in the second quarter of 2026 and $100 a barrel in the third quarter. The rates could finally fall to $80 a barrel in 2027. The bank said oil supply chains could take months to normalise even if a reopening of the Strait of Hormuz can be achieved.

**[OFFICIAL]** The International Monetary Fund cut its growth outlook on Tuesday due to Iran war-driven energy price spikes and supply disruptions and warned that the global economy would teeter on the brink of recession if the conflict worsens and oil stays above $100 per barrel through 2027. The World Economic Outlook's most optimistic "reference scenario" assumes a short-lived Iran war and forecasts 3.1% real GDP growth for 2026, down 0.2 percentage point from its previous forecast in January. Under this scenario, oil prices average $82 per barrel for all of 2026.

**[OFFICIAL]** War in the Middle East has "halted" economic momentum, IMF chief economist Pierre-Olivier Gourinchas wrote. "The closing of the Strait of Hormuz and serious damage to critical facilities in a region central to global hydrocarbon supply raise the prospect of a major energy crisis should hostilities continue."

**[OFFICIAL]** The situation remains very uncertain, and shipping through the Strait of Hormuz is yet to normalize. Even after a resumption of regular shipping flows through the Strait, it will take time for global supplies of key commodities to move back towards their pre-conflict levels—and fuel and fertilizer prices may remain high for a prolonged period given the damage to infrastructure.

**[REPORTED]** Iran's initial estimate of the damages from US-Israel strikes puts the approximate cost at $270 billion, Iranian government spokesperson Fatemeh Mohajerani was cited as saying, adding that the figure is not yet final.

**Assessment:** Markets are responding positively to diplomatic signals, with oil retreating ~5% from yesterday's highs. However, the IMF's recession warning and forecasts of prolonged supply disruption underscore the fragility of the current calm. A return to fighting would quickly push prices back above $100.

Neither Iran nor Gulf countries reported any strikes overnight, though Israel continued its attacks in Lebanon.

The combination of diplomatic back-channels, Trump's conciliatory statements, and muted blockade enforcement suggests both sides want to avoid immediate escalation while exploring negotiating options.

## Emerging Analysis: Nuclear Issue as Core Deadlock

The reports consistently identify nuclear enrichment as the central unresolved issue:

**[REPORTED]** The US asked for a 20-year enrichment moratorium and removal of highly enriched uranium; Iran countered with a shorter term and down-blending inside Iran. (Axios reporting)

**[VERIFIED]** The talks lasted 21 hours between 11 and 12 April 2026, and consisted of three rounds. The teams were reportedly able to agree on the main points of the 10-points ceasefire, with the exception of the issues regarding nuclear program and the Strait of Hormuz.

This suggests a potential deal structure exists on most issues, but the "enrichment red line" remains the single-point-of-failure. If neither side softens on timeline/verification, the blockade becomes a standing coercive regime rather than temporary pressure.
