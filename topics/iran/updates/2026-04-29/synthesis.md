## What Changed Since Yesterday (April 28)

### 1. Pentagon Confirms $25 Billion War Cost — First Official Estimate

**[VERIFIED]** The United States' war in Iran has cost $25 billion so far, a senior Pentagon official said on Wednesday, providing the first official estimate of the military's price tag for the conflict. Jules Hurst, who is performing the duties of the comptroller, told lawmakers on the House Armed Services Committee that most of that money was for munitions. Hurst did not detail what that cost estimate included and whether it took into account the projected costs of rebuilding and repairing base infrastructure in the Middle East damaged in the conflict.

**[VERIFIED]** The military has also spent money on running the operations and replacing equipment.

**Assessment:** This is the first official war cost disclosure, providing significant ammunition for Democrats linking the conflict to domestic affordability. At $25 billion over 60 days, the war is averaging over $400 million per day — though the ceasefire period likely reduced the daily burn rate from the initial $1.88 billion/day pace.

### 2. Hegseth Testifies Before Congress — First Time Since War Began

**[VERIFIED]** Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth is on Capitol Hill Wednesday for what is formally billed as a routine hearing on the Pentagon's budget request. But the appearance — the first before Congress for Hegseth since the war in Iran began in February — lands just two days before a 60-day deadline to wind down hostilities.

**[VERIFIED]** Questions about the war in Iran quickly dominated Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth's appearance before the House Armed Services Committee on Wednesday. Democratic lawmakers grilled Hegseth — along with Gen. Dan Caine, chairman of the Joint Chiefs of Staff — about why President Donald Trump decided to strike Iran two months ago, the administration's shifting reasons for the war, and the costs to Americans at home and abroad. Rep. John Garamendi (D-California) lobbed a direct criticism of Hegseth's leadership, saying that "the purposes and the strategic direction of this war" are in question.

**[OFFICIAL]** Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth said congressional Democrats are "the biggest adversary we face" as he parried questioning from lawmakers.

**[VERIFIED]** At the center of this week's hearings is the administration's request for $1.5 trillion in defense spending, the largest amount in the Pentagon's history and a jump of 50% over current levels, which would mark the largest single-year increase in a generation. The proposal would triple spending on drones and related technologies to more than $74 billion, while directing over $30 billion toward munitions procurement. But that budget request was developed months ago: not account for spending in the war with Iran.

**[REPORTED]** Sen. Thom Tillis, a Republican, said: "Tell us why. You know these are important positions. We are in a war posture with Iran." Tillis, who was a crucial vote to confirming the defense secretary, added that Hegseth's management of the Pentagon had caused him to have second thoughts on his support. "He may be able to clean it up, but on its face, you don't go through the number of highly reputable, senior-level officials, admirals and generals," Tillis added.

**Assessment:** This hearing represents the first real accountability moment since the war began. Hegseth's combative posture — calling Democrats the "biggest adversary" — and Republican Tillis's open regret over his confirmation vote signal growing bipartisan unease. The $25 billion cost disclosure and $1.5 trillion budget request that doesn't even include Iran war funding provide significant political ammunition.

### 4. Oil Surges Past $115 — Eighth Consecutive Day of Gains

**[VERIFIED]** Brent rose to 116.53 USD/Bbl on April 29, 2026, up 4.74% from the previous day. Over the past month, Brent's price has risen 8.52%, and is up 90.85% compared to the same time last year.

**[VERIFIED]** Brent crude traded at $115.29 per barrel on Wednesday, April 29, 2026, up 3.62% on the session and rising for the seventh consecutive day, the longest winning streak of the year and the highest closing level for the European benchmark since June 2022. The June 2026 contract has now gained more than 88% over the past 12 months, lifted by the de facto closure of the Strait of Hormuz, the ninth week of US-Iran conflict, and Tuesday's surprise announcement that the United Arab Emirates will leave OPEC and OPEC+ on May 1.

**[VERIFIED]** The shutdown of the strait, which normally handles about 20% of global oil trade, has triggered what the International Energy Agency described as the largest supply shock on record. Meanwhile, Washington is stepping up pressure with potential sanctions targeting Chinese refiners and countries paying transit fees through Hormuz.

**[REPORTED]** Goldman Sachs upgraded its Q4 2026 Brent forecast to $90 from $80 on April 26, the fourth upward revision since the war began. JPMorgan continues to flag a $150 overshoot if Hormuz disruption extends.

**[VERIFIED]** Crude Oil (WTI) rose to 104.72 USD/Bbl on April 29, 2026, up 4.79% from the previous day. Over the past month, Crude Oil's price has risen 1.79%, and is up 79.90% compared to the same time last year.

**Assessment:** Oil has jumped from $111 to $115-117 in 24 hours — a ~5% single-day surge reflecting Trump's escalating rhetoric, preparation for extended blockade, and the continued Hormuz closure. The eighth consecutive day of gains is the longest winning streak of the year. Goldman's fourth upward revision and JPMorgan's $150 scenario underscore persistent underestimation of disruption duration.

### 5. Israel Kills Emergency Workers in Lebanon — PM Condemns "War Crimes"

**[VERIFIED]** Lebanon's prime minister has accused Israel of perpetrating a "heinous crime" in the targeting and killing of civil defence emergency workers, three of whom were among five people killed in a double Israeli strike in southern Lebanon. Two successive Israeli strikes on a building in the town of Majdal Zoun on Tuesday killed five people, including three rescue workers who went to help those injured in the initial Israeli attack on the targeted building, Lebanon's Ministry of Health said.

**[VERIFIED]** The ministry later added that another two people were killed and 13 injured in an Israeli strike in the town of Jebchit in southern Lebanon, while one person was killed and 15 were hurt, including five children, in another strike.

**[VERIFIED]** The Lebanese army said that two of its troops were also wounded in the second Israeli strike that targeted its forces, the rescue workers and two civilian bulldozers. According to media reports, Israeli forces attacked a Lebanese military patrol, which was escorting the civil defence workers on the rescue mission at the site of the initial Israeli attack.

**[VERIFIED]** Meanwhile, the Israeli military said "an employee working for an engineering company carrying out projects on behalf of the Ministry of Defence was killed" in southern Lebanon on Tuesday.

**[OFFICIAL]** "Targeting elements of the Civil Defence in Majdal Zoun, and their killing while carrying out their humanitarian duty, constitutes a new and described war crime perpetrated by Israel," Prime Minister Nawaf Salam said. "It represents a flagrant violation of the principles and rules of international humanitarian law."

**Assessment:** The pattern of strikes on emergency workers continues to erode the Lebanon ceasefire. The "double-tap" strike — hitting initial responders — has drawn war crimes accusations from Lebanon's PM. The death of an Israeli Defense Ministry contractor to a Hezbollah drone confirms the ceasefire is failing to prevent casualties on both sides. Total Lebanon death toll since March 2 now exceeds 2,500.

### 6. May 1 War Powers Deadline — Now 1 Day Away

**[VERIFIED]** On May 1, a 60-day deadline will be met, at which point Trump must take further action if he wants the war in Iran to continue. Per the law, hostilities must come to a close if Congress doesn't authorize the war.

**[VERIFIED]** Multiple Democrats tell TIME they may take Trump to court if the war in Iran continues past Friday's 60-day deadline. Democrats in Congress are exploring a lawsuit against President Donald Trump if he continues the war in Iran beyond Friday's legal deadline without obtaining congressional authorization. The internal discussions were described by lawmakers as still in the early stages but could intensify in the coming weeks if the President presses ahead with military operations after a critical 60-day deadline expires on May 1.

**[VERIFIED]** Republicans have sent divided messages on how they view the 60-day deadline. At least two Republicans, Senators Thom Tillis and Susan Collins, have suggested they would not vote to approve further US military action following May 1. Senator Lisa Murkowski, a Republican, has said she is working on an authorisation of use of military force (AUMF) on the war.

**[VERIFIED]** Sen. Susan Collins told CNN that after the 60 day deadline laid out in the War Powers Act, the president "has to obtain congressional approval or Congress can block it. Those are the two choices, but there has to be action by Congress." Sen. Mike Rounds said he would be "surprised" if President Donald Trump doesn't ask to extend the deadline for congressional authorization an additional 30 days.

**[VERIFIED]** VP Vance said in January that "The War Powers Act is fundamentally a fake and unconstitutional law. It's not going to change anything about how we conduct foreign policy over the next couple of weeks, the next couple of months." Despite multiple administrations holding that view, the law has never been used to end a military action and courts have shied away from getting involved.

**Assessment:** The May 1 deadline arrives tomorrow with the administration showing no indication it will seek authorization. Democrats exploring litigation is a new development, though courts have historically avoided War Powers disputes. Collins's statement that the president "has to obtain congressional approval" represents the strongest Republican warning yet. The key question: Will Trump invoke the 30-day withdrawal extension or simply ignore the deadline entirely?

### 7. Iran Expected to Submit Revised Proposal — Possibly by Friday

**[REPORTED]** Iran is expected to submit a revised proposal on ending the war to mediators in Pakistan in the coming days. The development comes after Trump indicated he would not accept an earlier version, sources close to the mediation process told CNN.

**[REPORTED]** The proposal is likely to be made over the coming days. Upon returning from his trips to Pakistan, Oman and then Russia, Iranian Foreign Minister Abbas Araghchi is expected to consult with leaders, but the process takes time due to challenges in communicating with Supreme Leader Mojtaba Khamenei, allegedly kept at a secret location. Negotiations are flowing normally but chances of success rely on whether the Islamic Republic returns with a revised offer Washington might be more keen to accept.

**Assessment:** Iran preparing a revised proposal — rather than walking away — suggests Tehran still sees diplomatic options. The timing is notable: a proposal by Friday would arrive the day after the War Powers deadline. However, communication difficulties with the reportedly wounded and hidden Supreme Leader Mojtaba Khamenei continue to complicate Iranian decision-making.
