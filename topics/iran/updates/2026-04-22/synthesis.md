## What Changed Since Yesterday

### Ceasefire Extended But Stripped of Meaning

President Donald Trump on Tuesday extended the two-week U.S. ceasefire with Iran, saying the extension was warranted due to Tehran's government being "seriously fractured." Trump said the ceasefire would continue "until such time as" Iran's leaders and representatives submit a "unified proposal" to end the war with the U.S.

The extension came at Pakistan's request. The move on Tuesday comes at the request of Pakistani mediators, according to Trump. Pakistan's prime minister responded gratefully.

However, the ceasefire extension is effectively hollow because:
- The US naval blockade continues, which Iran considers an act of war
- No talks are scheduled, as Iran refuses to negotiate while the blockade continues
- Trump said Iran wants the Strait of Hormuz reopened but argued that lifting the US blockade of Iranian ports would undermine the prospects of a peace deal. But the blockade is a major sticking point for Iran.

**[REPORTED]** Iran's leadership has been engaged in an intense internal debate in recent days over how to proceed in talks with the Trump administration. Iran's civilian leaders — including Ghalibaf and Foreign Minister Abbas Araghchi — favored continuing talks to extend the ceasefire and reach a deal. But Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps commander Gen. Ahmad Vahidi and his deputies refused to offer concessions and opposed negotiations as long as the naval blockade continues. The U.S. seizure of an Iranian cargo ship in the Arabian Sea deepened the internal rift, with IRGC commanders accusing Iran's negotiators of being weak.

**Assessment:** Trump's extension is a tactical reversal designed to avoid blame if negotiations fail — he can now point to Iran's refusal to negotiate. However, by maintaining the blockade while demanding Iran submit a proposal, he has created conditions Iran cannot accept. The IRGC appears to be winning the internal debate in Tehran.

### IRGC Escalates in Strait of Hormuz

Within hours of the ceasefire extension, the IRGC launched its most significant maritime operation since the war began:

Iran attacked three ships in the Strait of Hormuz this morning, saying its Revolutionary Guard seized two of them and further inflaming tensions over the key waterway.

The incident followed an earlier report of an attack that took place roughly 15 miles northeast of Oman at 5:47 a.m. London time. The UKMTO said a container ship was approached by an Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps (IRGC) gunboat. The IRGC ship was said to have fired upon the ship, causing "heavy damage" to the bridge. All crew were reported safe.

The seized vessels have been identified: Two ships Iran says it seized in the Strait of Hormuz this morning appear to be just off the Iranian coast, according to ship tracking data. But it's a different story for a third ship that semiofficial Iranian agencies say was fired on and now "stranded on Iranian shores." MarineTraffic data shows that the Euphoria passed through the strait yesterday, south of the Iranian island of Larak. The Euphoria's last transmitted position was around 40 nautical miles south of the locations of the Epaminondas and Francesca.

**[VERIFIED]** Iran's foreign ministry is framing these as defensive actions: Iranian Foreign Ministry spokesperson Esmaeil Baghaei said Wednesday that the U.S.-Israeli war was imposed on Tehran and that all subsequent Iranian actions have been "in line with Iran's inherent right to legitimate defense against the military aggression."

**Assessment:** The IRGC's ship seizures demonstrate that:
1. The IRGC, not civilian negotiators, is directing Iran's response
2. Iran views the US blockade as justifying reciprocal maritime action
3. The ceasefire extension has not reduced tensions — it has shifted them to the maritime domain

This creates a dangerous tit-for-tat dynamic: the US seizes Iranian-linked vessels; Iran seizes vessels transiting "its" strait.

### IRGC Threatens Gulf Oil Infrastructure

The IRGC issued an unprecedented public threat to Gulf state oil facilities:

Iran's IRGC Aerospace Force commander warned Gulf Arab states that using enemy facilities to attack Iran could end their oil production.

This threat directly targets Saudi Arabia, UAE, and other Gulf states. The IRGC's threat matters because Iran has the capability to target Gulf oil infrastructure directly. Gulf states produce a large share of global crude supply, and any disruption would ripple through energy markets fast.

**Assessment:** This threat significantly elevates the risk of the conflict expanding beyond US-Iran to involve Gulf states. If Iran follows through, it would represent catastrophic escalation affecting global energy markets for years.

### No Islamabad Talks — Diplomatic Track Stalled

Yesterday's expectation that both delegations would arrive in Islamabad for Wednesday talks has completely collapsed.

Ahead of now-canceled talks in Pakistan, the Trump administration was dealing with virtual silence from the Iranians. The US had sent Iran a list of broad deal points that they wanted the Iranians to agree to in advance of the next round of talks. But days went by without a response, raising suspicions about how much Vice President JD Vance and others could achieve by heading to Pakistan for talks. Trump's top aides believe a main reason they didn't hear back was fractures within the current Iranian leadership. The administration's sense is that the Iranians don't have consensus on their position or how much to empower the negotiators on uranium enrichment.

However, Al Jazeera's correspondent in Tehran disputes the "fractured" characterization: "Iran has a very unified leadership since the assassination of former Supreme Leader Ayatollah Ali Khamenei. Why? Because the new leader, Mojtaba Khamenei, and the circle around him are a team that's been working together for the past 15 years. Members of this team are currently in the centres of decision in Tehran."

Iran's UN envoy has made the path forward clear: "As soon as Washington ends the naval blockade, I think the next round of negotiations will be held in Islamabad," Amir Saeid Iravani, Iran's permanent representative to the UN, told reporters.

**Assessment:** The fundamental impasse is now fully crystallized:
- **US position:** No talks until Iran submits a "unified proposal"; blockade continues
- **Iran position:** No talks until blockade ends; blockade is a ceasefire violation
- **Result:** Complete diplomatic stalemate

### London Summit: 30+ Nations Plan for Hormuz Reopening

Over 30 nations expected to attend the conference at UK's Permanent Joint Headquarters in North London. Military planners will turn diplomatic consensus into a detailed military plan for the Strait when hostilities end. Military planners from over 30 nations will today advance detailed planning to reopen the Strait of Hormuz in a two-day UK-hosted conference. The planning talks will take place at the UK's Permanent Joint Headquarters at Northwood, North London. The work is part of the UK and French leadership of a multinational coalition to reopen the Strait.

UK Defence Minister John Healey stated: "The task, today and tomorrow, is to translate the diplomatic consensus into a joint plan to safeguard freedom of navigation in the Strait and support a lasting ceasefire."

Britain and France emphasized the force would be entirely defensive and only deployed once lasting peace in the region was agreed. The United States and Iran, as warring parties, did not attend the talks.

**Assessment:** This represents significant international coordination independent of the US. However, the mission is contingent on a ceasefire holding — which is increasingly uncertain given today's ship seizures.
