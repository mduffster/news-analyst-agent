## Daily incremental update — US–Israel–Iran conflict  
**As of Wed, March 18, 2026 (ET)**  
**Window scanned:** developments reported since **Tue, March 17, 2026 (ET)**

### What changed since the last report

1) **Iran confirmed Ali Larijani’s death (after Israel claimed it).**  
Iranian authorities confirmed that **Ali Larijani** was killed in an Israeli strike (and that his son was also killed), after a delay of several hours following Israel’s announcement. **[VERIFIED]** ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/be5f46c171b2f9bf1dbd8325261a92a6))  
**Why it matters:** This reduces uncertainty around the “leadership decapitation” track and increases pressure for **symbolic/strategic retaliation** (or accelerated internal consolidation). It also suggests at least some Iranian willingness to confirm high-level losses publicly—important for gauging future “claim vs. confirmation” dynamics.

2) **Iranian state media confirmed the killing of Intelligence Minister Esmail Khatib; Israel claims responsibility.**  
AP reports Iranian state TV confirmed **Esmail Khatib** was killed; Israel’s defense minister said he was killed in an overnight strike. **[VERIFIED]** ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/d7ca062ba1bf99d1f8dc00c8073cf10f))  
**Why it matters:** This is a further escalation of the **targeted-leadership campaign**, and intelligence services are central to internal repression, counterintelligence, and wartime security. Taking out the intelligence minister can disrupt regime security—*but* it can also drive harsher internal crackdowns and risk-taking to reassert deterrence.

3) **Energy infrastructure is now more directly in the crosshairs (gas + regional oil facilities), and prices are reacting.**  
- AP reports an **Iranian offshore natural gas field** tied to **South Pars** was struck (attribution not fully clear in AP’s write-up), alongside intensifying Iranian strikes on Gulf neighbors’ energy facilities and threats to expand them. **[REPORTED]** ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/d7ca062ba1bf99d1f8dc00c8073cf10f))  
- AP also frames this as both sides “ratcheting up” pressure on energy infrastructure, with oil prices rising sharply (AP cites **above $108/barrel** and **>40%** since the start of the war). **[REPORTED]** ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/d7ca062ba1bf99d1f8dc00c8073cf10f))  
**Why it matters:** This increases the risk of (a) **regional spillover** involving Gulf states, (b) **maritime escalation**, and (c) **external pressure** on Washington from price/inflation effects.

4) **Hormuz isn’t “open,” but it isn’t “closed” either—limited transits are occurring via deconfliction/diplomacy.**  
AP cites maritime/trade data: **~89 ships crossed March 1–15**, far below the pre-war baseline; Iran still exported **>16 million barrels** since early March (per Kpler), and some India/Pakistan-linked vessels reportedly transited after talks. **[REPORTED]** ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/82a9acb473837f1bf7a821d0c3f95205))  
**Why it matters:** This supports a “selective corridor” reality: Iran appears to be using Hormuz as **coercive leverage** while allowing constrained flows (possibly for partners/neutral states or via “dark” shipping). That tends to **prolong** the conflict by avoiding the full costs of a total blockade while still imposing economic pain.
