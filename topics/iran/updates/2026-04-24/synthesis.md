## What Changed Since Yesterday

### 1. Israel-Lebanon Ceasefire Extended Three Weeks

**[OFFICIAL]** Israel and Lebanon agreed to extend their ceasefire by three weeks following a meeting in the White House with top U.S. officials, President Donald Trump said.

Israel's military said Friday it struck several Hezbollah sites in southern Lebanon after the Iran-backed militant group fired into Israel, a day after President Trump announced that Israel and Lebanon had agreed to extend their ceasefire by three weeks. The extension was announced at the White House on Thursday, where ambassadors from both countries met for high-level negotiations.

Hezbollah was not involved in the talks. But the ceasefire appeared fragile from the start.

**[VERIFIED]** Hezbollah's latest rocket fire into northern Israel came after an Israeli airstrike killed Lebanese journalist Amal Khalil Wednesday while she was reporting in southern Lebanon. Khalil's death makes her the eighth journalist killed by Israel in Lebanon in the past two months, according to the Committee to Protect Journalists.

**Assessment:** The three-week extension buys significant time for diplomacy, but ceasefire violations from both sides continued within hours of the announcement. Hezbollah's exclusion from the talks creates implementation risk. The Lebanon ceasefire is also tied to broader U.S. efforts to extend a separate ceasefire with Iran. Iran has insisted that the fighting in Lebanon remain paused in order to continue peace talks with the United States.

### 2. Iran FM Heading to Pakistan — Talks Resumption Possible

**[VERIFIED]** Iranian Foreign Minister, Abbas Araghchi, is expected to fly into Pakistan's capital on Friday night with a small delegation, in what officials said was a key step towards the resumption of direct talks with the United States aimed at ending their war.

For the moment, the Iranian state news agency IRNA said Araghchi's visit to Pakistan was bilateral in nature — to speak with Pakistani officials, rather than for talks right away with the US. Araghchi, IRNA said, would travel to Moscow and Muscat after Islamabad.

**[REPORTED]** Still, one Pakistani official said there was now a "high likelihood of a breakthrough" between the US and Iran, after days of escalating brinkmanship and rising tensions.

**[VERIFIED]** A US logistics and security team is already in Islamabad to prepare for the talks, the officials said in a message to reporters, asking not to be identified because the discussions aren't public.

Iranian Foreign Minister Abbas Araghchi is expected to arrive in Pakistan today for talks with mediators, sources tell CNN. The Iranian delegation is not expected to speak with US representatives, according to a US source and Iranian state media, but the Pakistanis anticipate the meeting will lead to a second round of talks between the US and Iran.

**Assessment:** This is the most significant diplomatic movement since Iran declined talks earlier this week. The presence of a US logistics team in Islamabad and Pakistani optimism about a "breakthrough" suggests both sides may be preparing for substantive engagement. The key constraint remains: Iran dismissed the extension as "meaningless" and said the continued U.S. naval blockade on Iranian ports is a violation of the deal. Iran negotiators say they will not return to the negotiating table until the blockade is lifted.

### 3. Oil Spikes to $106, Then Retreats on Talk of Diplomacy

**[VERIFIED]** At 9 a.m. Eastern Time today, oil was priced at $106.01 per barrel with Brent serving as the benchmark. That's a gain of $2.34 compared with yesterday morning and around $39 higher than the price one year ago.

Brent crude, the international benchmark, topped $106 per barrel early on Friday morning as Washington and Tehran stepped up their confrontation over the key maritime route for transporting the world's energy. Brent stood at $106.80 as of 01:00 GMT, up nearly 5 percent from its closing price on Wednesday, when it surpassed $100 per barrel for the first time in two weeks.

**[VERIFIED]** WTI crude oil futures slipped to $94.8 per barrel on Friday, reversing earlier gains and snapping a four-session winning streak as hopes of diplomatic progress between the US and Iran improved sentiment. Reports indicated Iranian Foreign Minister Abbas Araghchi was expected to arrive in Islamabad, with sources suggesting a high likelihood of a breakthrough in negotiations involving mediators such as Pakistan. Despite the pullback, oil remains on track for a weekly gain of about 14%.

**Assessment:** The ~14% weekly gain reflects the market pricing in prolonged disruption, while the Friday pullback shows hypersensitivity to any diplomatic signals. This volatility creates strong incentives for negotiation but also introduces uncertainty.

### 4. Hegseth Attacks European Allies for "Free-Riding"

**[OFFICIAL]** Secretary of Defense Pete Hegseth said Friday that the "free ride" is over for American allies he said were benefiting from U.S. actions in the Strait of Hormuz. "This should not be America's fight alone. We barely use the Strait of Hormuz as a country. Our energy doesn't flow through there, and we have plenty of energy," Hegseth told reporters at the Pentagon. "Europe and Asia have benefited from our protection for decades, but the time for free riding is over."

"We are not counting on Europe, but they need the Strait of Hormuz much more than we do, and might want to start doing less talking and having less fancy conferences in Europe and get in a boat," Hegseth said during a Pentagon press briefing.

He mocked a recent European summit in Cyprus, saying US allies "might want to start doing less talking" instead of holding "a silly conference."

French President Emmanuel Macron said that the US could not complain about a lack of support "in an operation they chose to undertake alone."

**[OFFICIAL]** "All they have to do is abandon a nuclear weapon — and in meaningful and verifiable ways. Or instead, they can watch their regime's fragile economic state collapse under the unrelenting pressure of American power. A blockade as long as it takes. Whatever President Trump decides."

**Assessment:** This represents a significant rhetorical escalation against NATO allies, coming as the UK and France are organizing a 51-country maritime mission. The administration is simultaneously criticizing allies for not helping while warning it will continue operations indefinitely.

### 5. Iran Deploying More Mines; Mine-Clearing Continues

**[REPORTED]** Iran's Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps (IRGC) navy laid more mines in the Strait of Hormuz this week, according to a U.S. official and a source with knowledge of the issue.

The mines can be relatively easy to lay for Iranians piloting small fishing-boat-sized Gashti vessels, which can each carry two to four mines and drop them in the strait. Iran still has scores of those vessels, which can be equipped with rocket launchers and mounted machine guns to interdict large tankers.

This is the second time Iran has mined the strait since the war began. It remains unclear whether all the mines from the first round have been found and cleared.

**[VERIFIED]** Pentagon denied that clearing the Strait will take six months: The Pentagon blasted as cherry picking and false on Thursday a news report saying that the department assessed it could take six months to completely clear the Strait of Hormuz of Iranian-laid mines. "One assessment does not mean the assessment is plausible, and a six-month closure of the Strait of Hormuz is an impossibility and completely unacceptable to the Secretary," Parnell told AFP on Thursday.

**Assessment:** Reports of additional Iranian mining operations directly contradicts prospects for early Strait reopening. Even if diplomacy succeeds, the mine threat could keep shipping suppressed for months.

### 6. Trump Rules Out Nuclear Weapons Use

**[OFFICIAL]** "President Trump emphatically insisted Thursday that the United States will not use a nuclear weapon against Iran as the conflict nears its third month. Speaking with reporters in the Oval Office, Mr. Trump called questions about the nuclear option "stupid."

"Why would I use a nuclear weapon when we've totally and in a very conventional way decimated them without it?" he said. "I wouldn't use it. A nuclear weapon should never be allowed to be used by anybody."

**Assessment:** This explicit ruling out of nuclear weapons provides some reassurance after weeks of apocalyptic rhetoric. It also underscores Trump's position that conventional operations have been successful.

### 7. May 1 War Powers Deadline — One Week Away

**[VERIFIED]** Trump has until May 1 to obtain congressional approval under the War Powers Resolution. It states that he must limit deployments in any ongoing conflict after 60 days – unless he is granted specific authorisation to continue. To grant this, both the House of Representatives and the Senate must pass a joint resolution – with a simple majority in each – within that 60-day limit. That's not happened so far.

**[VERIFIED]** The Senate has rejected another bid to rein in President Trump's ability to use further military force against Iran, marking Democrats' fifth effort to do so since the war began on February 28. On Wednesday, the Senate voted 46 to 51 to defeat the war powers resolution, with Senator Rand Paul the only Republican voting to advance the measure, and Senator John Fetterman the lone Democrat in opposition. Three senators did not vote.

**[VERIFIED]** It's too early to tell what most Republican lawmakers will do if the clock runs out on May 1 and the war against Iran is still ongoing. Some, such as Sen. James Lankford, have argued that it's too far out to speculate, while others, such as Sen. Susan Collins, have indicated that they would join with Democrats to vote in favor of resolutions ordering an end to the fighting.

Trump can extend the military campaign by a further 30 days, but that extension is meant to allow for the safe and orderly withdrawal of U.S. troops, not to continue prosecuting an unauthorized war, according to a 2025 report on the law by the Congressional Research Service.

**Assessment:** The May 1 deadline is becoming a real political constraint. While past administrations have stretched War Powers interpretations, the combination of $100+ oil prices, key Republican unease, and no end in sight may shift the calculus. Collins' statement that she considers the 60-day trigger "severe" is notable.

### 8. Blockade Continues to Expand

**[OFFICIAL]** Secretary of Defense Pete Hegseth stressed on Friday the strength of the U.S. blockade of Iranian ports and vessels linked to the Islamic Republic, saying it had gone "global." "This growing blockade has also gone global. Just this week, we seized two Iranian dark fleet ships in the Indo-Pacific region that had left Iranian ports before the blockade went into effect," Hegseth said. "They thought they'd made it out just in time."

Hegseth said the number of Iranian ships prevented from exiting the Strait of Hormuz by U.S. forces had risen to 34, an increase of one since Thursday evening.

The aircraft carrier USS George H.W. Bush and its strike group arrived Thursday in the U.S. Central Command (CENTCOM) area of responsibility. It will be the third U.S. aircraft carrier in the region — tightening the naval blockade on Iran and giving Trump more military options if he decides to resume the war, sources said.

## Analysis: The Diplomatic Window Opens

**What's changing:**

- Iran FM Araghchi traveling to Pakistan with planned stops in Oman and Russia
- US logistics and security team reportedly already in Islamabad
- Pakistani officials expressing optimism about "breakthrough"
- Israel-Lebanon ceasefire extended three weeks
- Oil prices retreating from $106 on talk of diplomacy

This doesn't resolve the fundamental impasse — Iran still demands blockade removal before talks, US still demands nuclear concessions — but it suggests both sides recognize the current trajectory is unsustainable.

**What's not changing:**

- Reports of additional Iranian mining
- Third US carrier arriving
- 34 ships now diverted
- Shoot-to-kill ROE remains in effect

**The paradox:**

We're seeing simultaneous diplomatic outreach and operational escalation. This could mean:
1. Both sides are hedging — talking while preparing for continued conflict
2. The pressure is working — forcing reluctant engagement
3. Neither side has a clear exit strategy — leading to muddled signaling

**May 1 becomes real:**

The War Powers deadline is emerging as a genuine constraint. Sen. Collins' statement that she considers the 60-day trigger "severe" and would potentially vote with Democrats is significant. The combination of $4+ gas prices, no end in sight, and key Republican unease could shift the political calculus.
