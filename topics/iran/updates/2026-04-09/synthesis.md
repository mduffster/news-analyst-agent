## What Changed Since April 8

### The Mine Problem — A New Dimension of Instability

**[VERIFIED]** The ceasefire's promise of "safe passage" through Hormuz now faces a technical obstacle that may prove more challenging than political will: sea mines. Iran has launched 21 confirmed attacks on merchant ships, and has reportedly laid sea mines in the strait.

**[OFFICIAL]** Iran announced alternative shipping routes through the Strait of Hormuz, including a new map designed to help vessels avoid mines. The IRGC Navy published a map outlining alternative routes for ships to help them avoid sea mines.

**[REPORTED]** Iran has indicated all along it wanted to keep control of the strait, and it's been formulating this toll system for ships to go through. Analysts say several oil tankers have paid at least a million dollars to transit the strait.

**[REPORTED]** If there is a peace deal or an agreement in the Strait of Hormuz and mines have been deployed, then for the foreseeable future, there will probably be a requirement, because of the dynamic nature of sea mines, to form a convoy and sweep for mines in front of that convoy. A convoy would probably operate in a channel a couple of kilometres wide which has been cleared of mines. It would not be the case that every square metre of the Strait of Hormuz would be cleared every day.

**Assessment:** The mine revelation transforms the Hormuz situation from a political standoff into an engineering problem. Even with political agreement, clearing mines and restoring normal shipping will take weeks or months, not days. Iran is using this reality to institutionalize IRGC control over strait transit.

### Hormuz Traffic — Still Blocked in Practice

**[VERIFIED]** Lloyd's List, which tracks maritime intelligence data, said that only three ships had transited the waterway since the ceasefire was announced.

**[REPORTED]** Even if Iran had kept it open, hundreds of other ships in and around the strait chose to stay put. Before the war, anywhere from 120 to 150 ships would sail through the strait every day.

**[VERIFIED]** The Strait of Hormuz remains mostly closed, though Gulf countries did not report being attacked overnight for the first time since the war began.

**Assessment:** The gap between claimed "opening" and actual traffic remains vast: ~3 ships vs. 120-150 daily in peacetime. The absence of Gulf attacks overnight is a positive sign, but insurance concerns and mine risks are keeping most vessels stationary.

**Assessment:** Lebanon is now the active war zone while the Iran front pauses. Israel's deployment of five divisions and plans to continue occupation "even after the Iran war ends" indicate a long-term commitment. Hezbollah's resumption of rocket fire on Thursday morning completes a dangerous cycle that could pull Iran back into direct conflict.

**[CRITICAL]** There are now three competing versions of the ceasefire framework:

- **Iran's published 10-point proposal:** Includes Lebanon, enrichment rights, Hormuz oversight
- **US/Pakistan mediated version:** Unclear on Lebanon; silent on enrichment details
- **What Trump says was agreed:** "Different than what Iran published"; to be discussed "behind closed doors"

Leavitt confirmed Axios' reporting that the 10-point counter-proposal that Iran sent to the White House on Monday was rejected and later amended and redrafted by the mediators in a way that was workable and in line with the White House's 15-point proposal.

**Assessment:** This textual confusion creates pretext for either side to claim violation. Iran can say the US violated enrichment rights; the US can say Iran never agreed to their terms. Saturday's talks will test whether any real common ground exists.

## Bottom Line

**Day 2 assessment: The US-Iran pause is holding but the ceasefire may be collapsing in slow motion as Lebanon burns and Hormuz remains mined.**

The ceasefire announced 36 hours ago faces three structural problems that may prove fatal:

1. **Lebanon is excluded — and everyone knows it matters:** Israel's "largest strike of the war" on April 8, Hezbollah's resumed attacks on April 9, and Iran's explicit threat to withdraw over Lebanon create a direct pathway to resumption. Israel now has five divisions in Lebanon and plans to stay.

2. **Hormuz is mined — and that's a technical problem, not just political:** Even with Iranian goodwill (which is uncertain), clearing mines takes time. Iran is using "safety coordination" to institutionalize IRGC control of the strait.

3. **Nobody agrees what was agreed:** Three different versions of the 10-point framework. Iran says enrichment rights were accepted; the US says no. Iran says Lebanon is included; Israel and the US say no.

**Saturday's Islamabad talks are now the hinge point.** If Vance and Ghalibaf can find a face-saving formula on Lebanon and a path forward on Hormuz verification, the ceasefire might survive. If either side arrives with maximalist positions, the two-week clock may not run out before fighting resumes.

**The ceasefire expires April 21-22. Lebanon may make that irrelevant.**
